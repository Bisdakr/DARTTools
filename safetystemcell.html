HPC-A Safety Considerations
Mobilization adverse events, vascular access, citrate management, donor-specific considerations, and the SCD-specific G-CSF caveat. Compiled from ASH, ASTCT, ASFA, and current gene-therapy collection protocols.

TL;DR — The safety story in one paragraph
HPC-A collection safety has two domains: mobilization-related risks (G-CSF bone pain, splenic rupture, the absolute SCD contraindication to G-CSF) and apheresis-related risks (citrate, vascular access, hemodynamic shift). For autologous patients, prior chemotherapy and underlying disease shape risk. For healthy related donors, the calculus is different — donor safety is paramount and the only acceptable adverse-event rate is essentially zero. The single most dangerous error is administering G-CSF to a sickle-cell patient — G-CSF can precipitate vaso-occlusive crisis and death in SCD. Plerixafor monotherapy is the safe alternative.

Mobilization Adverse Events
G-CSF bone pain, headache, splenomegaly

G-CSF (filgrastim, lenograstim, biosimilars) and plerixafor are the workhorse mobilization agents. Each has its own adverse-event profile.

AgentCommon side effectsRare / serious
G-CSFBone pain (~80%), headache, fatigue, nausea, elevated WBC, alkaline phosphatase rise, LDH riseSplenic rupture, capillary leak syndrome, allergic reaction, lung injury
PlerixaforInjection-site reaction, GI symptoms (nausea, diarrhea), fatigue, dizzinessAllergic reactions; theoretical splenic enlargement
Combination (G-CSF + plerixafor)Additive bone pain; somewhat more GISame as individual agents
🔑
Bone pain managementAcetaminophen first-line; NSAIDs if not contraindicated; opioids reserved for severe pain. The pain is mediated by G-CSF receptor signaling in marrow — typically peaks 24–48 hr after the third or fourth dose. Patients should be counseled in advance.
03
SCD — The Absolute G-CSF Contraindication
Why plerixafor monotherapy is the only safe path
⚠
CATASTROPHIC if given to SCD patientG-CSF is absolutely contraindicated in sickle cell disease. Multiple fatal cases have been reported. G-CSF causes leukocyte activation, increased adhesion, and rapid mobilization that precipitates severe vaso-occlusive crisis, acute chest syndrome, and death. Never give G-CSF to a patient with HbSS, HbSβ-thal, or HbSC disease.

For SCD patients undergoing autologous CD34 collection (e.g., for lentiviral gene therapy), plerixafor monotherapy at 0.24 mg/kg SQ approximately 6 hours pre-collection is the standard safe approach. Pre-procedure RBC exchange to bring HbS < 30% is often performed.

  • Verify SCD status in every patient list before any G-CSF order. Confirm Hgb electrophoresis on file.
  • Tag the medication record with a G-CSF allergy/contraindication flag in patients with SCD.
  • Pre-collection exchange may be needed for adequate HbS reduction.
  • Pre-collection IV hydration to maintain hemodilution and reduce VOC risk.
  • Avoid sustained higher-Hct states post-exchange (target Hct ≤ 30%).
04
Splenic Rupture — Rare but Catastrophic
Recognition, prevention, response

Spontaneous splenic rupture is a rare but well-documented complication of G-CSF mobilization. It occurs in approximately 1 per 5,000–10,000 cases but is potentially fatal.

⚠
Red flag symptomsLeft upper quadrant or left shoulder pain (Kehr sign), abdominal pain, hypotension, syncope. Any acute abdominal pain during or shortly after G-CSF mobilization should be evaluated urgently.
  • Patient counseling pre-mobilization: "If you develop severe left upper quadrant pain, left shoulder pain, light-headedness, or rapid heart rate, go to the ED immediately."
  • Avoid contact sports / heavy lifting during mobilization and for 1–2 weeks after.
  • Splenomegaly screening not required routinely but consider in patients with high baseline spleen size (post-splenectomy patients excluded from this risk).
  • Response to suspected rupture: emergent CT, hemoglobin / lactate / type & cross, surgical consult, hemodynamic resuscitation.
05
Citrate Management
Higher AC ratio = lower symptom rate; AIR safety cap

HPC-A collection commonly runs at AC ratios of 1:12 (standard) down to 1:8 (tight, for high-cell-density products). Tighter ratios increase citrate delivery rate and citrate symptom risk; instruments cap inlet flow to maintain AIR ≤ 1.2 mL/min/L TBV.

AC ratioCitrate loadTypical use
1:13 to 1:14LowestPatients with citrate intolerance, hepatic dysfunction
1:12StandardMost CMNC collections
1:10Moderate-highIf clumping at standard ratio
1:8HighSCD/TDT gene-therapy collections; expect more symptoms
1:6Very highRare; symptomatic management aggressive
👁
Calcium drip protocolMany institutions run a prophylactic calcium gluconate IV drip for HPC-A collections, particularly for tight AC ratios. Verify the institutional calcium replacement protocol before each procedure.
06
Vascular Access
Central catheter standard for most autologous collections

Most HPC-A collections require central venous access due to flow rate requirements (typically 60–80 mL/min adult). Healthy related donors are often peripherally accessed if antecubital veins are adequate.

  • Central catheter (apheresis-quality): ≥ 10 Fr for adult; ultrasound-guided placement; chest film if not IJ
  • Peripheral (donors): two large-bore (16–17 g) antecubital lines; warm packs and tourniquet; check patency every 30 min
  • CLABSI prevention: strict sterile technique at every connection; dedicated apheresis nurse; hand hygiene
  • Catheter removal timing: immediately after final collection day to minimize dwell-related infection risk

Donor advocacyMany centers have a separate donor-advocate role to ensure the donor's interests are protected independent of the recipient's needs. The collection center must support donor withdrawal at any time, even mid-procedure.
08
Pediatric Considerations
ECV ceiling, sedation, calcium
  • ECV ceiling 10% TBV — blood prime if exceeded
  • Pediatric TBV formulas (≤ 5 kg → 90 mL/kg · 5.1–15 kg → 80 mL/kg · 15.1–25 kg → 70 mL/kg)
  • Procedural sedation often required for younger children — coordinate with anesthesia / PICU
  • Aggressive citrate monitoring + prophylactic calcium
  • Central access required for most pediatric collections
  • G-CSF dosing typically 10 mcg/kg/day; plerixafor 0.24 mg/kg SQ
  • Family / caregiver support — multi-day mobilization + collection is stressful; coordinate with child life
09
Emergency Response
When something goes wrong
⚠ Suspected Splenic Rupture
  1. Recognize: severe LUQ pain, left shoulder pain (Kehr sign), hypotension, syncope, abdominal distension.
  2. Activate code response; emergent CT abdomen with IV contrast.
  3. IV access, large bore; type and crossmatch, hemoglobin, lactate.
  4. Resuscitation with crystalloid and blood products as needed.
  5. Surgical consult urgent for possible splenectomy or embolization.
⚠ Vaso-Occlusive Crisis in SCD Patient (post G-CSF error)
  1. STOP any further G-CSF. Document the error.
  2. IV hydration + oxygen + pain management per institutional VOC protocol.
  3. Consider emergent exchange transfusion if severe (acute chest syndrome, severe pain crisis).
  4. Hematology consult urgent.
  5. Report to safety event system as a serious adverse drug event.
⚠ Severe Hypocalcemic Symptoms
  1. STOP procedure or slow inlet.
  2. IV calcium gluconate 1–2 g over 10–15 min.
  3. Continuous cardiac monitoring; watch for QT prolongation.
  4. Loosen AC ratio and resume only when symptom-free.
