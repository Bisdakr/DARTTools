Education · Continuous Renal Replacement Therapy
Why CRRT?
Slow continuous solute and fluid removal in the hemodynamically unstable ICU patient — physiology, modalities, machines, the evidence behind dose targets, and the future of continuous extracorporeal organ support.
01 · How It Works 02 · History 03 · Modalities 04 · Machines & Filters 05 · Dose Evidence 06 · Anticoagulation 07 · Pre vs Post Dilution 08 · Indications 09 · Pediatric CRRT 10 · 2024–2025 References
TL;DR — The 60-second version
Continuous Renal Replacement Therapy provides slow, continuous solute and fluid removal for critically ill patients with acute kidney injury who cannot tolerate the rapid shifts of intermittent hemodialysis. Where standard HD removes 3,000 mL of UF over 4 hours, CRRT removes the same volume over 24 hours — far gentler on hemodynamics, less likely to cause cerebral / myocardial stunning, and able to be precisely titrated minute-by-minute. The standard dose per KDIGO is 20–25 mL/kg/hr of effluent. The dominant modality globally is CVVHDF (combined diffusion + convection). Regional citrate anticoagulation (RCA) has displaced heparin in most centers. Modern platforms like Prismax and Prismaflex enable bedside operation with integrated balance management and citrate protocols.

01
How CRRT Works
Three physical processes happening across a hollow-fiber membrane

CRRT uses the same physical principles as intermittent hemodialysis — but applied continuously and slowly. Blood is drawn from a central venous catheter, passed through a synthetic hollow-fiber membrane, and returned to the patient. At the membrane, three processes can be invoked depending on the chosen modality:

ProcessWhat it doesDriven by
DiffusionSmall solutes (urea, K, creatinine) move from blood to dialysateConcentration gradient (countercurrent dialysate flow)
ConvectionWater plus dissolved solute (including middle molecules) move with bulk fluid flowTransmembrane pressure (ultrafiltration)
ReplacementSterile replacement fluid replaces the ultrafiltrate volume (pre- or post-filter)Substitution pump
🔑
The slow-and-continuous advantageIntermittent HD removes 3,000 mL of UF in 4 hours (12.5 mL/min). CRRT removes the same volume in 24 hours (~2 mL/min). The slow rate avoids compartment shifts (intracranial pressure rise, myocardial stunning, mesenteric ischemia) and lets the body's adaptive mechanisms keep pace. This is why CRRT is the modality of choice for ICU patients with shock, cerebral edema, or fragile cardiac function.

The total volume of effluent (drainage fluid out of the filter) per hour, normalized to patient weight, defines the effluent dose — the standard prescription metric for CRRT.

02
A Brief History
From Kramer's accidental discovery to modern continuous therapy
1977
Peter Kramer at Göttingen, Germany, treats an oliguric patient with diuretic-resistant fluid overload by connecting an arterial-venous shunt to a small hemofilter. Continuous arteriovenous hemofiltration (CAVH) is born — accidentally, as a rescue maneuver — but immediately recognized as transformative for ICU AKI.
1980s
CAVH proliferates but has limitations: dependent on patient's own BP to drive flow; low clearance; arterial access. CVVH (continuous venovenous hemofiltration) emerges — pump-driven, more reliable, requires only venous access.
1990s
Dedicated CRRT machines launch. CVVHDF combining diffusion + convection becomes the dominant clinical modality. ICUs around the world standardize CRRT for ICU AKI.
2000
Ronco et al. publish a single-center trial suggesting CRRT dose > 35 mL/kg/hr improves mortality vs < 20. The "high-dose CRRT" era begins; many centers double their effluent flow.
2008
ATN trial (NEJM, US VA cohort) — intensive vs less-intensive RRT in 1,124 patients. No mortality benefit of higher dose. The high-dose era is challenged.
2009
RENAL trial (NEJM, Australian/NZ ANZICS cohort) — 1,508 patients randomized to 25 vs 40 mL/kg/hr effluent dose. No mortality benefit of the higher dose. The 20–25 mL/kg/hr standard is established.
2012
KDIGO AKI guidelines published — recommend 20–25 mL/kg/hr effluent dose, regional citrate anticoagulation as preferred over heparin (Grade 2B), and standardized timing/initiation criteria.
2014
STOP-AKI and similar trials begin testing early-vs-delayed CRRT initiation. The "best timing" question remains active.
2016
AKIKI trial (NEJM) — early vs delayed RRT in ICU AKI. Delayed strategy non-inferior; many patients in the delayed group never needed RRT.
2019
Prismax platform launches (Baxter/Gambro) — touchscreen interface, TPE-capable, integrated citrate balance, improved alarm logic. Many centers transition from Prismaflex to Prismax over the early 2020s.
2020
STARRT-AKI trial (NEJM) — definitive early vs standard initiation in 3,019 ICU patients. Earlier initiation does not improve mortality; cements the "wait unless absolutely necessary" approach.
2023–2024
Renewed interest in extended dialysis (PIRRT/SLED) as a bridge between intermittent HD and 24-hour CRRT. Adoption of medium cut-off membranes, novel anticoagulation strategies, and AI-supported prescription. Hyperammonemia and toxin-removal protocols expand CRRT into broader critical-care applications.

🔑
Why CVVHDF is dominantCVVHDF combines small-solute clearance (diffusion) with middle-molecule clearance (convection) in one prescription. The 50/50 dialysate/replacement split gives flexibility — you can shift toward more diffusion (if filter clotting) or more convection (if middle-molecule clearance is the goal). It's the workhorse of modern ICU CRRT.

Globally, CVVHDF accounts for roughly half of CRRT prescriptions; CVVH and CVVHD split most of the remainder. SCUF is uncommon (most patients also need solute support). HVHF was extensively trialed for septic shock but the IVOIRE trial (2013) showed no benefit and significant electrolyte/hypothermia downsides; it is now rarely prescribed.

CRRT Platforms — Generations & Filter Sets
Prismaflex, Prismax, and the membrane lineup

Modern CRRT machines are bedside instruments designed for ICU operation by nursing staff. The dominant platforms in North American and European ICUs are the Prismaflex (originally Gambro, now under Baxter) and its successor Prismax, alongside competing systems from other manufacturers (omitted here for educational scope).

PlatformGenerationHighlights
PrismaflexLegacy (1999 launch; widely deployed)Versatile CRRT + TPE-capable on M60/M100/M150/HF20 set lineup; integrated citrate protocols (Prismocitrate 18/0)
PrismaxCurrent (2019+)Touchscreen UI · improved alarm management · enhanced balance accuracy · TPE-capable · faster setup workflow · integrated training mode

Both platforms use disposable filter sets that bundle the hemofilter, tubing, and circuit components. The set is matched to patient size and prescribed dose:

💡
AN69 ST membraneThe AN69 (acrylonitrile / sodium methallyl sulfonate copolymer) membrane is highly biocompatible and the most common CRRT membrane in use globally. The "ST" surface treatment reduces bradykinin generation, important because ACE-inhibitor patients can have bradykinin reactions with untreated AN69 membranes. ST-AN69 has been the workhorse membrane for two decades.
🧪
Medium cut-off membranesNewer membranes (e.g., Theranova-class) with larger pores remove middle molecules including some cytokines. Trials in septic AKI continue; results to date are mixed. The traditional AN69 ST remains standard for most CRRT prescriptions.
05
Effluent Dose — The Evidence Behind 20–25 mL/kg/hr
Two landmark trials settled the question

The single most important number in a CRRT order is the effluent dose in mL/kg/hr. This represents the sum of dialysate flow + replacement flow + net UF, normalized to body weight. It corresponds roughly to "how aggressively we are clearing solute."

DoseEraEvidence
< 15 mL/kg/hrHistoricalInadequate; associated with worse outcomes (Ronco 2000)
20–25 mL/kg/hrCurrent standard (KDIGO 2012)RENAL, ATN trials — no benefit of higher; this is the target
35–45 mL/kg/hr"High-dose" era 2000sSingle-center Ronco trial suggested benefit; not replicated in larger trials
> 50 mL/kg/hr (HVHF)Sepsis cytokine eraIVOIRE: no benefit, more downsides (hypothermia, drug clearance, electrolyte loss)
🔑
Prescribed vs delivered doseDelivered dose is always lower than prescribed. Pre-filter dilution (the most common configuration) reduces effective solute concentration at the filter. Filter clotting / circuit interruptions further reduce delivered dose. To deliver 25 mL/kg/hr, prescribe ~30 mL/kg/hr to account for ~10–25% delivery loss. The DART calculator computes the pre-dilution correction explicitly.

Higher doses are not harmless. They increase: drug clearance (under-dosing antibiotics and sedatives is well-documented), electrolyte loss (hypophosphatemia, hypomagnesemia), and hypothermia. Stay near the 20–25 mL/kg/hr target unless a specific clinical scenario (severe hyperkalemia, hyperammonemia, drug overdose) warrants temporary escalation.

06
Anticoagulation Strategy
Regional citrate has displaced heparin in most ICUs

Without anticoagulation, the CRRT circuit will clot quickly — typically within hours. Three options:

StrategyHow it worksProsCons
Regional citrate (RCA)Citrate infused pre-filter chelates ionized Ca; calcium replaced post-filter to restore patient ionized CaNo systemic anticoagulation · longer filter life · KDIGO Grade 2B recommendationCitrate accumulation risk in hepatic failure; requires more nursing attention; cost
Systemic heparinContinuous heparin infusion; targets aPTT 1.5–2.0× or ACT 180–220Familiar · cheap · easy to monitorBleeding risk · HIT · circuit life shorter than RCA
None (saline flushes only)Periodic saline flushes through circuit; high BFR helps reduce stasisNo bleeding risk · simplestShortest filter life · frequent circuit changes · increased clotting alarms
🔑
Why RCA winsMultiple large trials and meta-analyses (Liu 2020, Bai 2015, Zarbock 2020) consistently show longer filter life and fewer bleeding events with RCA vs heparin. KDIGO 2012 gives a Grade 2B recommendation favoring citrate. Most modern ICUs have transitioned to RCA as their default; heparin remains for centers without an established RCA protocol or for patients with hepatic failure.

RCA mechanics: Citrate infused pre-filter chelates calcium → blood within the circuit is unable to clot. Citrate-bound complex is partially removed in the effluent; the rest enters systemic circulation. Liver metabolism converts citrate to bicarbonate, releasing calcium. Patient ionized calcium is restored by post-filter calcium infusion (CaCl₂ or calcium gluconate). The institutional RCA protocol prescribes the citrate dose (typically 3 mmol/L blood) and a starting calcium rate, then titrates to: systemic iCa 1.1–1.3 mmol/L and post-filter iCa 0.25–0.40 mmol/L (the latter confirming adequate circuit anticoagulation).

⚠
Citrate accumulationIn hepatic failure or shock with poor citrate clearance, citrate accumulates. The hallmark is rising total-to-ionized calcium ratio (> 2.5) with metabolic acidosis. Response: reduce citrate dose, consider switching to heparin or no anticoagulant.
07
Pre vs Post Dilution — The Trade-off
Anticoagulation vs effective clearance

Replacement fluid can be infused before the filter (pre-dilution) or after the filter (post-dilution). The trade-off shapes both filter life and effective dose delivered.

ConfigurationEffectBest for
100% Post-filterFull clearance of prescribed dose; highest blood concentration through filterMaximum clearance; filter clotting risk
30/70 (Pre/Post)Balanced — small dilution reduces clotting; modest dose reductionCommon balanced setting
100% Pre-filterMaximum dilution; longest filter life; greatest reduction in effective doseHigh filter-clotting risk; severely hypercoagulable patients
🔑
The math behind pre-dilution correctionWhen replacement is given pre-filter, the blood is diluted before reaching the membrane. The effective clearance is reduced by the dilution factor:

Effective dose = Effluent × (Plasma flow / (Plasma flow + Pre-rep rate))

To deliver 25 mL/kg/hr with 30% pre-dilution, prescribe ~30 mL/kg/hr. The DART calculator computes this correction explicitly.

Filtration Fraction (FFR) — the ratio of effluent flow to plasma flow at the filter — should stay under 25%. Above this, hemoconcentration at the filter increases viscosity, triggers clotting, and shortens filter life. Reduce FFR by: (1) increasing BFR, (2) shifting more replacement to pre-filter, or (3) reducing the effluent dose.

Indications & Patient Selection
ICU AKI and beyond

The dominant indication is AKI requiring RRT in a hemodynamically unstable ICU patient. The classic "AEIOU" indications for RRT remain valid:

IndicationExamples
A — AcidosisSevere metabolic acidosis (pH < 7.10, HCO₃ < 10) refractory to medical management
E — ElectrolytesSevere hyperkalemia (> 6.5) with ECG changes, refractory to insulin/glucose, kayexalate
I — IntoxicationsSalicylate, ethylene glycol, methanol, metformin lactic acidosis, valproate
O — OverloadDiuretic-resistant fluid overload (especially with pulmonary edema, ARDS)
U — UremiaBUN > 100, encephalopathy, pericarditis

CRRT-specific advantages drive choice over intermittent HD in:

  • Septic shock — vasopressor-dependent patients tolerate CRRT vs IHD's hemodynamic shifts
  • Cerebral edema (TBI, hepatic encephalopathy, fulminant liver failure) — IHD risks ICP rise; CRRT does not
  • Cardiogenic shock — IHD's UF removes too fast; CRRT titrates minute-by-minute
  • Hyperammonemia (urea cycle defects, post-transplant, valproate) — continuous clearance superior to IHD's "rebound"
  • Rhabdomyolysis — continuous myoglobin clearance protects kidneys
  • Massive fluid resuscitation — patients on continuous high-volume fluids need continuous removal
🔬
When to start? STARRT-AKI answeredThe STARRT-AKI trial (NEJM 2020, n=3,019) randomized accelerated vs standard initiation of RRT in ICU AKI. Earlier initiation did not improve mortality. Modern practice: wait for absolute indications (severe acidosis, hyperkalemia, intoxication, refractory volume overload, severe uremia) rather than pre-emptively starting on lab numbers alone.

Pediatric CRRT
Down to neonatal sizes with the right filter and prime

Pediatric CRRT presents two challenges: extracorporeal volume (ECV) as a percentage of TBV, and vascular access in small patients. Both are solvable with the right hardware and protocols.

Patient sizeFilter setVascular accessConsiderations
Neonate / < 5 kgHF20 (0.2 m²)5–6 Fr dual-lumenBlood prime almost always; very careful Ca balance; CARPEDIEM platform alternative
5–10 kgHF20 or M607 FrBlood prime if ECV > 10% TBV; reduced flows
10–25 kgM60 (0.6 m²)8–9 FrStandard pediatric set; weight-based dose 25–30 mL/kg/hr
> 25 kgM100/M15011–14 Fr (adult-style)Adult prescription principles
🔑
Pediatric ECV thresholdIf circuit ECV exceeds 10% of patient TBV, blood prime the circuit. Otherwise, the patient transiently loses 10%+ of their blood volume at circuit initiation — a major hemodynamic insult. This is standard pediatric ICU practice.

The CARPEDIEM platform (Cardio-Renal Pediatric Dialysis Emergency Machine) is a dedicated neonatal CRRT system designed for patients < 10 kg. It uses tiny circuit volumes (~30 mL) and small filters, enabling CRRT in newborns with severe AKI.

10
2024–2025 Developments
Where CRRT is going
🧪
Extended/Hybrid Dialysis (SLED, PIRRT)Sustained low-efficiency dialysis (SLED) and prolonged intermittent renal replacement therapy (PIRRT) offer a middle ground between IHD and CRRT — 8–12 hour daily sessions with slower flows. Comparable hemodynamic profile to CRRT, lower cost, less nursing burden. Adoption is growing in cost-constrained settings.
💊
Drug-removal protocolsCRRT for severe toxin/drug overdose continues to expand. New protocols for valproate, metformin-associated lactic acidosis, and severe salicylate toxicity integrate enhanced CRRT dosing with serial drug levels. Pharmacists increasingly integrated into ICU rounds for CRRT drug-dose adjustment.
🤖
AI-supported prescriptionMachine learning models trained on large CRRT registries (ATHN, AKIKI follow-up cohorts) are being evaluated for real-time prescription adjustment, predicting filter life, and optimizing fluid balance. Early trials show promise; clinical adoption pending.
🧬
Cytokine adsorption columnsStandalone cytokine-removal cartridges (CytoSorb, oXiris) can be added to CRRT circuits in cytokine-storm syndromes (sepsis, COVID-19, post-cardiac surgery). Evidence is mixed; some signals in specific subgroups; not yet standard care.
📊
CKRT registry and outcomesMulti-center collaborative registries (Australia/NZ, US, European) are now tracking long-term outcomes of ICU AKI survivors — including renal recovery rates, dialysis dependence, and quality of life. The picture is sobering: half of CRRT survivors have significant residual kidney dysfunction; many require chronic dialysis. AKI follow-up clinics are becoming standard.
CRRT is the only extracorporeal therapy that runs 24 hours a day in the ICU — the bedside nurse, not the machine, is the most important piece of equipment in delivering it well.
