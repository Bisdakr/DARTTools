Safety Reference · Therapeutic Plasma Exchange
TPE Safety Considerations
The major risks of therapeutic plasma exchange, how to recognize them early, and the monitoring and response strategies that prevent harm. Compiled from ASFA 2023, JCA recent literature, and standard apheresis safety practice.
01 · Risk Overview 02 · Citrate Toxicity 03 · Hemodynamic 04 · Replacement Fluids 05 · Coagulopathy 06 · Vascular Access 07 · Drug Interactions 08 · Pediatric 09 · Emergency Response 10 · Documentation References
TL;DR — The safety story in one paragraph
TPE is generally well-tolerated but every session carries predictable, mostly preventable risks. The dominant adverse events are citrate-related hypocalcemia (the most frequent — perioral numbness, paresthesias, tetany, rarely seizure or arrhythmia), hypotension (~5–10% of runs), allergic / anaphylactic reactions to plasma-containing replacement fluid (FFP rate ~1–5%), and coagulopathy from dilutional loss of clotting factors and fibrinogen with consecutive runs. Vascular access carries the usual catheter risks (infection, bleeding, pneumothorax for central lines). The clinical job during TPE is straightforward: watch the patient, not the machine — frequent BP, calcium-symptom screens every 15 minutes, fluid-balance running totals, and rapid response to the first hint of reaction.

01
The Major Risk Profile
What can go wrong — frequency and severity at a glance

The body of published TPE safety data — from the WAA registry, the American College of Pathology, ASFA 2023, and large single-center series — converges on a consistent picture: severe complications are rare (well under 1% of sessions) but minor complications are common (up to 50% of patients experience at least one transient symptom). Most are predictable and reversible with prompt recognition.

Adverse eventApproximate rateSeverity
Mild citrate symptoms (perioral numbness, paresthesias)30–50%Mild · self-limited
Hypotension (transient, intra-procedure)5–10%Mild–moderate
Urticarial reaction to FFP1–5%Mild–moderate
Severe hypocalcemic symptoms (tetany, arrhythmia, seizure)0.5–2%Severe

🔑
The 95% ruleThe vast majority of TPE complications fall into three buckets: citrate, hypotension, and allergic reactions to plasma. If your monitoring protocol covers those three reliably, you've covered 95% of what will actually happen. The rest are rare enough to manage by exception.
02
Citrate Toxicity — The Most Frequent Adverse Event
Recognition, monitoring, and prevention of hypocalcemia

Citrate is the workhorse anticoagulant in apheresis. It works by chelating ionized calcium at the membrane and centrifuge interface, preventing clotting in the extracorporeal circuit. Citrate is returned to the patient at every cycle. The liver normally metabolizes citrate within minutes, but the rate of return can exceed metabolism — and the net effect is a transient drop in serum ionized calcium.

Patients with reduced citrate metabolism are at higher risk: liver disease, hypothermia, shock states, neonates, and patients with hyperventilation (alkalosis further reduces ionized calcium).

Mild (very common) Perioral numbness · tingling in fingers/toes · "metallic taste" · mild anxiety
Moderate Muscle twitching · facial spasm · hand cramping (Trousseau sign) · cheek-tap sign (Chvostek)
Severe (uncommon, urgent) Tetany · laryngospasm · QT prolongation → arrhythmia · seizure · cardiac arrest
Atypical signs Nausea / vomiting · chills · light-headedness · sense of impending doom
👁
Monitoring at the bedsideScreen every 15 minutes: "Any numbness or tingling around your mouth or fingers? Any cramping in your hands?" Document baseline + each screen. Citrate symptoms emerge gradually; catching the perioral numbness early prevents progression to tetany.
🔑
Prevention strategiesLoosen the AC ratio (e.g., 1:13 → 1:15 → 1:18) if patient is symptomatic — this slows citrate delivery rate. Slow the return rate. Prophylactic calcium (oral or IV gluconate per protocol) is appropriate for high-citrate-load runs, hepatic dysfunction, or known-sensitive patients. Verify the institutional calcium replacement protocol before each run.
⚠
When citrate becomes dangerousHepatic dysfunction (cirrhosis, acute liver failure, post-transplant) markedly reduces citrate metabolism. Patients on multiple consecutive runs can develop cumulative metabolic alkalosis from citrate-to-bicarbonate conversion. Pre-procedure ionized calcium and bicarbonate values are useful in high-risk patients.
03
Hemodynamic Safety
Hypotension, ECV, fluid balance

Intra-procedure hypotension is the second most common adverse event. Causes vary: vasovagal response at venipuncture, extracorporeal volume (ECV) shift at start of run, citrate-induced vasodilation, hypocalcemia-related cardiac depression, or allergic / anaphylactic reactions.

🔑
The ECV / TBV check is non-negotiableIf extracorporeal circuit volume exceeds 15% of total blood volume (10% for patients < 30 kg), consider blood prime. Most modern apheresis circuits sit safely under this threshold for adults, but it's the first thing to verify in pediatric and small-adult cases.
PatternLikely causeResponse
BP drop in first 5–10 minECV shift; vasovagalTrendelenburg, slow inlet, IV bolus
Gradual BP decline over 30+ minFluid balance drift; citrate vasodilationReview running balance; consider calcium
Sudden BP drop + flushing / dyspneaAllergic / anaphylacticSTOP procedure; treat reaction
BP drop + chest pain + diaphoresisCardiac event (rare)STOP; cardiac workup; ECG
  • BP cycling every 5 min for first 30 min, then every 15 min once stable. More frequent for hemodynamically borderline patients.
  • Pre-procedure baseline HR, BP, weight. Compare against intra-procedure trends — absolute values matter less than the delta.
  • Running fluid balance display in clear view. Goal is isovolemic unless physician orders otherwise.
  • Slow-start protocol (lower inlet for first 50–100 mL) reduces ECV-shift hypotension dramatically.
04
Replacement Fluid Reactions
FFP anaphylaxis, albumin reactions, TRALI

The risk profile is dramatically different between albumin and plasma (FFP). Most non-TTP TPE uses albumin; FFP is reserved for TTP, severe coagulopathy, or specific indications where the replacement itself is the therapy.

Reaction typeAlbuminFFP
Urticarial / mild allergicRare (< 0.1%)1–3%
AnaphylaxisVery rare0.05–0.5%
TRALI (transfusion-related acute lung injury)Essentially zero~1 per 5,000–10,000 units
TACO (circulatory overload)Possible if rapid infusionPossible
Infection transmissionNegligible (heat-treated)Very low but non-zero
Citrate loadLowerHigher (FFP itself contains citrate)
⚠
Anaphylaxis red flagsSudden hypotension, flushing, urticaria, bronchospasm, angioedema, GI symptoms, sense of impending doom. Stop the procedure immediately. Maintain access (do not return circuit blood through a compromised line). Activate emergency response. IM epinephrine 0.3–0.5 mg, antihistamines, corticosteroids, fluid resuscitation, and oxygen per institutional anaphylaxis protocol.
⚠
TRALI vs TACOTRALI: hypoxemia + bilateral pulmonary infiltrates within 6 hr of plasma — non-cardiogenic. Treat with respiratory support; report to blood bank. TACO: volume overload with hypertension, jugular venous distension, pulmonary edema. Treat with diuresis and slowing/stopping infusion.

Premedications (diphenhydramine, hydrocortisone, ± acetaminophen) are commonly given before FFP-based TPE per institutional protocol. They blunt urticarial reactions but do not prevent anaphylaxis.

05
Coagulopathy & Bleeding Risk
Dilutional loss with consecutive runs

Plasma exchange depletes coagulation factors. A single procedure removes most factors; albumin replacement does not replenish them. The body restores most factors within 24–48 hours, but fibrinogen takes longer (48–72 hr) and accumulates depletion across consecutive sessions.

ScheduleCoag impactLab monitoring
Single TPE, then 48 hr breakMinimal residual deficitPre/post fibrinogen if > 1 procedure planned
2–3 consecutive daily TPEsCumulative factor depletionCheck fibrinogen, PT/aPTT before each
5+ consecutive daily TPEsMarked factor depletion (especially fibrinogen)Consider FFP component or cryoprecipitate replacement
TPE in patient with baseline bleeding riskHigh concernDaily fibrinogen, platelet count, clinical bleeding surveillance
👁
Threshold values to act onFibrinogen < 100 mg/dL → consider FFP or cryoprecipitate before next procedure. Platelet count < 50,000 (or rapid decline) → discuss with physician before proceeding. Active bleeding → STOP and re-evaluate the indication for continued TPE.

Procedures or invasive interventions (e.g., line placement, biopsy) within 24 hours of TPE should be timed carefully and may require fresh frozen plasma replacement or delay.

Vascular Access Safety
Catheter and peripheral access risks

Access complications are infrequent but can be serious. The choice of access (peripheral vs central) depends on the patient, procedure duration, and how many sessions are planned.

Access typeSpecific risksMitigation
Peripheral (two large-bore IVs)Infiltration, vasospasm, slower flow16–18 g; warm packs; check patency every 30 min
Central (IJ, subclavian)Pneumothorax, arterial puncture, bleeding, infection (CLABSI), thrombosisUltrasound guidance; chest film if non-IJ; full sterile barrier; minimize dwell time
AV fistula / graftRecirculation, infiltration, thrombosisSingle-needle vs two-needle technique; flow check; staff trained in cannulation
Tunneled catheter (HD-style)Infection, malposition, fibrin sheathStrict sterile technique at every access; flush per protocol
⚠
CLABSI preventionFor tunneled or central catheters: hand hygiene before access, sterile barrier precautions, chlorhexidine prep with adequate contact time, dedicated apheresis nurse for line handling, no routine antibiotic locks unless specifically indicated. CLABSI rate goal < 1 per 1,000 line-days.
07
Drug & Treatment Interactions
ACE-i bradykinin, IVIG timing, immunosuppression

Several drug interactions and timing considerations are unique to TPE:

  • ACE inhibitors + plasma exchange (especially with negatively charged membranes / dextran-sulfate columns): can cause bradykinin-mediated hypotension, flushing, GI distress, and anaphylactoid reactions. Hold ACE-i ≥ 24 hr before TPE (some institutions extend to 48 hr); consider switch to ARB.
  • IVIG given pre-procedure: TPE will remove a substantial fraction of the dose. Schedule IVIG after the planned TPE course, or at least not within 24 hr before.
  • Rituximab / monoclonal antibodies: Similar concern — TPE removes a fraction. Time administration to avoid TPE removal of the therapeutic dose.
  • Anticoagulants (warfarin, DOACs): Consider holding around the procedure based on bleeding risk vs thrombotic risk; individualize with the prescribing physician.
  • Drugs highly protein-bound: Levels may transiently fall post-TPE. Most clinically silent; significant only for narrow-window drugs (e.g., digoxin, antiseizure agents).
  • Live vaccines: Avoid within 2 weeks of TPE (immunoglobulin removal may blunt response).
⚠
The ACE-i + plasma exchange warningSome patients on ACE inhibitors experience severe anaphylactoid reactions during apheresis even with standard albumin. The risk is highest with adsorbent columns (dextran sulfate, polyacrylate) but documented across modalities. Verify ACE-i status pre-procedure; document the hold.

Pediatric Safety
ECV, prime, weight-based thresholds

Pediatric TPE follows the same principles as adult TPE but with tighter margins. Three considerations dominate:

ConsiderationPediatric standard
ECV ceiling10% of total blood volume (vs 15% adult)
Prime decisionNS prime if ECV < 10% TBV · Albumin or diluted RBC if 10–15% · Strongly diluted RBC if > 15%
TBV calculationWeight-based pediatric formula: ≤ 5 kg → 90 mL/kg · 5.1–15 kg → 80 mL/kg · 15.1–25 kg → 70 mL/kg · > 25 kg → Nadler
Citrate loadMore likely to symptom; aggressive calcium monitoring + replacement
Slow startSmaller children: extra-slow initiation, calcium drip prophylactic
Sedation / immobilityProcedural sedation may be needed for younger children; coordinate with PICU / anesthesia
⚠
Special pediatric populationsNeonates and infants on ECMO, with hepatic dysfunction, or with cardiomyopathy require especially careful planning. Many institutions require attending nephrology / hematology approval for any TPE in patients < 10 kg.
09
Emergency Response
When something goes wrong, in what order
⚠ Anaphylaxis Response
  1. STOP the procedure. Clamp the patient line; do not return circuit contents.
  2. Activate code response. Call for help, get the crash cart.
  3. Maintain airway and oxygenation. 100% O₂ via non-rebreather if needed.
  4. IM epinephrine 0.3–0.5 mg (0.01 mg/kg pediatric) in lateral thigh. Repeat every 5–15 min as needed.
  5. IV access independent of apheresis line. Rapid normal saline 1–2 L (or 20 mL/kg).
  6. Antihistamines + corticosteroids per institutional anaphylaxis protocol.
  7. Documentation: time of reaction onset, replacement fluid lot/donor, vital signs trend, treatments given.
⚠ Severe Hypocalcemic Symptoms (tetany, seizure, arrhythmia)
  1. STOP the procedure or slow inlet to minimum.
  2. IV calcium gluconate 1–2 g over 10–15 min (or per institutional protocol).
  3. Continuous cardiac monitoring. Watch for QT prolongation.
  4. Repeat ionized calcium in 10–15 min.
  5. If proceeding: loosen AC ratio, slow return rate, add prophylactic calcium drip.
⚠ Severe Hypotension
  1. Trendelenburg position. Stop return; slow or stop inlet.
  2. IV crystalloid bolus 250–500 mL (or 10 mL/kg pediatric).
  3. Identify cause: ECV shift? Allergic? Hypocalcemia? Cardiac?
  4. If hypotension persists: activate code; consider vasopressor; evaluate for anaphylaxis or cardiac event.
10
Documentation & Communication
What to record, who to tell, when

Comprehensive documentation is both a safety tool and a legal record. The minimum acceptable record covers what happened, what was given, what was observed, and what was changed.

  • Pre-procedure baseline: weight, height, vital signs, ionized calcium (when available), recent labs, current medications (especially ACE-i status), allergies, replacement fluid plan.
  • Intra-procedure log: vital signs every 5–15 min, fluid balance running totals, symptoms reported by patient, calcium given (route, dose, time), any alarm events, any rate changes.
  • Adverse event documentation: time of onset, vital signs trend, intervention(s), response, contributing factors, follow-up plan.
  • Post-procedure summary: total volume processed, replacement given, fluid balance, calcium dose total, patient tolerance, post-procedure vital signs, plan for next session.
  • Handoff communication: SBAR to receiving nurse including pending labs, next procedure timing, and any safety concerns.
  • Reportable events: any anaphylaxis, TRALI, severe hypocalcemia, line-related complication, or near-miss should be entered into the institutional safety event system.
If it wasn't documented, it didn't happen. If it's a near-miss, document the lesson — the next nurse on this patient deserves to know.
