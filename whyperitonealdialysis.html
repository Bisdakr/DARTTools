Why Peritoneal Dialysis?
Using the patient's own peritoneum as a living dialysis membrane — physiology, modalities, color-coded Dianeal solutions, Baxter cyclers, aseptic technique, peritonitis prevention, and the future of home dialysis.

TL;DR — The 60-second version
Peritoneal dialysis turns the patient's own peritoneal membrane into a dialyzer. A surgically placed Tenckhoff catheter delivers sterile, glucose-loaded fluid into the abdominal cavity. The dialysate dwells for a programmed period — small solutes (urea, K, creatinine) diffuse from blood vessels in the peritoneum into the dialysate; water follows the osmotic gradient created by dextrose. The spent fluid is drained out, replaced, and the cycle repeats. Done at home overnight on a Baxter cycler, PD restores enough kidney function for the patient to work, travel, sleep in their own bed, and avoid the thrice-weekly hemodialysis schedule. About 10% of US dialysis patients are on PD; uptake is rising under value-based payment reform.

01
How Peritoneal Dialysis Works
Three physical processes happening inside your abdomen

The peritoneum is a thin serous membrane that lines the abdominal cavity and covers most of the abdominal organs. Highly vascularized — with capillary loops sitting just under its mesothelial surface — it is, fortuitously, a near-perfect dialysis membrane. Total surface area in adults is roughly 1–2 m² (comparable to a synthetic dialyzer), and the membrane is freely permeable to small solutes while retaining proteins.

PD works through three simultaneous processes:

ProcessWhat it doesDriven by
DiffusionSmall solutes (urea, K, creatinine, phosphate) move from blood into dialysateConcentration gradient
Osmosis (ultrafiltration)Water moves from blood into dialysate; carries dissolved solute with itOsmotic gradient (dextrose or icodextrin)
ConvectionMiddle molecules (β2-microglobulin) move with the water flowBulk fluid movement
🔑
The peritoneum is the dialyzerThe fundamental difference from hemodialysis is that no synthetic membrane is involved. The patient's own peritoneum does the filtering. This means PD's clearance capacity is fixed by the patient's anatomy and physiology — you can't swap in a "bigger filter." You can only change the fill volume, dwell time, and number of cycles.

A Tenckhoff catheter — a silicone tube with two Dacron cuffs anchored in the subcutaneous tunnel and around the muscle layer — delivers dialysate into and out of the cavity. The catheter exits through a tunnel that, when healed, forms a biological seal against infection. Daily exit-site care is therefore non-negotiable.

A Brief History
From WWII desperation to in-home automation
1923
German physician Georg Ganter performs the first human PD using saline lavage of the peritoneum — abandoned as crude and infection-prone.
1946
Frank, Seligman & Fine perform PD in acute uremia survivors at Boston City Hospital — first reproducible clinical PD.
1968
Henry Tenckhoff in Seattle develops the cuffed silicone catheter that bears his name — the breakthrough that made chronic PD feasible by preventing pericatheter leaks and infection.
1976
Popovich and Moncrief publish the principle of CAPD (Continuous Ambulatory PD) — four daily manual exchanges that keep dialysis running 24/7 without a machine.
1980s
Twin-bag systems introduced — separate drain and fill bags reduce peritonitis rates dramatically. Y-set and flush-before-fill technique become the standard.
1990s
Baxter HomeChoice automated cycler launches — enables CCPD (Continuous Cycling PD) at home overnight. Patients reclaim their days.
2003
Extraneal (icodextrin) FDA-approved — first non-glucose osmotic agent for sustained UF over long dwells.
2014
Baxter Amia launches with touchscreen interface and Sharesource cloud connectivity — clinicians can adjust prescriptions remotely.
2019
CMS Advancing American Kidney Health Initiative — major incentives for home dialysis. US PD enrollment grows for the first time in decades.
2023
ISPD updated guidelines on peritonitis prevention, biocompatible solutions, and adequacy — endorsing weekly Kt/V ≥ 1.7 plus residual function and patient-reported outcomes.

PD Modalities — The Five Flavors
CAPD, CCPD/APD, NIPD, Tidal, IPD
ModalityHow it runsBest for
CAPD
Continuous Ambulatory PD		4–5 manual exchanges/day; ~2L each; long overnight dwell. No machine.Patients who want autonomy, no electricity needed, ambulatory lifestyle.
CCPD / APD
Continuous Cycling PD		Cycler runs 8–10 hr overnight (3–6 cycles) + daytime "last fill" dwell.Working adults, school-age children — frees daytime.
NIPD
Nocturnal Intermittent PD		Cycler runs overnight only — dry day, no daytime dwell.High-transporter membrane phenotype; patients with hernia, leak, or back pain from daytime fill.
Tidal PDEach cycle drains only 40–80% of the previous fill, then refills. Cavity is never empty.Slow drainers; pain at end-drain; small-solute clearance optimization; long therapy windows.
IPD
Intermittent PD		Inpatient acute setting — high-volume continuous exchanges over 24 hr.Acute kidney injury, urgent-start PD before chronic prescription is set.
💡
Why Tidal existsSome patients have pain at end-drain — the catheter tip suctions against the peritoneum when the cavity is emptied. Tidal mode leaves a residual volume in the cavity, eliminating that pain. It also keeps diffusion going during what would otherwise be drain time, modestly improving small-solute clearance.

Mode selection is dynamic. A patient may start on CAPD during training, switch to CCPD when they return to work, add a daytime icodextrin exchange when residual UF falls, and convert to NIPD if they develop a hernia. The DART PD calculator supports all five.

Color-Coded Solutions
The four Baxter bags every PD nurse memorizes

Baxter Dianeal and Extraneal solutions are color-coded by dextrose strength — a visual safeguard against the wrong bag being hung at 3 AM. Memorize these four.

1.5%
Dianeal Yellow
Light UF · gentlest on glucose load · for stable patients with low UF requirements.
2.5%
Dianeal Green

4.25%
Dianeal Red

Ico

⚠
The bag color is a safety netThe colors are not aesthetic — they are an error-prevention system. When a tired night-shift nurse reaches into a supply room with three different strengths, the color tells the brain what's about to be hung. Always read the label too — but color is the first check.

Baxter also makes Physioneal — a pH-neutral, low-GDP (glucose degradation product) bicarbonate/lactate buffered Dianeal alternative. Better biocompatibility, may preserve peritoneal membrane long-term. Available in 1.36% (≈1.5%), 2.27% (≈2.5%), and 3.86% (≈4.25%) strengths.

🧪
Nutrineal & Amino Acid PDNutrineal uses 1.1% amino acid mixture as the osmotic agent instead of dextrose — a niche option for malnourished PD patients who can use the osmolarity for UF while absorbing protein. Once-daily maximum due to acid-base concerns.
06
Dextrose Physics — Why Bag Strength = Fluid Pull
How an osmotic gradient becomes ultrafiltration

Dextrose is the osmotic engine of conventional PD solutions. The mechanism is direct: dialysate dextrose creates an osmolar concentration far higher than plasma. Water moves across the peritoneal membrane from blood into the cavity down that gradient, dragging dissolved solute with it. The net water removal — ultrafiltration (UF) — is what relieves fluid overload.

SolutionOsmolarity (mOsm/L)Typical UF over 4 hr dwell
1.5% (Yellow)~346~100–200 mL
2.5% (Green)~396~300–400 mL
4.25% (Red)~485~500–700 mL
Icodextrin (Purple)~284 (iso-osmolar)~400–600 mL over 8–16 hr (sustained)
🔑
The dextrose problemGlucose is small and rapidly absorbed across the peritoneum. Within 2–4 hours, much of the dextrose has crossed into the patient's blood — destroying the osmotic gradient and stopping (or reversing) UF. This is why dextrose-based dwells get shorter, not longer, for maximum UF.
🔑
Why icodextrin for long dwellsIcodextrin is a glucose polymer (starch-derived). Too large to cross the peritoneum quickly, it creates a sustained colloid osmotic gradient over 8–16 hours. Unlike dextrose, UF does not collapse halfway through. Limit: once per 24 hr because of slow lymphatic clearance.
⚠
Glycemic load — a real considerationA patient on 4.25% bags daily absorbs 100–200 g of glucose per day. Over time this contributes to insulin resistance, weight gain, dyslipidemia, and peritoneal membrane fibrosis. Conserve red bags for true UF emergencies; icodextrin substitutes for many long-dwell red bag use cases.
07
Standard vs Low-Fill Mode
The same machine, different fill ceiling

Baxter's HomeChoice Claria and Amia cyclers support a low-fill mode that allows fills as small as 60 mL per cycle. Why does this matter?

ModeFill rangeTypical use
Standard mode100 mL → 3000 mL per cycleAdults, older children
Low-fill mode60 mL → 1500 mL per cycleInfants, neonates, very small children (down to ~3 kg)

The pump pressures, drain detection algorithms, and minimum drain volumes are tuned differently in low-fill mode. Selecting the wrong mode for a small patient can lead to under-drain alarms, drain pain, or incomplete therapy.

💡
How to chooseLow-fill is typically selected for any patient where the prescribed fill is under ~500 mL per cycle. The DART PD calculator's weight-based fill safety check flags pediatric ranges where low-fill should be considered.
08
Pediatric Peritoneal Dialysis
BSA-based fills, low-fill cyclers, and weight thresholds

PD is the dominant chronic dialysis modality for infants and small children — the technical challenges of vascular access make hemodialysis impractical below ~10 kg, while the peritoneum scales well to any body size.

Age / sizeFill targetCycler mode
Neonate (< 5 kg)10–20 mL/kg start; titrate to 30–40 mL/kgLow-fill, small-bag manual exchanges initially
Infant (5–10 kg)20–30 mL/kg start; 30–40 mL/kg goalLow-fill cycler
Toddler / Child (> 10 kg)800–1100 mL/m² BSA goal (ISPD)Standard or low-fill depending on volume
Adolescent1100–1400 mL/m² BSA (adult-equivalent)Standard mode
🔑
BSA, not weight, for kidsOnce a child is > 10 kg, prescribing by mL/m² body surface area outperforms mL/kg because BSA correlates more closely with peritoneal surface area. The DART PD calculator computes BSA via the Mosteller equation and uses 800–1100 mL/m² as the pediatric safety band.

Special pediatric considerations: avoid icodextrin in infants (insufficient data and acid-base concerns); start with shorter dwells (45–60 min) and scale up; consider a fluoroscopy-guided Tenckhoff placement to verify catheter position; and never skip the daily fill-volume tolerance check (look for respiratory distress, abdominal distension, or vomiting that signals over-fill).

Aseptic Technique — The Non-Negotiable
Why PD is a war against peritonitis

The peritoneum has no resident bacterial flora. Any bug introduced — usually via the catheter at the connection-disconnection step — has a near-free run of the cavity. Peritonitis is the leading reason patients drop out of PD. Every connection is therefore performed under strict aseptic technique.

  • Dedicated, clean room. No pets, no open windows during exchange. Pet dander and droplet contamination from animal exposure are linked to peritonitis outbreaks (especially with Pasteurella).
  • Hand hygiene. Surgical scrub for ≥ 1 minute with antimicrobial soap, dry with single-use paper towel, then alcohol-based hand rub.
  • Surgical mask (and patient mask). Both patient and caregiver wear masks during connection — droplets from speech are a documented source of Streptococcus salivarius peritonitis.
  • Sterile field. Disinfect work surface; assemble all supplies before opening the catheter.
  • Flush-before-fill. Drain the bag's first ~100 mL through the drain line before infusing — clears any contaminant introduced at connection.
  • Exit-site care. Daily cleansing with chlorhexidine or povidone-iodine; mupirocin or gentamicin cream prophylactically. Inspect site every exchange.
  • Catheter handling. Never reuse a transfer set; replace per institutional schedule (typically every 6 months). Always cap when disconnected.
  • Sharps and equipment. No scissors near the catheter. No fingers in the bag opening. Avoid touch contamination of the spike.
⚠
The cardinal rule of PD nursingWhen in doubt, don't connect. If sterility is compromised, a new bag and new transfer set cost less than a peritonitis admission. Document and replace.

Peritonitis — Recognition & Response
The single most common PD complication

Peritonitis presents with the triad: cloudy effluent, abdominal pain, and fever. Cloudy effluent alone — even without pain — should prompt immediate evaluation. ISPD guidelines define peritonitis as ≥ 2 of: typical clinical features, effluent WBC > 100/µL with > 50% neutrophils, and positive effluent culture.

Organism categoryTypical sourceEmpiric coverage
Gram-positive (Staph aureus, coag-negative Staph, Strep)Skin/touch contaminationVancomycin or cefazolin IP
Gram-negative (E. coli, Klebsiella, Pseudomonas)Bowel translocation; severe constipationCeftazidime or aminoglycoside IP
Fungal (Candida)Prior antibiotic courses; severely immunosuppressedCatheter removal mandatory; antifungal IV
PolymicrobialBowel perforationSurgical consult; broad-spectrum IV + IP

Empiric IP antibiotics cover both Gram-positive and Gram-negative organisms (commonly vancomycin + ceftazidime), administered intraperitoneally in the dialysate at therapeutic concentrations. Most peritonitis resolves with 14–21 days of treatment. Catheter removal is required for refractory bacterial peritonitis (no response by day 5), recurrent peritonitis, fungal peritonitis, or mycobacterial peritonitis.

⚠
The peritonitis rate targetISPD recommends < 0.4 episodes per patient-year. Units exceeding 0.5 should review their training, exchange technique, and exit-site protocols. Repeat peritonitis is associated with technique failure and conversion to hemodialysis.

Prevention investments — exit-site mupirocin, mask use, dedicated exchange space, patient retraining — consistently outperform treatment investments dollar-for-dollar.

Adequacy — Weekly Kt/V and the PET
How we know PD is enough

PD adequacy is measured as weekly Kt/V urea. The 2020 ISPD guidelines target weekly Kt/V ≥ 1.7 from PD plus residual renal function combined. Patient-reported outcomes (well-being, sleep, appetite, work capacity) are now weighted equally with the Kt/V number in modern practice.

Adequacy metricTargetWhat it measures
Weekly Kt/V urea≥ 1.7Urea clearance × time / body water (PD + residual)
Weekly creatinine clearance≥ 50 L/1.73m²Adequate small-solute clearance
Net daily UF> 750 mL (anuric) or sufficient for fluid balanceFluid removal adequacy
Residual urinePreserved as long as possibleHighest-value clearance — protect it
🔑
PET — Peritoneal Equilibration TestThe PET classifies a patient's peritoneum as high, high-average, low-average, or low transporter. High transporters absorb glucose quickly (UF collapses fast) and benefit from short dwells (CCPD/NIPD). Low transporters maintain gradients longer and tolerate long dwells (CAPD with long overnight dwell). The PET typically performed at 1 month after PD start and repeated annually.

Residual renal function (any urine output) is the single most valuable predictor of PD success. Strategies to preserve it: avoid nephrotoxins, use ACE inhibitors/ARBs as tolerated, avoid hypotensive dialysis sessions, and consider iso-osmolar icodextrin to minimize osmotic stress on the kidneys.

12
2024–2025 Developments
Where PD is going
🏠
Home dialysis policy wind at PD's backCMS's End-Stage Renal Disease Treatment Choices (ETC) model and bundled payment reforms continue to financially incentivize home modalities. US PD prevalence has grown for ten consecutive years — the longest growth streak in PD's history. New centers are opening dedicated PD training programs.
🧪
Biocompatible solutions becoming standardPhysioneal, Balance, and similar pH-neutral, low-GDP, bicarbonate-buffered solutions show preserved peritoneal membrane function on serial PET testing. Adoption is now standard in Europe and growing in the US.
🤖
Sharesource AI prescription supportBaxter is rolling out AI-assisted prescription optimization in Sharesource — analyzing UF trends, drain alarms, and patient-reported outcomes to suggest prescription tweaks before the patient develops fluid overload or under-clearance.
🔬
Urgent-start PD pathwaysThe traditional 2-week wait between catheter placement and PD initiation is being challenged by urgent-start PD protocols (low-volume, supine fills starting 24–72 hours after placement). Data show comparable peritonitis rates and substantial savings vs starting hemodialysis as a bridge.
🧬
PD as bridge to transplantThe kidney transplant queue has lengthened. PD's preservation of residual renal function and lower vascular access burden make it the preferred bridging modality for transplant candidates — particularly in younger patients.
📱
Patient-reported outcomes get teethThe SONG-PD initiative (Standardized Outcomes in Nephrology — PD) has standardized PROs across PD trials. Sleep quality, social participation, and "ability to travel" are now formally measured alongside lab values.
Peritoneal dialysis is the oldest modern dialysis modality and quietly the most futuristic — the patient runs their own treatment in their own bedroom while a cloud platform watches every cycle.
