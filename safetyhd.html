HD Safety Considerations
Intradialytic hypotension, disequilibrium syndrome, UFR ceilings, vascular access, dialyzer reactions, and the safety of the most common chronic dialysis modality. Compiled from KDOQI, KDIGO, AAMI standards, and current nephrology safety literature.

TL;DR — The safety story in one paragraph
Chronic hemodialysis carries the highest cumulative safety burden of any apheresis-related modality because it's performed three times weekly for years. The dominant intra-procedure issue is intradialytic hypotension (IDH, ~10–30% of sessions), driven by aggressive ultrafiltration. KDOQI's UFR < 13 mL/kg/hr ceiling is the single most important safety threshold — above this, mortality and end-organ stunning (myocardial, cerebral, mesenteric) climb sharply. Acute disequilibrium syndrome from rapid solute shifts is now rare with appropriate first-session pacing. Long-term, vascular access (CLABSI, fistula thrombosis, AV graft infection) is the biggest cumulative complication, followed by the cardiovascular consequences of repeated hemodynamic stress.

02
Intradialytic Hypotension
The most common adverse event in chronic HD

IDH is defined as SBP drop > 20 mmHg or MAP drop > 10 mmHg from pre-dialysis baseline, with symptoms. Multiple etiologies: excessive UFR, autonomic dysfunction (especially diabetic patients), cardiac dysfunction, sepsis, anti-hypertensives, food intake.

  • Pre-dialysis assessment: weight, BP, dry-weight evaluation, target UF goal
  • Slow start: first 30 min at lower BFR (250 mL/min) if hypotension-prone
  • BP monitoring: every 30 min standard, every 15 min if hypotension-prone
  • Cool dialysate (35.5°C): reduces IDH frequency significantly
  • Sodium modeling: higher Na at start, lower at end — but increases interdialytic thirst
  • Avoid food intake during dialysis in hypotension-prone patients (post-prandial vasodilation)
  • Hold anti-hypertensives morning of dialysis
  • If IDH occurs: stop or slow UF, Trendelenburg, IV bolus 100–250 mL NS, reassess dry weight
⚠
Recurrent IDH = end-organ stunningRepeated IDH causes myocardial stunning, white-matter ischemic injury, mesenteric ischemia, and accelerates cardiovascular mortality. Don't normalize recurrent IDH — investigate dry weight, anti-hypertensive timing, cardiac function, autonomic neuropathy.
03
UFR Safety — The 13 mL/kg/hr Rule
KDOQI's single most important threshold

Observational data from Flythe et al. and others show that UFR > 13 mL/kg/hr is independently associated with increased mortality, myocardial stunning, and cardiovascular events. UFR > 10 is a yellow-zone caution band.

UFRRisk bandAction
< 10 mL/kg/hrSafeStandard monitoring
10–13 mL/kg/hrKDIGO cautionCloser BP monitoring; reassess dry weight
> 13 mL/kg/hrKDOQI ceiling exceededExtend session time OR reduce UF goal OR add session — do not exceed
🔑
The arithmeticFor a 70 kg patient: UFR = total UF (mL) / time (hr) / weight (kg). 2,500 mL UF over 4 hr = 625 mL/hr ÷ 70 kg = 8.9 mL/kg/hr (safe). 4,000 mL over 4 hr = 14.3 mL/kg/hr (exceeds ceiling — extend to 5 hr → 11.4 mL/kg/hr).

Patient education about interdialytic weight gain (target < 2.5 kg between sessions) is part of UFR safety. Counsel on sodium and fluid intake.

04
Disequilibrium Syndrome
Rare now — but devastating when it occurs

Dialysis disequilibrium syndrome (DDS) results from rapid solute clearance during the first dialysis sessions of a severely uremic patient. Urea drops faster in plasma than in brain, creating an osmotic gradient that draws water into brain tissue → cerebral edema → headache, nausea, seizure, coma.

  • Highest risk: first 1–3 sessions of severely uremic patient (BUN > 150)
  • Modern prevention strategies (largely effective):
    • Short first session (1–2 hr)
    • Low BFR (150–200 mL/min)
    • Small dialyzer (low surface area)
    • Gradual UF
    • Cool dialysate
    • Hypertonic mannitol during session if very high BUN
  • Symptoms during/after session: headache, nausea, restlessness, AMS, seizure
  • Response: stop dialysis, mannitol, supportive care, neurology consult if severe
05
Vascular Access Complications
The dominant long-term morbidity

HD access is the single largest source of HD morbidity and a major mortality driver. Sequential priority: AV fistula > AV graft > tunneled catheter.

AccessMajor complicationsSurveillance
AV fistulaThrombosis, stenosis, aneurysm, steal syndromeThrill / bruit assessment each session; flow studies; vein mapping for maturation
AV graftHigher thrombosis rate, infection, pseudoaneurysmSame as fistula; lower CLABSI than catheter
Tunneled catheterCLABSI (10× higher than fistula), thrombosis, mechanical failureDaily exit site assessment; strict sterile technique; transition to fistula ASAP
⚠
CLABSI preventionCatheter-related bloodstream infection is the leading cause of HD-associated hospitalization and mortality. Strict sterile technique at every access connection, chlorhexidine dressings, antibiotic lock per protocol, and aggressive transition to fistula are non-negotiable.

Hand hygiene, sterile barriers, chlorhexidine prep with adequate dry time, and dedicated nursing staff are evidence-based bundle elements.

06
Membrane / Dialyzer Reactions
First-use, complement activation, anaphylaxis

Modern dialyzers are highly biocompatible. First-use anaphylactoid reactions (Type A, EtO-sterilant-related) and complement-mediated reactions (Type B) are now rare with modern e-beam or gamma-sterilized synthetic polysulfone / polyethersulfone membranes.

  • Type A (anaphylactoid): immediate (first 30 min) — flushing, dyspnea, hypotension, GI symptoms. STOP, supportive care, switch dialyzer brand
  • Type B (complement-mediated): 30–60 min in — chest/back pain, dyspnea, mild hypotension. Usually resolves; can switch dialyzer if recurrent
  • Pruritus / urticaria: usually idiopathic uremic pruritus, not dialyzer reaction
07
Electrolyte Management — The K Bath
Arrhythmia risk from steep gradients

K bath selection is the single most common cause of inpatient HD-related arrhythmia. Aggressive K removal (K=1 bath against high serum K) creates steep early gradients and can precipitate ectopy or unstable rhythms.

K = 1Severe hyperkalemia (> 6.5) refractory to medical therapyArrhythmia from rapid K shift
K = 2Standard chronic; serum K 5.0–6.0Moderate

Bicarbonate, calcium, sodiumRoutine baths: HCO3 32–35, Ca 2.25–3.0 mEq/L, Na 137–140. Higher Na = better hemodynamics but more interdialytic thirst. Lower temp (35.5°C) reduces IDH.
08
Pediatric Hemodialysis
ECV margin, sedation, slow first sessions
  • ECV ceiling 10% TBV — blood prime for circuits exceeding this
  • Smaller dialyzers (Optiflux F160NR / Revaclear 300, neonatal husky tubing)
  • Lower BFR (3–5 mL/kg/min)
  • Shorter sessions (often 3 hr) and tighter UFR ceilings
  • Slow first sessions in pediatric uremic patients — DDS risk is real
  • Sedation often needed for younger children; coordinate with anesthesia / PICU
  • Catheter management requires meticulous CLABSI prevention
09
Emergency Response
When something goes wrong
⚠ Severe Intradialytic Hypotension
  1. STOP UF (set to zero); continue dialysis at low flow.
  2. Trendelenburg position; 100% O₂.
  3. IV crystalloid bolus 100–250 mL NS through circuit.
  4. If unresponsive: reassess dry weight, investigate cardiac event, sepsis, bleeding.
  5. Sustained hypotension: stop dialysis, admit if outpatient, cardiac workup.
⚠ Anaphylactoid Reaction (First-Use)
  1. STOP dialysis immediately; do not return circuit blood.
  2. IM epinephrine 0.3–0.5 mg lateral thigh.
  3. Standard anaphylaxis protocol: antihistamines, corticosteroids, IV fluids, oxygen.
  4. Document dialyzer brand/lot; switch to different membrane for next session.
⚠ Air Embolism (Rare but Catastrophic)
  1. STOP all flow immediately; clamp venous line.
  2. Position patient left lateral decubitus, Trendelenburg (traps air in right atrium).
  3. 100% O₂ via non-rebreather.
  4. Call code response; consider hyperbaric oxygen if cerebral symptoms.
  5. Root-cause analysis; equipment check.
