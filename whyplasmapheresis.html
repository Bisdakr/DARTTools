Why Plasmapheresis?
From civil-war-era plasma fractionation to anti-NMDA encephalitis: a deep but readable guide to the science, the diseases, and the parameter choices behind every Optia TPE run.

TL;DR — The 60-second version
Therapeutic plasma exchange (TPE) is a plumbing solution to an immunology problem. Pathogenic antibodies, immune complexes, paraproteins, and toxins live in the plasma compartment. The Optia draws blood from the patient, spins it in a centrifuge to peel plasma off the cells, sends that plasma to a waste bag, and gives the patient back the cells plus a replacement fluid (albumin, FFP, or both). One pass through removes most of the patient's plasma volume — the immune mediators with it. Repeat over days, and you can pull a patient out of a TTP crisis, a Guillain-Barré paralysis, or a kidney-transplant rejection. It's been doing this since the 1950s, and the 2023 ASFA guidelines now recognize 77 diseases with 119 indications.

01
How It Actually Works
A centrifuge, two pumps, and a basic law of physics

Whole blood is a colloidal suspension: dense red cells (Hct ≈ 35–45%) suspended in a watery plasma (the other 55–65%). Spin it fast enough, and these two phases separate by density — RBCs at the bottom, plasma on top. That's the whole secret. Everything else is engineering.

In a TPE procedure, the Optia draws blood from a venous catheter into a tubing circuit at 60–100 mL/min. The blood enters a continuous-flow centrifuge channel where it accelerates around a curved track. RBCs and WBCs (the dense fraction) pack to the outside; plasma (the light fraction) accumulates toward the inside. A plasma collection port skims off the plasma layer and routes it to a waste bag. The cells come back to the patient through the return line, alongside a replacement fluid (5% albumin, fresh frozen plasma, or a mixture) hung on the machine.

🔑
Key concept The therapeutic effect comes from removing solutes that live in plasma — pathogenic IgG, IgM, immune complexes, paraproteins, monoclonal light chains, anti-GBM antibodies, vWF multimers in TTP, free thyroid hormone in thyroid storm, copper-albumin complexes in Wilson disease. The replacement fluid keeps the patient's intravascular volume and oncotic pressure stable; it doesn't have to do the cleaning.

The volume removed is what defines "intensity" of exchange. A 1.0 plasma volume exchange (PVE) removes roughly 60% of intravascular IgG and similar fractions of other plasma proteins (math: 1 − 1/e). A 1.5 PVE pushes that to about 78%. There are diminishing returns past 1.5× — you're processing a lot of blood for incrementally smaller removal, and re-equilibration from the extravascular pool refills what you took out within hours.

💡
The math behind PVE The Optia targets Plasma Removed = TBV × (1 − Hct) × PVE. For a 70 kg patient with Hct 35%: TBV ≈ 5 L → plasma volume ≈ 3.25 L → at 1.2 PVE, plasma removed ≈ 3.9 L. The replacement bag is slightly smaller than that (more in §06).

What makes apheresis therapeutic isn't just removal. By giving back albumin (no antibodies), FFP (clotting factors + ADAMTS13), or a split, you can correct the deficit the underlying disease created. TTP gets ADAMTS13 back in the form of FFP. ALF patients get clotting factors. Patients with hyperviscosity get a less viscous plasma to live in.

02
The Optia in 60 Seconds
What's actually happening inside the machine

The Spectra Optia Apheresis System (Terumo BCT) is what most US apheresis centers use today. It's the successor to the COBE Spectra, which itself replaced the Haemonetics V50 — each generation faster, smarter, more automated. The Optia's centrifuge isn't a bowl; it's a single-use sealed channel with a translucent window that lets an optical sensor read the position of the cell-plasma interface in real time. The machine actively steers the interface to keep plasma extraction clean — that's why the operator can mostly let it run while watching the patient.

Three pumps run the show:

PumpRoleTypical rate
InletDraws whole blood + AC from patient into the centrifuge50–100 mL/min adult
ACInfuses ACD-A citrate into the blood lineInlet flow ÷ ratio (e.g., 70 ÷ 10 = 7 mL/min)
PlasmaExtracts plasma waste from the centrifuge~50–80 mL/min (varies with Hct)
ReplacementReturns albumin/FFP to the patient~30–60 mL/min
🔬
What's in the circuit The Optia's TPE tubing set holds about 185 mL of fluid, plus another 50 mL in the warmer line. That 235 mL extracorporeal volume (ECV) matters: in a pediatric patient under 25 kg, that 235 mL might be 20% of total blood volume — which is why the DART calculator surfaces a prime-decision banner (NS prime, albumin prime, or diluted PRBC prime) based on ECV/TBV %.

The machine also continuously monitors AC Infusion Rate (AIR), expressed in mL/min/L of patient TBV. The default cap is 1.2 mL/min/L TBV — above that, the patient's ionized calcium drops too fast and citrate toxicity (perioral tingling, then carpopedal spasm, eventually arrhythmia) becomes likely. Tighten the AC ratio (from 12:1 to 8:1), and the Optia automatically reduces inlet flow to stay under the AIR cap. That's why your throughput goes down when you go from a standard 10:1 ratio to a Bluebird-gene-therapy 8:1.

03
A Short History
From WWII plasma fractionation to modern automated apheresis

Plasma exchange has a longer history than most clinicians realize. The intellectual roots go back to the 1940s and a Harvard biochemist named Edwin J. Cohn.

1944 · Cohn fractionation
The Cohn ethanol fractionation method is published
Wartime need for stable blood-derived therapeutics (albumin, immunoglobulin, fibrinogen) drives the development of cold ethanol precipitation. The methodology that creates "5% albumin" in your replacement bag today was developed during World War II.
1959 · First therapeutic plasmapheresis
Schwab and Fahey treat Waldenström macroglobulinemia hyperviscosity
The first deliberate use of plasma exchange to treat human disease. Crude manual technique — phlebotomy, plasma separation, return of red cells — but it worked.
1970s · Continuous-flow centrifuges
Haemonetics, IBM 2997, Cobe Spectra arrive
Continuous-flow apheresis replaces manual exchanges. Procedures shrink from hours to a couple of hours; plasma volumes become predictable.
1976–1991 · ASFA forms; first guidelines
American Society for Apheresis founded (1976); first category guidelines published 1986
The 4-category system (I = first-line through IV = ineffective/harmful) becomes the gold standard for indication decision-making.
2010s · Spectra Optia replaces COBE
Software-driven interface management, automated TPE, RBCx, MNC, ECP protocols on one platform
Terumo BCT acquires CaridianBCT, consolidates the Optia. Apheresis becomes a software discipline as much as a hardware one.
2023 · ASFA 9th Special Issue
77 diseases · 119 indications · updated category & grade for each
The current authoritative classification. Notable shifts: anti-NMDA encephalitis upgraded to Category I; aHUS with anti-FH antibody Cat I; FSGS recurrence post-transplant Cat I; CAR-T-cell collection cycle integration; gene therapy collection workflows codified.
If you can identify the molecule, and that molecule lives in plasma, you have a candidate for plasma exchange.
— Loose paraphrase of the modern apheresis ethos

💭
A pattern you'll notice The strongest evidence (Category I) clusters in diseases where a single, well-defined molecule drives the pathology: ADAMTS13 inhibitor in TTP, anti-AChR in MG, anti-GBM in Goodpasture, anti-NMDA-R in NMDA encephalitis, anti-FH in aHUS. When the target is murky (SLE-broad, ANCA-broad), evidence weakens and ASFA drops to II or III.
05
The ASFA Category System
Four buckets, two axes

Every ASFA indication has two labels: a Category (I–IV) and a Grade (1A through 2C). Together they tell you the strength of recommendation and the quality of evidence behind it.

Category I
First-line therapy
Apheresis is accepted as primary treatment, either standalone or as part of a multi-modal regimen. Strong evidence base.
Category II
Second-line therapy
Apheresis is accepted as second-line, either standalone or with other modalities. Evidence supportive but not first-line.
Category III
Individualized
Optimum role of apheresis is not established. Decision-making should be individualized — patient-by-patient.
Category IV
Ineffective or harmful
Published evidence demonstrates or suggests apheresis is ineffective or harmful. IRB review recommended if used.

Grading uses the GRADE methodology: 1 = strong recommendation, 2 = weak recommendation; A = high-quality evidence, B = moderate, C = low. So 1A is the strongest possible recommendation (TTP); 2C is a weak recommendation based on low-quality evidence (most Cat III indications).

⚠
A category is not a free pass A Cat I indication means TPE is well-established for that disease, not that TPE is appropriate for every patient with that disease. The treating physician still needs to assess the individual: is the antibody-driven pathology the active problem? Is the patient stable enough? What's the goal — bridge to definitive therapy, or rescue from crisis?
06
Why 1.0 vs 1.2 vs 1.5 PVE?
The arithmetic of antibody removal

This is one of the most common parameter questions, and the answer is satisfyingly mathematical.

Plasma exchange is a first-order kinetic process. If you removed plasma without simultaneous replacement, you'd extract everything in linear fashion. But replacement happens in real time — fresh albumin or FFP is going in as patient plasma is going out — so the patient's remaining target molecule concentration follows an exponential decay:

📐
The formula Fraction of target remaining = e−PVE

1.0 PVE → 1/e ≈ 0.37 remaining = 63% removed
1.2 PVE → e−1.2 ≈ 0.30 remaining = 70% removed
1.5 PVE → e−1.5 ≈ 0.22 remaining = 78% removed
2.0 PVE → e−2.0 ≈ 0.14 remaining = 86% removed

Look at the diminishing returns. Going from 1.0 → 1.5 PVE removes another 15%, but you're processing 50% more blood. Going from 1.5 → 2.0 only buys you another 8%, and the procedure takes significantly longer. That's why 1.0–1.5 PVE is the clinical sweet spot, and 2.0+ is reserved for special situations (e.g., very-large-molecule disease where extravascular pool is small).

There's a second reason. Once you remove circulating antibody, the extravascular pool (lymph, interstitium) starts re-equilibrating with the depleted intravascular pool. Within hours, antibody flows back from tissue to blood. A single session removing 78% (1.5 PVE) doesn't mean the patient's total body antibody pool dropped 78% — the intravascular concentration drops 78%, then partially refills overnight. This is why TPE is rarely done as a single session: course of 5–7 sessions every other day is the most common prescription.

IndicationTypical PVERationale
TTP (mild)1.0–1.5Daily exchanges until platelets recover; modest per-session intensity preferred
TTP (severe / neuro)1.5Maximize ADAMTS13 replenishment and inhibitor removal
Hyperviscosity1.0–1.5Rapid symptomatic relief — viscosity drops nonlinearly
Anti-GBM / RPGN1.0–1.5Daily course × 14 days typical
Wilson, fulminant1.5–2.0Maximal copper removal; bridge to transplant
Drug overdose (highly protein-bound)1.5–2.0Driven by toxicokinetics; one or two large exchanges

Citrate, AC Ratio, and That AIR Number
Why we trade calcium for non-clotted blood

The Optia uses ACD-A (Acid-Citrate-Dextrose, Formula A) as its anticoagulant. ACD-A is mostly water, dextrose, and citrate (sodium citrate + citric acid). Citrate works by chelating calcium — and since calcium is a required cofactor for the coagulation cascade, no calcium = no clotting.

Beautiful design, except the citrate also enters the patient's circulation. Inside the patient, citrate is rapidly metabolized by the liver (and to a lesser extent kidney, muscle) into bicarbonate within minutes — but while it's circulating, it's chelating the patient's own calcium too. That's where citrate toxicity comes from: perioral tingling, then carpopedal spasm, then arrhythmia at the extreme.

💡
AC Ratio explained The "Inlet:AC = 10:1" ratio displayed on the Optia means 1 mL of ACD-A is added for every 10 mL of total inlet flow. So if the inlet pump is at 70 mL/min, the AC pump is at 7 mL/min. Tighter ratios (8:1, 6:1) mean more AC per unit of blood — more anticoagulation, but also more citrate exposure.

The Optia continuously displays AIR (AC Infusion Rate) in mL/min per liter of patient TBV. This is the citrate dose-rate. The machine caps AIR at 1.2 mL/min/L TBV by hard ceiling — operator can't override. Above that, citrate symptoms are statistically inevitable.

AIR (mL/min/L TBV)Citrate effectSymptoms
< 0.8~10–15% iCa dropUncommon
0.8–1.0~15–25% iCa dropMild perioral tingling possible
1.0–1.2~20–35% iCa dropTingling, cramping, sometimes nausea
> 1.2Machine cap reachedInlet flow auto-reduced

So when you change AC ratio from 12:1 to 8:1 (e.g., for a Bluebird gene-therapy collection), the Optia must reduce inlet flow to stay under AIR 1.2 — and your total processing time goes up, and total blood processed goes down for the same run duration. There's no free lunch.

⚠
Citrate counterintuitively More AC ≠ more "safety." The dose-limiting issue isn't clotting risk (the machine has plenty of AC headroom); it's patient calcium. Standard 10:1 with prophylactic calcium gluconate infusion is the typical adult TPE setup.
08
Albumin vs FFP — How to Choose
The replacement fluid carries clinical weight, not just volume

Replacement fluid isn't a passive volume swap. It actively modulates what you give back to the patient. Three choices:

FluidWhat it givesWhat it doesn'tWhen you'd use it
5% Albumin Volume + oncotic pressure No clotting factors, no immunoglobulins, no enzymes (e.g., ADAMTS13) Default for most TPE indications — clean, no infectious risk, no transfusion reactions
FFP (Fresh Frozen Plasma) Volume + oncotic + clotting factors + ADAMTS13 + complement + everything else Carries transfusion risk (TRALI, TACO, allergic, ABO compatibility needed) TTP (essential — replenishes ADAMTS13), Acute Liver Failure (clotting factors), anti-FH aHUS (Factor H), CAPS with bleeding
Albumin + FFP split Most albumin volume + some FFP-supplied clotting factors Partial of either Long courses where you want to limit FFP exposure but maintain coag factors (e.g., daily TPE for prolonged GBS course)
🩸
TTP is a non-negotiable FFP case The pathology of TTP is ADAMTS13 deficiency. The enzyme cleaves ultra-large vWF multimers; without it, microthrombi form everywhere. Albumin replacement removes the inhibitor but doesn't replenish the enzyme — the patient stays in crisis. FFP supplies functional ADAMTS13. This is why the DART calculator auto-switches to FFP when you select TTP, and shows a red warning if you try to override with albumin.

Other FFP-required indications: anti-Factor H aHUS (FFP provides functional Factor H), acute liver failure (provides factors II, V, VII, IX, X, fibrinogen, ADAMTS13), and some CAPS cases when coagulopathy or DIC is active.

Complications — What to Watch For
The nursing-eye view of TPE risk

Adverse events occur in roughly 5–15% of TPE sessions, most of them mild. Severe events are rare (≪1%). The mental model:

Citrate toxicityCitrate chelates ionized Ca²⁺Most common (~5–10%)Slow inlet, IV calcium gluconate, oral calcium, antacid
HypotensionVolume shifts, vasovagal, AC effects2–5%Trendelenburg, fluid bolus, hold UF, slow flow
Allergic / urticarialFFP / albumin proteins1–3%Pause replacement, antihistamine, steroid if severe
AnaphylaxisFFP > albumin; ACE-i + dextran-sulfate< 0.5%Stop, epi, fluids, code if needed
Vascular accessCatheter dysfunction, infection, thrombosisVariableCheck patency, flush, escalate to access team
TRALI / TACO (FFP)FFP-related lung injury / overloadRarePulmonary support, diuretics if TACO, ICU if TRALI
CoagulopathyLong albumin runs deplete coagulation factorsSubclinical common, clinical rareTrend PT/aPTT/fibrinogen; switch to FFP if bleeding
💉
The calcium gluconate calculation The institutional rule is 400 mg of calcium gluconate per 500 mL of replacement (= 0.8 mg/mL replacement). For a typical 2.5 L replacement TPE: 2500 × 0.8 = 2000 mg of calcium gluconate, or 20 mL of the standard 10% solution. Usually given as a slow infusion piggyback or in divided IV pushes throughout the procedure.

Pediatric Considerations
When the patient is smaller than the circuit

Pediatric apheresis follows the same physics but with smaller margins for error. Three things change:

1. TBV calculation. The Nadler equation is calibrated for adults and underestimates pediatric blood volume. Institutional practice uses weight-banded mL/kg:

Age / weightTBV factor
Neonate (≤ 5 kg)90 mL/kg
Infant / small child (5.1–15 kg)80 mL/kg
Child (15.1–25 kg)70 mL/kg
Above 25 kgNadler equation (adult math)

2. Extracorporeal volume (ECV) as % of TBV. The Optia TPE/RBCX circuit holds 235 mL of fluid. For a 70 kg adult that's ~5% of TBV — trivial. For a 12 kg toddler it's ~25% — substantial. When ECV exceeds 10–15% of TBV, the patient can't tolerate the volume shift without a custom prime:

🔑
Pediatric prime decision (institutional) ≤ 10% TBV: Standard NS prime. Patient stable.
10–15% TBV: Consider Albumin Prime (closer 10%) or Diluted PRBC Prime at 35% Hct (closer 15%).
> 15% TBV: Strongly recommend Diluted PRBC Prime, 250 mL at 35% Hct. Maintains isovolemic state during initiation.
Albumin prime → rinseback ON (Optia auto-disables it; manually re-enable). Diluted blood prime → NO rinseback (maintains equilibrium).

3. AC tolerance. Citrate toxicity hits pediatric patients sooner because their liver mass is smaller relative to AC dose. Prophylactic calcium infusion is essentially mandatory, and AC ratio often goes to 12:1 or 13:1 (looser) for pediatric runs.

🔬
Tiny patients, real outcomes Successful TPE has been performed in patients as small as 3 kg for catastrophic indications (refractory neonatal lupus, fulminant Wilson). Requires meticulous prime calibration, real-time iCa monitoring, and a hemodynamics-trained physician at the bedside. The technique exists; the judgment is everything.
11
What's New (2023–2025)
Where the field is moving

The last two years have brought meaningful shifts.

🆕
Anti-NMDA encephalitis upgraded The 2023 ASFA guidelines reclassified anti-NMDA receptor encephalitis to Category I based on accumulating evidence that early immunotherapy (including TPE) improves long-term cognitive outcomes. Combination protocols — steroids + IVIG + TPE up front, often with rituximab — are now standard.
🧬
Gene therapy integration The arrival of lovo-cel (Lyfgenia, Bluebird Bio) and exa-cel (Casgevy) for sickle cell and TDT created a new role for apheresis services: collecting autologous HPC for ex-vivo gene editing. The collection technique borrows from HPC-A but with critical differences — no G-CSF in SCD (vaso-occlusive crisis risk), HbS < 30% pre-collection, plerixafor-driven mobilization, tight AC ratios (8:1 to 6:1), and DARK collection-preference settings on the Optia. The DART calculator integrates these as company-specific protocols.
📊
FSGS post-transplant breakthrough Multiple 2023–2024 studies demonstrated that early TPE initiation (within 7 days of recurrence detection) substantially improves kidney graft survival in recurrent FSGS — possibly via removal of the circulating permeability factor (suPAR remains the leading candidate, though debated). Institutional protocols now favor proactive monitoring of urinary protein post-transplant in high-risk recipients.
🧠
CAPS triple therapy outcomes The CAPS Registry (~600 patients) has matured enough to validate triple therapy (anticoagulation + steroids + TPE or IVIG) as the standard of care. Mortality dropped from historical ~50% to ~30%, with TPE-based regimens showing a modest edge over IVIG in the most recent analyses.
⚡
High-volume TPE for liver failure A 2024 multicenter trial confirmed earlier APACHE-trial signals: high-volume TPE (1.5–2× plasma volume per session, with FFP replacement) improves transplant-free survival in acute liver failure. The mechanism is multi-pronged: removal of hepatotoxins, supply of clotting factors, modulation of systemic inflammation. Not yet first-line where transplant is available, but increasingly used as a bridge.
12
Where the Field Is Going
Selective apheresis, AI, and beyond plasma

TPE has been doing the same thing for 60 years: remove plasma, replace fluid. The next decade may finally change the "remove plasma" part.

Selective immunoadsorption uses affinity columns (Staphylococcal protein A, polyclonal anti-IgG, or disease-specific ligands) to remove only the pathogenic antibody while returning the cleaned plasma to the patient. This avoids the need for replacement fluid entirely. Already approved in Europe for several indications; trials ongoing in the US. It's apheresis without the volume problem.

AI-guided procedure optimization is starting to show up in trial publications. Models trained on thousands of TPE sessions can predict optimal inlet flow, AC ratio, and replacement composition for individual patients based on their clinical state — potentially reducing adverse events and procedure time.

Engineered biopolymer adsorbents are in early-stage trials for sepsis (removing cytokines, endotoxin) and post-cardiac surgery inflammation. The principle: instead of dumping plasma to a waste bag, run it through a smart filter that removes only what's harmful.

The next era of apheresis is selectivity. Whole-plasma exchange is a hammer; the future is a scalpel.
— Common refrain at recent ASFA meetings
