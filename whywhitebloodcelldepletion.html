Education · Therapeutic Leukapheresis
Why WBC Depletion?
When hyperleukocytosis becomes leukostasis: a deep but readable guide to emergent cytoreduction via apheresis — what it does, what it doesn't, and how to bridge to definitive therapy.
01 · How It Works 02 · Hyperleukocytosis 03 · Leukostasis 04 · Indications 05 · Parameters 06 · A Bridge 07 · Complications 08 · Pediatrics 09 · 2024–2025 References
TL;DR — The 60-second version
Therapeutic leukapheresis (white blood cell depletion) is an emergency cytoreduction technique for patients with hyperleukocytosis — WBC counts so high (typically > 100 × 10³/µL in AML, often > 200 in CML blast crisis) that leukocytes themselves obstruct microcirculation, causing leukostasis — pulmonary failure, neurologic decline, organ damage, and death within hours. The Optia uses its CMNC protocol with collection preference set toward leukocyte capture; each procedure typically reduces circulating WBC by 25–45%. It buys time for definitive cytoreductive chemotherapy to take effect — but it doesn't cure the underlying leukemia. ASFA 2023 designates this as Category III because supporting trial data is limited, but the physiologic rationale and case-series benefit are strong in true leukostasis emergencies.

01
How Leukapheresis Works
Buffy-coat skimming with intent to discard

Leukapheresis uses the same Optia CMNC platform as stem cell collection — but instead of preserving the collected buffy coat as a therapeutic product, the operator discards it as biohazard waste. The goal is rapid removal of circulating white cells, not collection.

The centrifuge separates blood into RBC, buffy coat (where leukocytes concentrate), and plasma layers. The CMNC software actively maintains the buffy coat in the collection port, and the leukocyte-rich product is pulled off continuously. Patient cells minus leukocytes (plus replacement fluid if needed) returns through the venous line.

Each session processes 1.5–2.5 TBV of blood and removes a substantial fraction of circulating leukocytes — typically 25–45% reduction in measured WBC per session. The reduction isn't linear because pool re-equilibration from marrow happens fast; multiple daily sessions may be needed before WBC stabilizes below the symptomatic threshold.

💡
Discard, don't preserveBecause the collected buffy coat is biohazard waste, the institutional infectious-disease and waste-management protocols apply. The collection bag is sealed, labeled, and disposed of per hospital policy. This is the one apheresis procedure where the product is the problem, not the goal.
02
Hyperleukocytosis — The Numbers
When WBC counts become a physiologic problem
DiseaseHyperleukocytosis thresholdWhy
AMLWBC > 100 × 10³/µLLarge blasts (15–20 µm), rigid, undeformable; highest leukostasis risk
ALLWBC > 300 × 10³/µLSmaller lymphoblasts, lower viscosity risk per cell
CMLWBC > 300 × 10³/µL (often higher)Mature granulocytes; less stiff; tolerated until very high
CLLWBC > 400 × 10³/µLSmall mature lymphocytes; viscosity tolerated even higher

The thresholds differ because cell-size and rigidity vary by lineage. AML blasts are huge and stiff; they clog capillaries. CLL lymphocytes are small and deformable. So an AML patient with WBC 90 may be in active leukostasis while a CLL patient with WBC 400 is asymptomatic. The clinical question is always: is the patient actually symptomatic, not just numerically high?

💭
Hyperleukocytosis ≠ leukostasisHyperleukocytosis is a lab number. Leukostasis is a clinical syndrome. Many patients with hyperleukocytosis are asymptomatic — particularly chronic CML and CLL patients with mature, deformable cell types. Apheresis is reserved for symptomatic leukostasis or anticipated leukostasis in acute leukemia with stiff cells. Just having WBC > 100 doesn't earn an emergent apheresis order.
03
Leukostasis — The Clinical Emergency
When obstruction becomes organ failure

Leukostasis is the clinical syndrome where hyperleukocytosis causes microvascular obstruction. Mortality without intervention is high — historically up to 40% within 1 week of presentation in symptomatic AML.

⚠
Symptoms that demand emergent leukapheresisPulmonary: dyspnea, hypoxia, ground-glass opacities on CT. Neurologic: confusion, headache, vision changes, seizure, intracranial hemorrhage. Renal: AKI, oliguria. Bleeding: DIC complicating high blast count (especially APL). Priapism: in CML. Any of these + WBC at threshold = emergent procedure consideration.

The DART WBC Depletion calculator flags emergency status when any of these symptoms are checked OR when the diagnosis is selected as emergency-category (e.g., AML with leukostasis). The emergency banner persists throughout the result panel as a constant reminder of time-criticality.

Notably, APL (Acute Promyelocytic Leukemia) is generally not leukapheresed — the procedure is associated with worsening DIC, intracranial hemorrhage, and death. APL is ASFA Category IV (contraindicated/harmful in most series). Treatment is ATRA + cytoreductive chemo, with apheresis specifically avoided.

04
Indications & Anti-Indications
Who benefits, who doesn't
AML hyperleukocytosis (Cat III, but emergency) CML blast crisis (Cat III) CML chronic phase (Cat III) ALL hyperleukocytosis (Cat III) CLL symptomatic (Cat III) Priapism with leukostasis (Cat III)

Despite all being ASFA Category III, the practical urgency varies dramatically. AML with active leukostasis is a true emergency — the procedure may start within hours of presentation. Chronic CML or CLL leukapheresis for elevated counts without leukostasis symptoms is elective and rarely performed in the modern targeted-therapy era.

🚫
APL exceptionAcute Promyelocytic Leukemia (APL, AML M3) is the major contraindication to leukapheresis. The procedure has been associated with intracranial hemorrhage and worsening DIC. Treatment: ATRA + anthracycline-based chemo, avoiding leukapheresis even when WBC is elevated.

06
A Bridge, Not a Cure
What leukapheresis does and doesn't do

This is the most important conceptual frame: leukapheresis buys time, but it doesn't change the underlying disease. Within 24 hours of stopping, WBC re-equilibrates from the marrow back to peripheral blood. The procedure is a bridge until cytoreductive chemotherapy takes effect.

🔑
Concurrent cytoreduction is mandatoryHydroxyurea 50–100 mg/kg/day starts simultaneously with apheresis (or before, if patient already on the floor). For AML, induction chemotherapy (7+3 cytarabine + daunorubicin or equivalent) should not be delayed for apheresis. The apheresis nurse is part of an oncologic emergency team; the goal is keeping the patient alive long enough for definitive treatment to work.

Sessions continue until WBC drops below ~50 × 10³/µL or symptoms resolve. After that, cytoreductive chemo handles the disease and apheresis stops. The classic time course is 2–4 daily sessions over 2–4 days, with concurrent chemo doing the long-term work.

08
Pediatric Leukapheresis
Same physics, tighter margins

Pediatric hyperleukocytosis is most common in T-ALL with very high blast counts and in some AML subtypes. The institutional pediatric TBV formulas (90/80/70 mL/kg for neonate/infant/child) apply. The Optia ECV is 303 mL for CMNC — over 15% of TBV for any patient under ~15 kg, which triggers the diluted PRBC prime decision.

🩸
Viscosity caveat in peds hyperleukocytosisPediatric hyperleukocytic blood is even more viscous due to higher relative cell content. Diluted PRBC prime can worsen hyperviscosity if the prime adds total cell mass before apheresis brings the WBC down. Coordinate with hematology on prime timing — sometimes the prime is held until WBC drops below an initial threshold via the first portion of the procedure, then re-evaluated.
09
2024–2025 Developments
Refining the role
📊
Targeted therapy era reduces leukapheresis demandFLT3 inhibitors (gilteritinib), IDH inhibitors (ivosidenib, enasidenib), and venetoclax-based combinations have lowered presenting WBC counts in newly diagnosed AML — the actual number of patients reaching leukapheresis threshold has decreased. The procedure is increasingly reserved for cytogenetic or molecular subgroups where rapid hyperleukocytosis is more common.
📈
Survival meta-analysesRecent systematic reviews continue to show no overall mortality benefit from leukapheresis in AML, but a clear symptomatic improvement in active leukostasis. The current consensus: emergent leukapheresis for symptomatic leukostasis, not as a prophylactic measure for high counts without symptoms.
⚡
Concurrent venetoclax considerationsThe shift toward venetoclax + hypomethylating agents (azacitidine/decitabine) as front-line AML therapy in older adults has changed the cytoreduction landscape. Hydroxyurea + apheresis remains the bridge in symptomatic patients while waiting for venetoclax to take effect.
