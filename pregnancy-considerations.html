Pregnancy & Extracorporeal Therapy
Modality-specific guidance for pregnant patients on TPE, HD, PD, CRRT, LDL apheresis, and ECP. Indications, dosing adjustments, fetal monitoring, anticoagulation, replacement fluid selection, and obstetric coordination.

TL;DR — Two Patients, Coordinated Care
Every extracorporeal therapy in a pregnant patient involves two patients with intertwined physiology. The mother's volume status, blood pressure, electrolytes, anticoagulation, and any medication / replacement fluid choices affect uterine perfusion and fetal well-being. The single most important principle: coordinated care with MFM (Maternal-Fetal Medicine) from the first decision, with continuous fetal monitoring during procedures past viability, careful anticoagulation choices (heparin > citrate when possible past viability), and avoidance of teratogenic agents. ASFA recognizes pregnancy-specific TPE indications as Category I for several conditions.
01
General Principles
  • MFM consultation at the outset — Maternal-Fetal Medicine should be involved in every decision about extracorporeal therapy in pregnancy.
  • Position to avoid IVC compression — Past 20 weeks gestation, left lateral tilt (15–30°) during all procedures to prevent supine hypotension syndrome.
  • Hemodynamic margin — Aim for higher BP than in non-pregnant patients to maintain uterine perfusion; avoid intra-procedural hypotension.
  • Anticoagulation choice — Heparin does not cross the placenta and is preferred over citrate in many scenarios; in apheresis, citrate is generally safe but monitor for maternal hypocalcemia which can worsen uterine irritability.
  • Replacement fluid — Albumin is generally preferred to plasma to minimize transfusion-associated risks; coordinate with OB if coagulopathy / DIC concern.
  • Fetal monitoring — Continuous external fetal monitoring (EFM) during procedures past viability (≥ 24 weeks); intermittent at minimum at non-viable gestation per OB recommendation.
  • Medications — Verify pregnancy category / FDA category B vs. C vs. D vs. X. Many immunosuppressants used adjunctively (cyclophosphamide, mycophenolate) are teratogenic.
  • Anti-D Rh immunoprophylaxis — Verify Rh status; administer Rh immunoglobulin per OB if Rh negative and any potential exposure.
  • Vascular access — Avoid femoral if possible (DVT risk increased in pregnancy); internal jugular preferred. AVF is acceptable.

TPE in Pregnancy

Plasma exchange has well-established indications in obstetric / pregnancy-associated conditions per ASFA 2023 (9th Edition):

ConditionASFA CategoryTPE Approach
HELLP syndrome — postpartum, persistentCat III (decision individualized)1–1.5 PV exchange with FFP replacement when MAHA / TMA features persist beyond 72 hr postpartum.
Atypical HUS (aHUS) — pregnancy-associatedCat I (with eculizumab)1–1.5 PV with FFP; bridge to eculizumab; coordinate with MFM and nephrology.
TTP in pregnancyCat IUrgent 1–1.5 PV daily with FFP; continue until platelet recovery; pregnancy itself precipitates TTP relapse.
Antiphospholipid syndrome — catastrophic (CAPS) in pregnancyCat I1–1.5 PV with albumin/FFP; combined with anticoagulation and immunosuppression.
Rh isoimmunization (severe fetal HDFN)Cat III1–1.5 PV with albumin in second-trimester pregnancies with rising titers and inability to delay intrauterine transfusion; case-by-case.
Acute Fatty Liver of Pregnancy (AFLP)Cat IIITPE for hepatic encephalopathy and coagulopathy after delivery if not improving with supportive care.

NMOSD relapse in pregnancyCat IITPE typically after steroid failure; coordinate.
🧪
Citrate & PregnancyCitrate is generally tolerated. However, hypocalcemia (acute and prolonged) can worsen uterine irritability and contribute to preterm labor. Use the standard 15:1 inlet-to-citrate ratio (or as low as possible per protocol); calcium chloride infusion through a separate line is preferred over calcium gluconate alone; monitor ionized calcium more frequently than usual; consider electrolyte replacement during long procedures.
⚠
Replacement Fluid ChoicePlasma exchange in pregnancy: use albumin alone for non-bleeding indications. Use FFP when actively bleeding, with coagulopathy, or for TMA syndromes (TTP, HELLP, aHUS). Cryoprecipitate may be needed in HELLP or DIC.
03
Hemodialysis in Pregnancy

Pregnancy on hemodialysis carries significantly elevated risk of fetal loss, preterm delivery, IUGR, and preeclampsia. Intensified hemodialysis is associated with substantially improved fetal outcomes.

  • Frequency: Increase from 3× / week to 6 days / week (or daily nocturnal) for ≥ 36 hours per week of dialysis.
  • Target BUN: Pre-dialysis BUN < 35–45 mg/dL (uremia is teratogenic / fetotoxic; pregnancy-on-dialysis registries show better outcomes with low urea exposure).
  • Anticoagulation: Heparin first choice (does not cross placenta); avoid warfarin (teratogenic). LMWH acceptable; consider dose adjustment for pregnancy-associated changes.
  • Phosphorus: Pregnancy increases requirements; may need to liberalize phosphate restriction; do not need to bind phosphate as aggressively.
  • Calcium: Use lower dialysate calcium concentration (2.5 mEq/L) to avoid hypercalcemia; supplement orally as needed.
  • Erythropoietin / iron: Increased requirements; do not under-dose. Maintain Hb 10–11 g/dL.
  • UF: Conservative; avoid hypotension (uterine hypoperfusion). Aim for euvolemia, not aggressive dry weight reduction.
  • Folate, vitamins: Liberal supplementation; pregnancy + dialysis losses both increase need.
  • Continuous external fetal monitoring during sessions past 24 weeks; coordinate with OB / MFM.
  • Coordinate delivery planning — Postpartum HD usually continues unchanged.
🔑
Outcomes DataToronto Pregnancy and Kidney Disease (PARTY) registry and other cohort data show fetal survival improves from ~50% (conventional 3×/week HD) to > 85% with intensified HD ≥ 36 hr/week. The Canadian and US guidelines now recommend intensification when feasible.
04
Peritoneal Dialysis in Pregnancy

PD in pregnancy is feasible but increasingly complicated as the uterus enlarges. Many patients are transitioned to HD by mid-second trimester.

  • Reduce fill volumes — As uterus enlarges (typically beyond 20 weeks), tidal or reduced-fill prescriptions to accommodate intra-abdominal space.
  • Increase exchanges / cycler frequency — To compensate for reduced per-fill clearance.
  • Monitor for catheter dysfunction — Uterine growth may impair drainage; reposition / catheter manipulation may be needed.
  • Plan to transition to HD if PD becomes inadequate — typically late second / early third trimester.
  • Peritonitis prevention — Standard aseptic technique; increased peritonitis risk reported in pregnancy.
  • Delivery planning — Vaginal delivery is preferred; cesarean if obstetric indication. Catheter may be left in place during cesarean if planning to continue PD postpartum.
05
CRRT in Pregnancy / Postpartum AKI

Pregnancy-associated AKI may occur in the setting of preeclampsia / HELLP, AFLP, severe sepsis (puerperal), TMA, or obstetric hemorrhage. CRRT is often required.

  • Modality: CVVH or CVVHDF typically; modality choice per nephrology / ICU.
  • Anticoagulation: Citrate is generally safe in pregnancy; monitor maternal ionized calcium closely. Heparin acceptable; avoid LMWH at term due to neuraxial anesthesia concerns.
  • Hemodynamic management: Higher BP targets to maintain uteroplacental perfusion. Vasopressor choice: norepinephrine first-line; avoid vasopressin if undelivered (uterine effect).
  • Continuous fetal monitoring during CRRT if undelivered and at viable gestation.
  • Coordinate with MFM and ICU obstetrics — Decision about delivery timing is critical (sometimes the cure for severe preeclampsia / HELLP / AFLP is delivery).
  • Postpartum AKI — Often recovers within weeks; some HELLP / preeclampsia AKI may be permanent. Monitor renal recovery and plan modality transition.
06
LDL Apheresis in Pregnancy

LDL apheresis is well-established for homozygous familial hypercholesterolemia (HoFH) and is generally continued throughout pregnancy. Statins are contraindicated in pregnancy, making LDL apheresis the primary therapy.

  • Continue therapy schedule — Weekly or biweekly per non-pregnant regimen.
  • Replacement fluid: Standard saline / albumin per protocol.
  • Volume management: Conservative; pregnancy expanded plasma volume tolerates standard procedure well.
  • Cardiac surveillance: Echocardiogram for aortic root, valvular involvement (HoFH carries aortic stenosis risk); coordinated with cardiology.
  • Coordinate delivery with cardiology and MFM; vaginal delivery acceptable unless cardiac contraindication.
  • Postpartum: Continue LDL apheresis; resume statin only after lactation if not breastfeeding (statins are contraindicated in lactation per most guidance).
07
ECP in Pregnancy — Generally Contraindicated
⚠
Methoxsalen (8-MOP) Is TeratogenicMethoxsalen, the photoactivator used in ECP (Therakos CELLEX), is classified as pregnancy category C with evidence of teratogenicity and is generally contraindicated in pregnancy. Alternative therapies should be sought.
  • Avoid in pregnancy if at all possible — Alternative immunomodulators per indication (e.g., for GVHD: high-dose steroids, ECP alternatives like ruxolitinib pending pregnancy data).
  • If absolutely required: MFM and dermatology / oncology / transplant team consultation; risk/benefit discussion; documented informed consent.
  • Lactation: Methoxsalen excretion into breast milk is not well-defined; avoid breastfeeding during ECP courses or pump-and-discard per institutional pharmacy guidance.
  • Occupational consideration: Pregnant staff should not handle methoxsalen photoactivator; reassign per institutional employee health policy.

🔑
Left Lateral TiltFrom 20 weeks onward, position the patient with a 15–30° left lateral tilt during procedures. Supine position can cause IVC compression by the gravid uterus, dropping preload by 30–40% and risking maternal hypotension and uterine hypoperfusion.
