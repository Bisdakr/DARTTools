HD Standards of Practice
A USA-aligned standard for performing in-center hemodialysis using Fresenius Medical Care platforms (2008T, 2008K, 2008K@home). Aligned with KDOQI 2015 Hemodialysis Adequacy Guideline, KDIGO 2020 Vascular Access Guideline, CMS Conditions for Coverage for ESRD facilities, AAMI/ISO water quality standards, and current device labeling.

Standard of Practice — Overview
Hemodialysis (HD) provides intermittent extracorporeal solute and fluid removal for patients with end-stage kidney disease and selected acute kidney injury. This SOP describes in-center hemodialysis using Fresenius 2008T or 2008K series platforms with Optiflux or Revaclear dialyzers and bicarbonate dialysate. The dominant intra-session safety threshold is the KDOQI ultrafiltration rate ceiling of < 13 mL/kg/hr, above which mortality and end-organ stunning increase sharply. Vascular access hierarchy: AV fistula > AV graft > tunneled central catheter. CLABSI prevention is the dominant long-term safety priority for catheter-dependent patients.

01
Purpose & Scope

Purpose: To define the standardized procedure for in-center maintenance hemodialysis (and applicable inpatient acute hemodialysis) using Fresenius Medical Care delivery systems.

Scope: Applies to adult and pediatric ESRD patients on maintenance HD, and inpatient AKI patients receiving intermittent HD. All licensed dialysis nurses, dialysis technicians (per state regulations), nephrologists, water treatment staff, and biomedical staff.

Regulatory framework: CMS Conditions for Coverage for ESRD Facilities (42 CFR Part 494); state-specific nursing and technician practice acts; AAMI/ISO 23500 water quality standards; OSHA bloodborne pathogen requirements; CDC dialysis-event surveillance protocols.

02
Roles & Responsibilities

Nephrologist

Reviews monthly orders (prescription, K bath, dialysate composition, dialyzer, heparin, ESA, target weight); rounds at least monthly; manages adequacy.

Dialysis Charge Nurse / Primary RN

Verifies pre-procedure assessment; supervises technicians per scope; cannulates access (or supervises trained technician); manages alarms and adverse events; documents.

Patient Care Technician (PCT) / Dialysis Technician

Per state scope: performs machine setup and prime, cannulates access (if certified), monitors vital signs, performs treatment per nursing supervision; does not adjust prescription parameters.

Water Treatment Technician

Verifies AAMI-grade water; daily chlorine/chloramine test before treatment; weekly endotoxin and culture per protocol.

Renal Dietitian

Monitors nutritional status; phosphorus / potassium / fluid education; coordinates with prescribing physician.

Social Worker

Vocational, transplant readiness, psychological support, financial counseling.

Biomedical / Equipment Technician

Maintains delivery system per manufacturer; runs calibration; tracks dialyzer prime / reuse protocols.

03
Pre-Procedure Verification & Assessment
  1. Two-identifier patient ID.
  2. Order verification. Read back: dialyzer brand and surface area, tubing set, K bath, Ca bath, Na, HCO3, temperature, BFR, dialysate flow, treatment time, UF goal, heparin order.
  3. Pre-dialysis assessment. Vital signs, pre-weight, target weight, interdialytic weight gain, fluid status, access inspection, symptom review (chest pain, dyspnea, recent illness, missed treatments).
  4. Calculate UFR. UFR = UF goal / time / weight. If UFR > 13 mL/kg/hr, do not proceed at the prescribed time — extend treatment or reduce UF goal in consultation with nephrologist.
  5. Anti-hypertensive review. Per institutional protocol, common practice is to hold AM anti-hypertensives on dialysis days for hypotension-prone patients. Document instruction.
  6. Pre-dialysis labs (per monthly schedule). Confirm recent BUN, creatinine, K, Ca, phosphorus, Mg, albumin, Hgb, ferritin, transferrin saturation, parathyroid hormone.
  7. Water quality check. Daily total chlorine/chloramine test must be acceptable before treatment initiation.
  8. Machine pre-treatment self-test passed. Conductivity, temperature, pressure, dialysate flow self-tests.

Access hierarchy per KDIGO 2020: AV fistula preferred > AV graft > tunneled central venous catheter. Each session requires inspection and skilled cannulation/connection.

CLABSI Prevention BundleFor all tunneled catheter patients: (1) hand hygiene; (2) chlorhexidine prep with appropriate dry time; (3) sterile field at every access; (4) dedicated apheresis nurse at the access step; (5) prompt transition to fistula whenever possible. CDC NHSN CLABSI rate target < 1 per 1,000 line-days; institutional benchmarks may be tighter.
🔑
Catheter Removal Advocacy — Every Shift, Every PatientEvery dialysis catheter is a potential CLABSI. The dialysis RN should actively advocate for catheter removal at every encounter when the catheter is no longer needed — for example, when the patient is approaching discharge, when an AV fistula has matured, when the patient is transitioning to peritoneal dialysis or recovering renal function. Communication: SBAR to the nephrologist or primary team, "This catheter has been in for X days. Plan for removal — what is needed to remove?" Final decision per physician discretion, but the advocacy is a nursing responsibility.
  • Daily catheter need assessment. Is the catheter still needed today? If not, document the conversation with the primary team about removal timing.
  • Maturation surveillance. For patients with maturing AVF, track maturation progress; remove the catheter as soon as the fistula is reliably cannulating.
  • Modality change. Patients transitioning from HD to PD or to transplant should have the catheter removed once dialysis support is no longer required.
  • Recovery from AKI. Document declining BUN/creatinine without HD support; coordinate removal with the primary team.
  • End-of-life / comfort care. Catheter removal may be part of comfort care; coordinate with palliative care service.
05
Machine Setup & Prime
  1. Power on; run self-test. Verify conductivity, temperature, pressure within range.
  2. Install dialyzer. Confirm brand, size match to patient prescription (e.g., Optiflux F180NR for standard adult). Install per orientation (arrows match blood flow direction).
  3. Install tubing set. Confirm size match (Husky / Pediatric / Adult). Verify all tubing connections per orientation diagram.
  4. Hang priming solution. Standard 1 L bag NS 0.9%. For pediatric or large-circuit prime decision per institutional ECV/TBV policy, use albumin or PRBC prime.
  5. Set dialysate parameters. K bath (1, 2, 3, or 4 mEq/L per order; standard 2 mEq/L); Ca bath (2.25–3.0 mEq/L); Na (137–140 mEq/L); HCO3 (32–35 mEq/L); temperature (35.5–36.5°C).
  6. Set machine flow parameters. Dialysate flow 600–800 mL/min standard; BFR per prescription (target 350–450 adult).
  7. Program UF goal and treatment time. Verify UFR < 13 mL/kg/hr calculation again at programming.
  8. Run automated prime. Saline prime fills the circuit; verify no air, no leaks, no pressure issues.
06
Initiation
  1. Final pre-treatment verifications — patient identifications, access patency, parameter readback.
  2. Cannulate access. Sterile technique. Confirm needle position with blood return on both arterial and venous needles.
  3. Connect. Attach arterial line (red) to arterial needle; venous line (blue) to venous needle.
  4. Heparin bolus (if heparinized). Per order — typical 1,000–3,000 U IV bolus through arterial line at initiation; continuous infusion as ordered.
  5. Initiate blood flow slowly. 100–150 mL/min for first 5 minutes; verify hemodynamic stability and absence of access alarms.
  6. Advance to target BFR. 300–450 mL/min per prescription.
  7. Begin UF and dialysate per program.
  8. Initial pressure documentation. Arterial pressure, venous pressure, transmembrane pressure recorded.

UFR Safety — The Critical Threshold

UFR is calculated as: UFR (mL/kg/hr) = UF goal (mL) / time (hr) / weight (kg).

UFRRisk bandAction
< 10 mL/kg/hrSafeStandard monitoring
10–13 mL/kg/hrKDIGO cautionCloser BP monitoring; reassess dry weight
> 13 mL/kg/hrKDOQI ceiling exceededDo not exceed — extend treatment time, OR reduce UF goal, OR add session. Discuss with nephrologist before exceeding.
🔑
Interdialytic CounselingUFR safety begins between sessions. Patient education on sodium and fluid restriction (target interdialytic weight gain < 2.5 kg) reduces UFR pressure at the next session. Renal dietitian involvement is part of UFR safety.
09
Termination & Rinseback
  1. Verify time and UF goal met.
  2. Slow blood flow rate.
  3. Reinfusion via saline rinseback — clamp arterial line, attach NS to inlet, slowly rinse circuit blood back to patient via venous line. Verify no air, watch for hemodynamic response.
  4. Final vital signs and post-weight.
  5. Discontinue access. Remove needles or disconnect catheter per access type protocol. Apply pressure to needle sites for ≥ 10 minutes; tunneled catheter: heparin lock per protocol.
  6. Final assessment. Post-dialysis weight, BP, symptom check, access inspection.
10
Post-Treatment
  • Patient education — Sit up slowly; report any persistent hypotension, dizziness, ongoing bleeding from access, chest pain, dyspnea; verify next session.
  • Documentation completion — All session parameters, vital signs, weight change, adverse events.
  • Pharmacy / med administration — Post-dialysis injections (ESA, iron, anti-hypertensives) per order.
  • Schedule next session.
  • Monthly labs review at next session — Adequacy, ferritin, PTH per CMS / KDOQI cadence.
  • Long-term tracking — Kt/V, URR, weight stability, access health, hospitalization events.

Water Treatment & Water Quality
RO, pH, chlorine/chloramine, TDS, AAMI/ISO compliance

Hemodialysis water is a drug delivery vehicle. Each patient is exposed to approximately 120 liters of dialysate per treatment, in direct osmotic contact with their blood across the dialyzer membrane. Contaminants — chlorine, chloramine, endotoxin, bacteria, metals — that would be insignificant in drinking water cause hemolysis, methemoglobinemia, pyrogenic reactions, and chronic toxicity in dialysis. AAMI/ISO 23500 sets the global standard; CMS Conditions for Coverage adopt and enforce these requirements in the United States.

Water Treatment Train

City water (or well water) enters the water room and passes through, in sequence:

  1. Backflow preventer and pre-filter (sediment). Removes particulates; required by plumbing code.
  2. Carbon tanks (dual-tank in series). Removes chlorine and chloramine by adsorption. The single most critical step — chloramine breakthrough causes hemolysis. Two tanks in series provide redundancy: the worker tank does the work; the polisher tank catches breakthrough.
  3. Water softener. Removes calcium and magnesium (hard water minerals) by ion exchange to protect downstream RO membranes.
  4. Reverse Osmosis (RO). Removes 95–99% of dissolved solids, bacteria, endotoxin, and most chemical contaminants. The RO product water is the primary dialysate source.
  5. Ultraviolet (UV) light disinfection. Bacterial kill at the RO outlet.
  6. Ultrafilters (endotoxin retention) at point of use. Final endotoxin polishing immediately before the dialysis machine.
  7. Distribution loop. Stainless steel or PEX plumbing, looped (no dead legs), continuous recirculation to prevent biofilm formation.

  • Monthly summary reviewed by water treatment technician and medical director.
  • Calibration records for testing reagents (chlorine test kits, pH meter, conductivity meter).
  • Annual chemistry analysis reviewed and filed.
  • Carbon tank change-out log.
  • RO membrane integrity log.

Machine Disinfection & Maintenance
Heat, bleach, acid — daily, weekly, monthly

Hemodialysis delivery systems harbor bacterial biofilm in their dialysate fluid pathway if not regularly disinfected. The internal pathway contains warm bicarbonate solution — an ideal growth medium. Each modern machine (Fresenius 2008T/2008K) has built-in disinfection cycles for heat, chemical (bleach), and acid (descale) cleaning.

Heat disinfectionHot water (≥ 80°C / 176°F)Typically daily between patients or end-of-dayBacterial kill in fluid pathway; preferred method for routine disinfection. No chemical residue.
Chemical (bleach)Sodium hypochlorite 0.5–1% (5,000–10,000 ppm)Weekly or per institutional scheduleBroad-spectrum disinfection; biofilm penetration; supplements heat.
Acid descaleCitric acid (warm) or peracetic acid blendMonthly or per manufacturer recommendationRemoves calcium / mineral deposits (scale) that protect biofilm and reduce flow.
External surfaceEPA-registered disinfectant wipes (e.g., bleach wipes for blood contact, quat for other surfaces)Between each patient — wipe machine exterior, chair, IV pole, scaleStandard precautions; infection control.
Bicarb tank cleaningPer manufacturer protocolPer shift / dailyBicarbonate tanks accumulate scale and biofilm; daily empty/clean essential.

Verification

  • Disinfection cycle completion verified before next patient (machine displays "ready").
  • Residual chemical test after bleach or acid disinfection — verify residual is below detectable limit before next treatment.
  • Logged in machine disinfection record — date, time, type, technician initials.
  • Periodic culture verification per institutional protocol — sample dialysate from the machine outlet; culture results must meet AAMI standards for dialysate (< 200 CFU/mL).
  • Filter changes — bicarbonate and acid concentrate filters per manufacturer schedule; documented.
  • Annual preventive maintenance by biomedical service — calibration, pressure transducer check, conductivity meter check, alarm verification.
⚠
Residual BleachAfter bleach disinfection, residual hypochlorite must be flushed completely. A residual chlorine test on the next-treatment dialysate is mandatory before initiating treatment. Even small residual bleach causes hemolysis.

Infectious Patient Protocols
Hepatitis B, HCV, HIV, MDRO; serologic surveillance

Dialysis units have a long history of bloodborne pathogen transmission events. CDC and CMS define specific surveillance and isolation protocols. Hepatitis B is the highest-priority bloodborne pathogen given its environmental survival and historical outbreak record.

Hepatitis B (HBV)

  • HBsAg status verified at admission and monthly for all non-immune patients (anti-HBs < 10 mIU/mL).
  • HBV vaccination for all susceptible patients — accelerated 4-dose schedule (0, 1, 2, 6 months) at double the standard adult dose; verify post-vaccination anti-HBs.
  • Anti-HBs annual surveillance for vaccinated patients — booster if titer falls below 10 mIU/mL.
  • HBsAg-positive patients require dedicated machine, dedicated staff, dedicated supplies, and a physically separated room or section per CDC. The machine is not used for any HBsAg-negative patient.
  • Staff handling HBsAg+ patients do not cross-cover other patients during the same shift.
  • All staff vaccinated against HBV with documented anti-HBs titer; post-exposure protocol available 24/7.
  • Strict standard precautions at all times for all patients — gloves, eye protection, gown if splash anticipated.

Hepatitis C (HCV)

  • HCV antibody at admission and every 6 months; HCV RNA if antibody-positive.
  • No mandatory dedicated machines for HCV+ patients per CDC (unlike HBV) — standard precautions suffice. However, separation is institutionally optional.
  • Coordinate with hepatology for direct-acting antiviral treatment; most HCV+ dialysis patients now achieve sustained virologic response.

HIV

  • HIV testing per patient consent and institutional schedule.
  • No dedicated machine required per CDC — standard precautions suffice; coordinate ARV therapy with infectious disease.

MDRO (MRSA, VRE, CRE, etc.)

  • Surveillance cultures per institutional protocol — often nasal MRSA screening at admission.
  • Contact precautions for known carriers; cohort or isolation per CDC guidance.
  • Enhanced environmental cleaning between patients in dedicated areas.
⚠
Never Share Supplies Between PatientsMulti-dose vials are not shared between patients in dialysis. Heparin vials, saline flush vials, and any injectable supplies must be dedicated to a single patient. Disposable supplies (alcohol wipes, gauze, tape) must not be carried between patient stations. Mobile carts must be cleaned between patient stations.

Catheter-Related Bloodstream Infection (CLABSI) Management
Recognition, antibiotic salvage, line holiday, removal criteria

The CDC and IDSA frameworks define dialysis catheter-related bloodstream infection management. Catheter salvage with antibiotic lock therapy is attempted when feasible; catheter removal is required for specific organisms and clinical scenarios.

Recognition

  • Symptoms during or shortly after treatment: fever, chills, rigors, hypotension, leukocytosis. May present subtly in chronic patients — any unexplained instability in a catheter patient should trigger evaluation.
  • Exit-site infection (ESI): erythema, drainage, tenderness at exit; may or may not have systemic symptoms.
  • Tunnel infection: erythema and tenderness extending along the tunnel tract; more advanced infection.
  • Bacteremia without obvious exit: blood cultures positive without external site findings; differential time-to-positivity (catheter vs peripheral) helps confirm catheter as source.

Initial Response Protocol

  1. Blood cultures, two sets. One peripheral, one from each catheter lumen (paired). Note differential time-to-positivity — catheter-positive ≥ 2 hours earlier than peripheral supports catheter as source.
  2. Empiric IV antibiotics initiated promptly per institutional protocol — typical regimen: vancomycin (load 25 mg/kg, then per pharmacy) + Gram-negative coverage (cefepime, piperacillin-tazobactam, or carbapenem per institutional antibiogram). Often given at the start of dialysis to take advantage of long inter-dialytic interval; vancomycin dosed post-HD per pharmacy.
  3. Notify ID consultation per institutional CLABSI protocol; vascular access team notified.
  4. Continue dialysis through the line while attempting salvage — unless catheter must be removed (see below).
  5. Document NHSN CLABSI event per CDC surveillance protocol.

Catheter Salvage with Antibiotic Lock Therapy (ALT)

For patients without absolute removal indications, attempt salvage:

  • Antibiotic lock therapy (ALT): high-concentration antibiotic solution (vancomycin, gentamicin, ceftazidime, or organism-specific agent) instilled into each catheter lumen at the end of each treatment, dwells in the lumen between treatments, aspirated and discarded at next treatment start.
  • Concurrent systemic antibiotics for 10–14 days minimum (longer for resistant organisms).
  • Repeat blood cultures 48–72 hours after antibiotic initiation; if persistently positive, escalate to catheter removal.
  • Surveillance: daily clinical assessment, weekly cultures during therapy, end-of-therapy cultures, and post-therapy surveillance.

Line Holiday — When to Wait Before Replacing

After removal of an infected catheter, a "line holiday" (period of no central catheter) is often appropriate to allow source clearance before placing a new catheter. Approaches:

  • Catheter removed; treat 48–72 hours via temporary access (peripheral / arterial / temporary non-tunneled line). Allows blood cultures to clear and biofilm site to heal.
  • Blood cultures repeated 48–72 hours after removal; must be negative before new tunneled catheter placement.
  • New catheter placed at a different anatomic site when possible (e.g., contralateral IJ).
  • Avoid catheter exchange over a guidewire in infected line — exchanging over the wire in the same site carries higher recurrence risk than full removal and new site placement.
  • Coordinate fistula or graft creation if not already in progress.
🔑
The Hierarchy of Catheter DecisionsWhen in doubt: remove the catheter. The longest catheter retention bias in dialysis carries CLABSI, metastatic infection, and mortality consequences. Salvage attempts are appropriate in select patients (no removal indications, organism amenable to ALT, no metastatic infection signs), but the threshold for removal should be lower than higher. Re-confirm with the medical director and the patient.

Exit-Site and Tunnel Infection (Without Bacteremia)

  • Exit-site infection (ESI): culture exit site, topical mupirocin or chlorhexidine, oral antibiotics per organism, increased frequency of dressing change, daily inspection.
  • Tunnel infection: IV antibiotics, infectious disease consultation; catheter removal often required if no rapid resolution.
  • Distinguish ESI from CLABSI: ESI without bacteremia generally does not require removal if responding to therapy; tunnel infection has lower salvage success and usually requires removal.

Hospital / Inpatient Hemodialysis Considerations
Acute HD, ICU support, transfer logistics, and isolation rooms

Inpatient hemodialysis differs from outpatient maintenance dialysis in several important ways. Acute and ICU patients are sicker, often with multi-organ dysfunction; medication adjustments are dynamic; the hemodynamic margin is narrower; isolation requirements and equipment transport add complexity.

Acute (Inpatient New-Start) Hemodialysis

  • Indications follow the classic AEIOU framework — Acidosis (pH < 7.10), Electrolytes (K > 6.5 with ECG changes), Intoxication, Overload (refractory pulmonary edema), Uremia (encephalopathy, pericarditis).
  • First-treatment caution — In severely uremic patients (BUN > 150), risk of disequilibrium syndrome; prescribe shorter time (1.5–2 hours), low BFR (150–200 mL/min), smaller dialyzer, low UF. Increase gradually over the first 2–3 treatments.
  • Hemodynamic support — Coordinate with ICU team for vasopressor titration during HD; midodrine PO or IV phenylephrine drip may be needed.
  • Heparin alternatives — Bleeding patients: no heparin (saline flushes) or regional citrate per institutional protocol; HIT patients: argatroban.
  • Drug dosing — Stat pharmacy consult; many ICU drugs require renal-replacement-specific dosing.

ICU Bedside HD vs Transport to Dialysis Unit

  • Bedside HD preferred for hemodynamically unstable patients (vasopressor-dependent, on mechanical ventilation, post-operative). Requires portable RO system or appropriate plumbing; coordinate with ICU charge nurse and water treatment service.
  • Transport to dialysis unit appropriate for stable inpatients on telemetry or step-down; verify oxygen needs, IV access, transport monitoring per institutional policy.
  • Telemetry / continuous monitoring in transport for at-risk patients.
  • Code-status verification before transport.

Isolation Room HD

  • Contact precautions (C. diff, MDRO carriers, draining wounds) — dedicated machine for the duration of admission; staff gown/glove; enhanced cleaning between treatments.
  • Droplet precautions (influenza, COVID-19, RSV) — appropriate PPE; consider portable HEPA if available.
  • Airborne precautions (TB, measles) — negative-pressure room; N95 respirator; coordinate with infection prevention.
  • Multi-Drug-Resistant Organism (MDRO) screening — per institutional admission protocol; results determine room assignment.

Communication & Care Coordination

  • Nephrology consultation coordinates the dialysis prescription with the primary admitting service.
  • Pharmacist on rounds for medication review.
  • Vascular access team for AVF planning, IR catheter placement, and removal coordination.
  • Discharge planning — Transition to outpatient HD unit; AVF maturation timeline; PD or transplant consideration.
  • End-of-life discussions — Withdrawal of dialysis is a recognized palliative decision; coordinate with palliative care, family, and primary team.
