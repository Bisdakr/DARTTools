Standard of Practice — Overview
Red Blood Cell Exchange (RBCx) replaces a defined fraction of the patient's circulating sickle/abnormal red cells with phenotypically matched donor red cells. The dominant indication is sickle cell disease — for acute complications (acute chest syndrome, stroke, multi-organ failure) and chronic primary or secondary stroke prevention. This SOP describes the standardized procedure using the Spectra Optia system. RBCx differs from TPE in that it is a transfusion procedure: every step carries blood-bank-grade verification, ABO/Rh checking, and alloimmunization mitigation. Hyperviscosity safety dictates a post-exchange Hct ceiling of approximately 30% in SCD.

01
Purpose & Scope

Purpose: To define the standardized procedure for Red Blood Cell Exchange in patients with sickle cell disease, malaria/babesiosis with high parasitemia, or other RBC-related indications, using a continuous-flow centrifugal apheresis system.

Scope: Adult and pediatric patients (≥ 10 kg) meeting an ASFA Category I, II, or selected III indication for RBC exchange. Applies to all licensed apheresis registered nurses, apheresis physicians, blood bank staff, and pharmacy staff involved in RBCx.

Indications recognized: Sickle cell disease acute stroke (Category I, Grade 1A); sickle cell disease chronic transfusion for stroke prevention (Category I, Grade 1A); acute chest syndrome (Category I, Grade 1C); severe malaria with parasitemia > 10% (Category II); severe babesiosis (Category II); selected hemoglobinopathies and per institutional approval for off-category indications.

🔑
RBCx is a Transfusion ProcedureEvery step that involves donor red cells must follow AABB transfusion standards: positive patient identification, ABO/Rh verification, antibody screen and crossmatch as appropriate, two-RN bedside checks for first unit, reaction surveillance, and reporting to the blood bank. The apheresis component runs concurrently with — and is governed by — transfusion service policies.
02
Roles & Responsibilities

Attending Apheresis Physician

Reviews indication and ASFA category; specifies FCR target, target Hct, replacement product type and phenotype; reviews pre-procedure labs including baseline Hct and HbS%; available for emergent consultation.

Transfusion Service / Blood Bank

Performs extended phenotype matching (minimum C, E, K; preferred full Rh/Kell/Duffy/Kidd/MNS for chronic SCD); prepares and crossmatches PRBC; supplies the specified number of units with appropriate phenotype; investigates reactions; maintains comprehensive antibody history.

Apheresis Primary RN

Verifies all pre-procedure requirements; performs 2-RN check on each unit; operates machine; monitors patient and transfusion reaction signs; documents.

Second RN (Blood Verification)

Independent ID and unit verification at bedside before each unit hangs; available for first 15 min of each new unit (per institutional transfusion policy).

Apheresis Medical Director / Hematology Service

Reviews chronic exchange schedule; coordinates with hematology / SCD program; tracks long-term alloimmunization and iron status.

03
Pre-Procedure Verification
  1. Patient identification — two identifiers per policy. Match against order set, blood bank documentation, electronic record.
  2. Informed consent verification. Apheresis consent + transfusion consent (separate documents). Re-confirm patient understanding for elective procedure.
  3. Order verification. Read back: indication, FCR target (commonly 0.30 = 70% replacement), target Hct (commonly 30%), expected unit count, phenotype requirements, anticoagulation strategy.
  4. Pre-procedure labs. CBC with reticulocyte count, comprehensive metabolic panel, ionized calcium, hemoglobin electrophoresis (current HbS%), type and screen, antibody screen. Verify within institutional age limits (typically 24–72 hr).
  5. Antibody history review. Confirm any clinically significant antibodies are on the current type and screen; communicate with blood bank if anything new. For chronic patients, review the running alloantibody record.
  6. Vascular access verification. Most RBCx requires central or AV-fistula access (typical inlet 60–80 mL/min). Verify patency, position, infection-free site.
  7. Baseline vital signs and weight. Pre-procedure temperature is the baseline for transfusion reaction surveillance; record carefully.
  8. Calcium replacement plan. Citrate symptoms in RBCx are typically milder than TPE (volume-matched return), but calcium gluconate IV or drip per institutional protocol should be available.
04
Blood Product Verification
The critical pre-transfusion checks
  1. Receipt of units. Verify number of units against order. Inspect each unit for clots, hemolysis, expiration, integrity of seal.
  2. Two-RN bedside check. Independently verify, for each unit: (a) patient name + DOB + MRN; (b) unit number; (c) ABO + Rh of unit matches plan; (d) crossmatch tag matches patient; (e) extended phenotype documented matches order; (f) expiration date.
  3. Documentation of verification. Both RNs sign on the unit tag and in the procedure record. The transfusion service procedure record is the primary document.
  4. Stage units sequentially. Hang units in the order they will be infused. For chronic exchanges, prefer fresher units (< 14 days) for citrate metabolism, K⁺ levels, and 2,3-DPG.
  5. Phenotype reverification. For each unit, especially in newly identified or refreshed antibodies, confirm phenotype on the unit label against the patient's required match.
  6. Patient ID band check at machine start. Final ID band check before initiating the procedure; ensure consistency with all unit tags.
⚠
ABO Mismatch — The Most Important Error to PreventAcute hemolytic transfusion reactions from ABO incompatibility are the most catastrophic preventable error in any transfusion procedure. Never skip the two-RN bedside check. If anything in the verification doesn't match, stop, do not proceed, return all products to blood bank for re-verification.
05
Machine Setup, Prime & Functional Test
  1. Power on, run self-test. Confirm RBCX procedure selected. Verify procedure parameters menu: 15:1 AC ratio default, target Hct, FCR.
  2. Install the TPE/RBCX disposable set. Same kit as TPE but with the RBCX program. Connect saline, ACD-A, replacement PRBC, and (typically) a return-line warmer set to 50 mL standard volume.
  3. Hang solutions. ACD-A 1000 mL (yellow spike); 4 L NS (white spike); PRBC units staged in line (orange spike — verify each unit ID at the spike). For SCD chronic, expect 5–8 units typically.
  4. Program patient parameters. Sex, height, weight, baseline Hct, HbS%; verify TBV calculation; enter FCR target, target Hct.
  5. Verify rinseback setting. Off — RBCx prime stays in line and is not rinsed back per institutional standard. The patient receives the volume of replacement PRBC; saline prime fluid is discarded.
  6. Prime & functional test. Initiate automated prime; observe to completion; address any failures.
  7. Verify return line warmer. Set to standard 50 mL warming volume per institutional protocol. Verify warmer reaches operating temperature before initiation.
06
Initiation
  1. Pre-connection: final identifications. Last patient ID check, blood unit verification, baseline vital signs documented.
  2. Sterile access connection. Don sterile gloves and mask; prep access; connect inlet (red) and return (blue) lines.
  3. Slow-start. Inlet 25 mL/min, return 10 mL/min for first 5 minutes. Observe for transfusion reaction signs: fever, chills, flank pain, hypotension, hemoglobinuria, rash, dyspnea.
  4. 15-minute window observation. The first 15 minutes of any transfusion is the highest-risk window for an acute hemolytic reaction. Vital signs every 5 minutes; stay at the bedside.
  5. Advance to full flow. If no reaction at 15 minutes, advance inlet to 50–80 mL/min steady-state typical adult. Pediatric: weight-based, typically 1–2 mL/kg/min initial.
  6. Continuous transfusion surveillance. Verify each unit hang with two-RN check; document each unit start time; maintain transfusion reaction watch.

⚠
Hyperhemolysis Awareness (SCD)In SCD, a unique and life-threatening reaction can occur in which both donor and recipient cells are destroyed, dropping Hgb below pre-procedure baseline. Recognition: post-procedure Hgb falls below baseline by ≥ 25%, marked LDH rise, jaundice. Response: STOP further transfusion unless life-threatening; IVIG 1–2 g/kg + IV methylprednisolone; hematology consult; flag the chart prominently.
08
Termination
  1. Verify FCR and target Hct achieved. Machine displays the achieved exchange. Compare against prescribed values; document final FCR, total PRBC infused, total NS infused, total ACD-A.
  2. No rinseback for RBCx. Standard practice. Discard prime fluid in circuit per institutional waste protocol.
  3. Disconnect. Clamp inlet and return; flush each lumen per central catheter protocol (saline + heparin lock or appropriate). Apply dressing.
  4. Post-procedure point-of-care Hct. Verify post-procedure Hct ≤ 30% target (especially in SCD). If higher than target, notify physician — possible hyperviscosity concern.
  5. Post-procedure vital signs documented.
09
Post-Procedure
  • Post-procedure observation — Minimum 30 minutes bedside; longer if first procedure or any reaction signs.
  • Post-procedure labs — CBC with retic, ionized calcium, magnesium, post-procedure HbS% (confirms FCR was met). Type and screen for next session.
  • Antibody surveillance — If chronic patient, ensure antibody panel updated for next session. Notify blood bank of any changes.
  • Iron / ferritin trending — For chronic exchanges, monitor ferritin every 3–6 months to confirm exchange is preventing iron overload.
  • Patient education — Sit/stand cautiously; report any fever, chills, jaundice, dark urine, persistent weakness, dyspnea, flank/chest pain.
  • Schedule next session — For acute SCD: per disease course. For chronic prophylaxis: typically every 4–8 weeks based on HbS% trend and clinical response.

Documentation & Quality Indicators
  • Identifiers — Patient, date, accession, RN signature.
  • Order detail — Indication, FCR target, target Hct, phenotype, units number.
  • Each unit — Unit number, ABO/Rh, phenotype, expiration, two-RN signatures, start/stop time.
  • Procedure — Inlet flow, AIR trend, vital signs at frequency, citrate screens, fluid balance hourly.
  • End — Final FCR, total PRBC, total ACD, total NS, post-procedure Hct, post-procedure HbS%, post-procedure vitals.
  • Reaction documentation — Time, type, intervention, response, blood bank notification.
  • Communication / handoff — SBAR, follow-up labs, next-session timing.
