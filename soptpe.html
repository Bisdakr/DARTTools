Standard of Practice — Overview
Therapeutic Plasma Exchange (TPE) is performed to remove a defined volume of patient plasma — containing pathogenic autoantibodies, alloantibodies, immune complexes, paraproteins, or protein-bound toxins — and replace it with an appropriate fluid (5% albumin or fresh frozen plasma). This SOP describes the standardized workflow using the Spectra Optia apheresis system for TPE indications per ASFA Category I/II/III recommendations. Every step in this document is consistent with USA federal labeling, AABB accreditation standards, and current evidence-based apheresis nursing practice.

01
Purpose & Scope
What this SOP covers and who it applies to

Purpose: To define the standardized procedure for performing Therapeutic Plasma Exchange (TPE) in adult and pediatric patients (≥ 10 kg) using a continuous-flow centrifugal apheresis system. This SOP ensures patient safety, regulatory compliance, and reproducible technique across all apheresis nursing staff.

Scope: Applies to all licensed apheresis registered nurses, apheresis physicians, blood bank staff, and pharmacy staff involved in the prescription, preparation, performance, or post-procedure management of TPE. Does not cover indications outside ASFA-recognized categories or modifications requiring deviation from manufacturer instructions for use; such cases require attending physician approval and supervisory nursing review.

Patient population: Patients meeting the following criteria — (a) ASFA Category I, II, or selected Category III indication; (b) physician order specifying plasma volume, replacement fluid, schedule; (c) adequate vascular access; (d) hemodynamically stable or stabilized for procedure; (e) no absolute contraindication present.

02
Roles & Responsibilities
Who does what — accountability framework

Attending Apheresis Physician

Reviews indication against ASFA category; specifies the prescription (plasma volumes to exchange, replacement fluid, schedule, anticoagulation strategy); confirms informed consent; reviews pre-procedure labs; is available for emergent consultation throughout procedure.

Apheresis Registered Nurse (Primary RN)

Performs patient verification and consent confirmation; verifies machine setup, prime, and test; initiates and operates the procedure; performs hourly fluid balance verification; monitors patient hemodynamics every 15 minutes; documents all parameters, adverse events, and interventions; communicates with physician and blood bank.

Apheresis Registered Nurse (Backup / Float RN)

Available during initiation, rinseback, and any rate change; assists with patient repositioning and vital signs; covers primary RN for restroom breaks or charting; ensures continuous bedside presence.

Blood Bank / Transfusion Service

Prepares and supplies the prescribed replacement fluid (5% Albumin, FFP, or combination); performs crossmatch when blood prime ordered; investigates transfusion reactions; verifies ABO/Rh and labels.

Pharmacy

Supplies anticoagulant (ACD-A), calcium replacement (calcium gluconate IV bolus and/or drip), premedications (diphenhydramine, methylprednisolone) per institutional protocol; reviews drug interactions especially ACE inhibitors.

Apheresis Technician (where applicable)

Assists with machine setup, disposable set installation, fluid spike-and-hang, equipment troubleshooting; does not independently initiate or operate procedure.

Apheresis Medical Director

Maintains institutional protocols; performs quality reviews; investigates adverse events; ensures regulatory compliance (AABB, FACT, CMS conditions of participation).

03
Pre-Procedure Verification & Preparation
Patient, orders, labs, access, premedications

Pre-procedure verification is performed in the following sequence by the primary apheresis RN, with documentation in the institutional procedure record. No procedure proceeds with any item unresolved.

  1. Patient identification. Two patient identifiers per institutional policy (name + DOB, or name + medical record number). Verify against the order set, replacement fluid labels, and electronic record.
  2. Informed consent verification. Confirm signed consent for therapeutic apheresis and for transfusion of blood products if FFP or albumin is being used. Re-confirm patient understands the procedure if conscious and competent. Pediatric: parent/guardian signature plus age-appropriate assent.
  3. Order verification. Read back the physician order: indication, plasma volumes to exchange, replacement fluid type and volume, anticoagulation (typically citrate via ACD-A at 15:1), session schedule. Address any inconsistencies before proceeding.
  4. Pre-procedure laboratory review. Confirm within 24 hours: CBC with platelets, comprehensive metabolic panel (including ionized calcium when available), fibrinogen, PT/PTT. If multiple consecutive procedures, verify fibrinogen > 100 mg/dL and platelets > 50 × 10⁹/L before each subsequent procedure. Type and screen current per blood bank policy.
  5. Medication reconciliation — ACE inhibitor check. Verify with patient and chart that any ACE inhibitor (lisinopril, enalapril, ramipril, captopril, etc.) has been held for ≥ 24 hours. If not held, contact ordering physician before proceeding; consider conversion to ARB for chronic patients.
  6. Vascular access assessment. Inspect the planned access (peripheral, central venous catheter, AV fistula, or tunneled catheter). Verify patency, absence of infection, and adequacy for the prescribed flow rates. For central catheters, confirm proper position by recent radiograph if newly placed.
  7. Vital signs baseline. Obtain pre-procedure temperature, heart rate, respiratory rate, blood pressure, oxygen saturation, weight. Document weight in kilograms — used for TBV calculation and AIR safety limit.
  8. Premedication. Administer institutionally prescribed premedications per indication. Typical regimens: FFP replacement — diphenhydramine 25–50 mg IV plus methylprednisolone 40–125 mg IV approximately 30 minutes prior. Albumin replacement — typically no premedication unless prior reaction.
  9. Calcium replacement plan. Verify standing order for calcium gluconate IV (commonly 1–2 g IV during the procedure, titrated to symptoms or by protocol). Some institutions use a continuous calcium gluconate infusion mixed in normal saline.
  10. Patient positioning & environment. Position supine or semi-recumbent. Pre-warm extremity if peripheral access is planned. Ensure call light within reach. Confirm emergency response equipment, including crash cart and IV anaphylaxis kit, is accessible.
⚠
Hold-Point: Do Not Proceed IfAny of the following is present: ACE inhibitor not held; vascular access compromised or not patent; replacement fluid not yet available; informed consent missing; severe hypocalcemia or symptomatic electrolyte abnormality uncorrected; unstable hemodynamics not yet stabilized; physician order incomplete or unclear. Resolve through physician contact before continuing.
04
Machine Setup, Prime & Functional Test
Disposable set, solutions, programming

Setup is performed by the primary apheresis RN. Verification of each step is documented before proceeding to the next.

  1. Power on and run self-test. Turn on the Spectra Optia. Verify the most recent maintenance is current and all self-test indicators clear. Confirm the procedure type selected matches the order (e.g., TPE/RBCX program with 15:1 AC ratio default).
  2. Install the disposable set. Use the TPE-rated disposable set. Follow the on-screen instructions in sequence: cassette installation, pump segments routing, line organizer, return line warmer (if used), bag spike preparation. Confirm all hardware orientation is correct.
  3. Hang solutions and connect. Anticoagulant: ACD-A 1000 mL × 1–2 (yellow spike). Saline 0.9%: 4–6 L total (typically two 1L bags + larger reserve). Replacement fluid: 5% albumin per prescribed volume (typically 1.0–1.5 PV equivalent). For FFP, organize bags for sequential connection in the order to be infused.
  4. Program the procedure. Enter patient sex, height, weight, hematocrit. Verify the calculated total blood volume and target plasma volume to exchange against the physician order. Confirm AC ratio (15:1 standard for albumin; some institutions reduce to 14:1 or 13:1 for FFP-heavy procedures). Verify rinseback setting (ON for albumin/saline prime; toggle as appropriate per institutional protocol if blood prime).
  5. Run the prime & functional test cycle. Initiate the automated prime. Observe the prime cycle to completion; address any prime test failures (typically pump segment, pressure sensor, or air detector issues) before proceeding.
  6. Verify alarm settings. Confirm pressure limits, air detector, return line warmer (if used), and AIR limit (1.2 mL/min/L TBV is the machine maximum; the device will throttle inlet flow as needed).
  7. Stage replacement fluid. Pre-spike albumin bags or stage FFP bags for sequential exchange. Verify each replacement bag against the patient identifier and the physician order. For FFP: re-verify ABO compatibility, expiration, and thaw timestamp.
  8. Verify calcium drip / standing dose. Confirm calcium gluconate is hung or syringe is prepared. Verify rate or bolus protocol is consistent with the standing order.
●
AC Ratio & AIR ReminderThe 15:1 AC ratio is standard for adult TPE with albumin replacement. The Spectra Optia automatically calculates AC Infusion Rate (AIR) — the citrate dose-rate per liter of patient total blood volume — and limits inlet flow if AIR would exceed 1.2 mL/min/L TBV. Tighter AC ratios (10:1 or 8:1) deliver more citrate per liter blood, reducing maximum allowable inlet flow proportionally. Loosening to 16:1 or 18:1 raises the inlet flow ceiling but increases circuit clotting risk.
05
Procedure Initiation
Connecting the patient and the slow-start protocol
  1. Pre-connection verification. Final patient identification, replacement fluid verification, vital signs documentation, IV calcium readiness. Confirm second RN at bedside for the connection step.
  2. Vascular access preparation. Don sterile gloves and mask. Prep access site per institutional sterile technique. Connect the patient access line(s) — for central catheter, withdraw the heparin lock, confirm blood return on each lumen, flush with saline per protocol.
  3. Connect inlet (red) and return (blue) lines. Connect inlet line to the lumen designated for blood withdrawal. Connect return line to the lumen designated for blood return. Verify orientation against machine labeling and order. Open all clamps.
  4. Initiate slow-start. Begin with inlet flow at 25 mL/min and return flow at 10 mL/min. Observe the patient for first 5 minutes for vasovagal response, hypotension, citrate symptoms. Verify all pressures within normal range and absence of alarms.
  5. Begin replacement. Replacement fluid pump initiates per machine prompt as plasma is removed. Verify the replacement fluid bag label one final time as the first replacement bag is hung in line.
  6. Advance to full flow. If the patient remains stable after 5 minutes, advance inlet flow incrementally (25 → 35 → 45–55 mL/min for typical adult). Most adults tolerate 50–60 mL/min steady-state; pediatric patients require weight-based reduction.
  7. Verify first hour fluid balance. At T=15 min, T=30 min, T=60 min, verify the running fluid balance display matches manual calculation. Address any > 100 mL discrepancy with the physician.
💡
First 30 Minutes Are the Highest Risk WindowThe majority of adverse events — citrate symptoms, allergic reactions, vasovagal hypotension — present within the first 30 minutes. Stay at the bedside. Avoid scheduling other patient interventions during this window. Vital signs every 5 minutes for the first 30 minutes, then every 15 minutes thereafter while stable.

The Citrate Symptom ScreenEvery 15 minutes, ask the patient: "Are you feeling any tingling around your mouth or fingers? Any cramping in your hands or feet? Any numbness?" Document the response. Even subtle perioral numbness is the earliest sign of hypocalcemia and warrants calcium replacement adjustment. Do not wait for tetany.
07
Termination & Rinseback
Completing the prescribed exchange, returning circuit blood
  1. Verify prescribed volume met. Confirm the target plasma volume removed matches the order (typically 1.0–1.5 PV). Document final volumes: plasma removed, total replacement infused, citrate delivered, net fluid balance.
  2. Initiate rinseback per machine prompt. Verify rinseback setting matches the prime used. Saline / albumin prime: Rinseback ON returns the circuit blood with saline. Diluted blood prime: Rinseback OFF — the prime fluid is left in the circuit and discarded.
  3. Monitor during rinseback. Continue vital signs every 5 minutes. Watch for delayed hypotension, volume overload symptoms (especially in cardiac or pediatric patients), or allergic reaction from residual circuit fluid.
  4. Disconnect the patient. Clamp inlet and return lines. Per institutional protocol, flush each lumen with saline followed by heparin lock or appropriate anticoagulant flush (e.g., heparin 100 U/mL, 5 mL per lumen, or per central catheter protocol).
  5. Inspect access site. For central catheter: secure dressing, check for hematoma or oozing. For peripheral: remove IV per facility protocol; apply pressure to puncture site for 5 minutes minimum, then dress.
  6. Final vital signs. Obtain post-procedure temperature, heart rate, respiratory rate, blood pressure, SpO2, weight. Compare to baseline and document.
  7. Patient comfort & symptom check. Verbally assess the patient: presence of citrate symptoms, light-headedness, nausea, fatigue. Reassure and review post-procedure instructions.
08
Post-Procedure Management & Patient Instructions
Observation, labs, education, follow-up
  • Post-procedure observation — Minimum 30 minutes bedside; longer if first session, transfer-out pending, hemodynamic borderline, or per physician order.
  • Post-procedure vital signs — Every 15 minutes × 2, then every 30 minutes × 2 until discharge/transfer.
  • Post-procedure labs — Ionized calcium and magnesium within 1 hour of procedure end (especially first session, prolonged procedure, or symptomatic). Repeat CBC and fibrinogen if > 1 procedure in 24 hours.
  • Hold ACE inhibitor for at least 24 hours after procedure if ACE-i restart is planned and albumin was used.
  • Patient education — Sit/stand cautiously due to potential orthostasis; report numbness or cramping; report any bruising, bleeding, fever, dyspnea, chest pain; verbal review and printed handout per institutional teaching materials.
  • Schedule next session — Per the prescribed schedule. For TTP: daily until clinical/biochemical remission. For autoimmune disease: every 1–3 days for 5–7 sessions typical induction.
  • Vascular access plan — Confirm continuation plan for chronic patients; verify central catheter dressing intact at discharge.
  • Coordinate replacement fluid for next session — Notify blood bank in advance for FFP procedures (thaw time required).
09
Fibrinogen Monitoring & Replacement Decisions
Why it falls, when to check, when to act

Fibrinogen is the single most important coagulation factor to monitor in TPE because it has the slowest recovery time of any plasma protein removed. Understanding why and when matters for both education and safety.

Why Fibrinogen Falls During TPE

Plasma exchange physically removes plasma proteins regardless of identity — autoantibodies (the therapeutic target), immune complexes, paraproteins, and all the proteins we'd rather keep, including fibrinogen, factor V, factor VIII, von Willebrand factor, and other coagulation factors. When the replacement fluid is albumin (5%) — the most common choice — none of these coagulation factors are replaced. The result: a single 1.0–1.5 plasma-volume exchange removes approximately 60–70% of circulating fibrinogen.

The body resynthesizes most coagulation factors within 24 hours. Fibrinogen is the exception — hepatic resynthesis is slower; recovery to baseline takes 48–72 hours. This is why consecutive daily TPE sessions cause cumulative depletion that single sessions do not.

When Fibrinogen Becomes Clinically Important

Monitoring Frequency by Scenario

  • Single TPE planned, then 48-hour break: Baseline fibrinogen acceptable; recheck only if next session planned within 72 hours.
  • 2–3 consecutive daily TPEs: Check fibrinogen before each session after the first. Replace if below 100 mg/dL.
  • 5+ consecutive daily TPEs (e.g., TTP induction): Check fibrinogen before every session. Include FFP component (typically 1–2 units) in replacement fluid for sessions 3+ to mitigate cumulative depletion.
  • TPE with concurrent bleeding risk (recent surgery, GI bleed, intracranial pathology, anticoagulant therapy): Baseline + before each session + after if low; consider FFP replacement from the start.
  • Invasive procedure scheduled within 24 hours after TPE (line placement, biopsy, surgery, lumbar puncture): Verify fibrinogen and platelet count; replace as needed to maintain fibrinogen > 150 mg/dL and platelets > 50 × 10⁹/L.

Patient and Family Education

  • Explain why fibrinogen matters — "Fibrinogen is a blood-clotting protein that gets removed by TPE. We track it to make sure your blood can still clot normally."
  • Recognize bleeding signs at home — Easy bruising, persistent gum bleeding, nosebleeds > 10 minutes, blood in urine or stool, severe headache (intracranial concern), heavy menstrual bleeding.
  • Avoid invasive procedures and certain medications in the 48-hour window after TPE without consulting the apheresis team.
  • Reassurance — Fibrinogen recovers naturally; the monitoring is to identify the few patients who need replacement, not because every TPE patient needs replacement.
🔑
The Cryoprecipitate / FFP DecisionCryoprecipitate is concentrated fibrinogen (~150–250 mg per unit); 10 units raise plasma fibrinogen by approximately 70–100 mg/dL. Indicated when isolated fibrinogen depletion is the issue. FFP (1 unit ≈ 200–250 mL plasma containing all coagulation factors) is preferred when multi-factor deficiency is suspected or when TPE is being performed on a patient with established consumptive coagulopathy. Coordinate with blood bank in advance — both require thaw or preparation time.
⚠
TTP-Specific ConsiderationIn TTP, FFP is typically used as the replacement fluid throughout the induction course because (a) replacement of ADAMTS13 is therapeutic, and (b) consecutive daily exchanges would otherwise cause severe coagulopathy. Cryoprecipitate is generally avoided in TTP because it contains higher von Willebrand factor multimers that may worsen platelet aggregation.

Documentation & Quality Indicators
What to record; what to audit

Documentation must capture the prescription, performance, patient response, and any deviations. The institutional procedure record is the primary document; supplementary documentation in the electronic medical record and the blood bank computer system as applicable.

  • Identifiers — Patient name, MRN, DOB, date and time of procedure, accession number, RN signature.
  • Order — Indication, ASFA category, prescribing physician, plasma volume target, replacement fluid type and volume, AC strategy, schedule.
  • Pre-procedure — Verification of labs, ACE-i status, vascular access, consent, baseline vital signs, weight.
  • Procedure — Start time, machine model and serial, disposable set lot, replacement fluid lots and unit numbers (for FFP), AC ratio, inlet flow trends, AIR trends, vital signs at the prescribed frequency, citrate symptom screens, fluid balance hourly, calcium replacement total, replacement fluid totals.
  • End — End time, total plasma removed, total replacement infused, net fluid balance, rinseback method, post-procedure vital signs, post-procedure assessment.
  • Adverse events — Time, type, vital signs trend, interventions, response, physician notification, blood bank notification (if reaction to FFP), event report into safety event system if reportable per institutional definition.
  • Communication — Handoff to receiving nurse using SBAR; documentation of physician orders received; patient/family education topics covered.
