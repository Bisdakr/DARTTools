Why Hemodialysis?
Diffusion meets convection across a synthetic membrane: how the modern dialyzer replaces 50,000 nephrons, what every order parameter actually does, and the bedside math that keeps patients safe.
01 · Diffusion & Convection 02 · The Dialyzer 03 · High vs Low Flux 04 · Kt/V Adequacy 05 · Order Parameters 06 · UFR Safety 07 · CritLine 08 · Pediatrics 09 · 2024–2025 References
TL;DR — The 60-second version
Hemodialysis replaces the lost work of failed kidneys by moving solutes across a synthetic semipermeable membrane. Two physical processes do the work: diffusion (small molecules — urea, K, phosphate — move down their concentration gradient into dialysate) and convection (water plus dissolved solute moves under hydrostatic pressure during ultrafiltration). The modern Fresenius dialyzer has ~1.5–2.2 m² of synthetic membrane surface — equivalent to roughly 50,000 nephrons of clearance. A typical 4-hour session, three times a week, restores enough solute control to keep patients alive while waiting for transplant or continuing chronic care.

01
Diffusion + Convection: The Two Engines
What actually moves across the membrane, and why

Hemodialysis is the controlled application of two physical principles. Diffusion moves solutes down their concentration gradient: a uremic patient has BUN of, say, 90 mg/dL; the dialysate has zero BUN. Across the membrane, urea diffuses from blood to dialysate. The rate is governed by the concentration gradient, the blood flow rate (Qb), the dialysate flow rate (Qd), the surface area of the membrane, and the permeability coefficient for that solute.

Convection moves water plus dissolved solute under hydrostatic pressure — the same physics as drinking a soda through a straw. During ultrafiltration, the dialysis machine pulls plasma water across the membrane; whatever's dissolved goes along for the ride. This is how the machine removes the interdialytic fluid weight gain.

🔑
Diffusion vs Convection — solute size mattersSmall solutes (urea, K, phosphate, creatinine) clear well by diffusion. Middle molecules (β2-microglobulin, parathyroid hormone, advanced glycation end-products) clear better by convection. This is why hemodiafiltration (HDF) — which boosts convection deliberately — is associated with improved survival in some trials.

The dialysate flows countercurrent to blood, which maintains a maximum concentration gradient along the entire length of the dialyzer. If they flowed concurrently, the gradient would equilibrate halfway down and the second half of the membrane would do nothing.

02
The Dialyzer — A Bundle of Hollow Fibers
10,000 capillaries, 2 m² of surface, 100 mL of priming volume

The modern dialyzer is a cylindrical case containing roughly 10,000 hollow polysulfone or polyethersulfone fibers, each about 200 µm in internal diameter. Blood flows through the lumen of these fibers; dialysate flows in the space surrounding them. The total surface area for exchange is 1.5–2.2 m² depending on the model.

Selecting the right dialyzer is partly about clearance efficiency (which scales with surface area) and partly about priming volume, which matters for hemodynamic stability and pediatric care. The DART HD calculator's dialyzer/tubing selector accounts for both.

💡
Why "NR" mattersThe "NR" suffix on Optiflux dialyzers indicates non-reuse — single patient, single use, then discarded. This is now the dominant US practice. Reuse was common in the 1990s but raised infection-control concerns.

High Flux vs Low Flux
Membrane pore size, β2-microglobulin clearance, and the HEMO trial

Dialyzer "flux" describes how permeable the membrane is to middle-sized molecules and water:

TypeKUf (mL/hr/mmHg)β2-MG clearanceModern use
Low-flux< 10MinimalRare today (acute, certain countries)
High-flux20–60SignificantDominant standard
Super-flux / HDF> 60HighHemodiafiltration centers

The HEMO trial (2002) compared high-flux vs low-flux dialysis. The primary endpoint (all-cause mortality) was negative, but secondary endpoints favored high-flux — particularly in patients on dialysis > 3.7 years. Subsequent European trials (MPO) confirmed mortality benefit of high-flux in specific subgroups. Today the US standard is high-flux.

🔬
Why β2-microglobulin mattersβ2-microglobulin (~11.8 kDa) accumulates in dialysis patients and deposits in joint cavities as dialysis-related amyloidosis, causing the carpal-tunnel-like syndrome seen after years on low-flux. High-flux clears β2-MG ~30 mL/min vs essentially zero for low-flux. After 5+ years, the difference shows up in joint disease.
04
Kt/V — The Adequacy Number
Why ≥1.4 single-pool is the modern target

The single most important number in chronic HD is Kt/V:

  • 📏 K = dialyzer urea clearance (mL/min)
  • ⏱️ t = treatment time (min)
  • 💧 V = patient total body water volume (mL)

Kt/V is a dimensionless number representing the fraction of total body water cleared of urea per session. Kt/V of 1.0 means one full equivalent of body water has been cleared. Modern targets:

PopulationSingle-pool Kt/VEquilibrated Kt/V
Adult thrice-weekly minimum≥ 1.2≥ 1.05
Adult thrice-weekly target≥ 1.4≥ 1.2
Pediatric target≥ 1.5≥ 1.3
Twice-weekly (with residual function)≥ 1.8—

The HEMO trial showed no benefit beyond a Kt/V of ~1.4. Pushing higher carries the cost of longer sessions, more hemodynamic stress, and patient burnout. The aim is "just enough" adequacy combined with the lowest-stress treatment.

🔑
URR — the simpler bedside numberUrea reduction ratio = (pre-BUN − post-BUN) / pre-BUN. URR of ≥ 65% roughly corresponds to spKt/V of 1.2. Easier to measure with a single pre/post lab pair. Many centers track both.

⚠
Watch the K bathThe single most common cause of inpatient HD-related arrhythmia is aggressive K removal — typically using a K=1 bath against a serum K of 5.5. The K gradient becomes steepest early in the session when serum K hasn't yet equilibrated downward, and this is when ectopy and unstable rhythms appear. Most centers reserve K=1 for true emergencies and use K=2 or K=3 for routine elevation.
06
UFR Safety — The 13 mL/kg/hr Rule
Why aggressive ultrafiltration kills

Ultrafiltration rate (UFR) is fluid removed per hour, normalized to body weight: UFR = UF goal (mL) / time (hr) / weight (kg). Above ~13 mL/kg/hr, the risk of intradialytic hypotension, end-organ ischemia (myocardial stunning, mesenteric ischemia, white-matter injury), and post-dialysis fatigue rises sharply.

⚠
The 13 mL/kg/hr thresholdObservational data (Flythe, KDIGO) suggest that UFR > 13 mL/kg/hr is independently associated with increased mortality. UFR > 10 is a "yellow zone" prompting extended time or restricted interdialytic fluid intake. The DART HD calculator flags this number directly.

The clinical lesson: a patient who gains 5 kg between Friday and Monday treatments and gets removed in 4 hours has UFR ~15 mL/kg/hr (for 80 kg dry weight) — too high. Options: extend time (5-hour session), counsel on interdialytic fluid intake, or accept a smaller removal target and split removal across more sessions.

07
CritLine — Real-Time Hematocrit Monitoring
Refilling, plasma volume, and the IDH puzzle

The CritLine monitor measures blood-side hematocrit continuously throughout an HD session. As plasma water is removed by ultrafiltration faster than the interstitium can refill the vascular compartment, hematocrit rises — and the rate of rise reveals the patient's plasma refilling rate.

Three patterns are clinically useful:

Hct patternInterpretationAction
Flat or slow riseGood refilling; volume overloadedContinue UF — patient tolerating
Steep rise (>5% in 30 min)Refilling exhausted; near dry weightReduce or stop UF
Sudden plateau or dropHemorrhage, circuit issueInvestigate urgently

CritLine-guided dialysis is associated with fewer intradialytic hypotensive episodes in observational studies, though randomized trials have been mixed. The technology is most valuable in patients with hypotension during conventional UF — the visual feedback helps the nurse titrate.

08
Pediatric Hemodialysis
Where ECV percentage becomes critical

Pediatric HD is dominated by a single safety constraint: the extracorporeal volume (dialyzer prime + tubing prime) must not exceed ~10% of the patient's total blood volume without prime modification.

🔑
Prime decision rules (ECV / TBV)< 10% → standard NS prime acceptable. 10–15% → consider albumin prime or diluted-blood prime. > 15% → blood-prime required (typically 1 unit PRBC). The DART HD calculator computes ECV% from the selected dialyzer + tubing automatically.

Pediatric TBV bands (used by the DART calculator and most pediatric nephrology references):

Weight bandTBV estimate
Neonate (< 5 kg)90 mL/kg
5–10 kg85 mL/kg
10–25 kg80 mL/kg
> 25 kgNadler equation (~70 mL/kg)

Other pediatric adjustments: smaller dialyzer (Revaclear 300, Optiflux F160NR), lower Qb (3–5 mL/kg/min), lower Qd (proportional to Qb), shorter sessions (often 3 hr), and tighter UFR ceilings.

09
2024–2025 Developments
HDF, expanded hemodialysis, and home dialysis growth
🔬
CONVINCE trial — HDF wins mortalityThe CONVINCE trial (2023, published in NEJM) randomized European patients to high-volume online hemodiafiltration vs standard high-flux HD. HDF reduced all-cause mortality by 23%. This is the first prospective evidence that boosting convection improves hard endpoints. HDF requires water-quality and machine upgrades not universal in US centers — adoption is now an active US policy discussion.
🧪
Expanded Hemodialysis (HDx)Medium cut-off (MCO) membranes such as Theranova approach HDF-like middle-molecule clearance without the water requirements. Several 2024 trials are evaluating HDx vs high-flux for inflammation markers and patient-reported outcomes.
🏠
Home HD continues to growThe Tablo and NxStage systems have lowered the barrier to home HD. CMS payment reform (TPNIES, Bundle changes) continues to incentivize home modalities. Patients on more-frequent home HD (5–6× weekly, shorter sessions) show better volume control and fewer interdialytic symptoms.
⚖️
Incremental dialysisFor patients with residual renal function starting maintenance HD, twice-weekly schedules with adjusted Kt/V targets are gaining acceptance. The ASN/KDIGO guidance has shifted toward preserving residual function rather than starting full thrice-weekly dialysis immediately.
🤖
Wearable / portable kidneyThe wearable artificial kidney (WAK) and other continuous portable dialysis devices remain in clinical-trial stages. Early data shows feasibility for 24-hour continuous low-flow dialysis with potential physiologic advantages, but supply-chain and durability hurdles remain.
The history of dialysis is the history of making the synthetic kidney slightly more biological — first by treating it for longer, then by clearing larger molecules, and now by sensing the patient's hemodynamics in real time.
