CRRT Safety Considerations
Citrate toxicity in hepatic failure, electrolyte and acid-base management, hypothermia, drug clearance, vascular access, and the 24/7 nursing burden of a continuous extracorporeal therapy in the ICU. Compiled from KDIGO 2012, RICH/RENAL/STARRT-AKI trials, and current ICU CRRT safety literature.

TL;DR — The safety story in one paragraph
CRRT is one of the safer renal replacement modalities for hemodynamically unstable patients because it's slow and continuous — but the 24/7 nature means safety issues compound. The dominant therapy-specific concerns are citrate toxicity in patients with poor citrate metabolism (hepatic failure, shock), cumulative electrolyte loss (phosphate, magnesium, ionized calcium), hypothermia (cold replacement fluid running continuously), drug clearance unpredictability (sedatives, antibiotics, vasopressors all behave differently), and filter clotting from excessive filtration fraction. Vascular access infection is the dominant long-term complication. The most important safety system is the bedside nurse — careful protocol adherence, hourly fluid balance verification, and prompt response to alarms.

Citrate Toxicity — The Defining RCA Safety Issue
Recognition, prevention, response

Regional citrate anticoagulation (RCA) relies on the liver, muscle, and kidney to metabolize citrate at a rate matching delivery. In hepatic failure (cirrhosis, acute liver failure, post-transplant) or shock states with poor perfusion, citrate metabolism slows and citrate accumulates.

🔑
The hallmark — total/ionized calcium ratioCitrate binds ionized calcium and creates a citrate-calcium complex. Total serum calcium remains stable (or rises slightly), but ionized calcium drops. The ratio Total Ca (mmol/L) / Ionized Ca (mmol/L) > 2.5 indicates citrate accumulation. Most institutions check this every 6–12 hr in high-risk patients.
Citrate accumulation severityTotal/iCa ratioOther featuresResponse
None< 2.25Normal acid-baseContinue as prescribed
Mild2.25–2.5Mild metabolic acidosisVerify metabolism, adjust calcium drip
Moderate2.5–3.0Worsening metabolic acidosis, ↑anion gapReduce citrate dose; consider switching anticoagulant
Severe> 3.0Severe metabolic acidosis, hypotension, low iCaSTOP citrate; switch to heparin or saline-only; aggressive Ca replacement
⚠
Pre-emptive screeningFor any patient with cirrhosis (Child-Pugh B/C), acute liver failure, severe shock requiring norepinephrine > 0.3 mcg/kg/min, post-cardiac arrest, or severe lactic acidosis, monitor the total/ionized calcium ratio every 6 hours. Consider starting with reduced citrate dose (2 mmol/L) or alternative anticoagulant.

Other RCA-related safety considerations:

  • Calcium replacement titration — Target systemic iCa 1.1–1.3 mmol/L; post-filter iCa 0.25–0.40 mmol/L (the latter confirms adequate circuit anticoagulation)
  • Metabolic alkalosis from citrate-to-bicarbonate conversion (citrate yields 3 bicarbonate per molecule). May require adjusting dialysate bicarbonate.
  • Hypernatremia from sodium in citrate solutions (Prismocitrate 18/0 = 140 mEq/L Na). Verify daily Na trend.

Electrolyte & Acid-Base Management
Cumulative depletion is universal

CRRT removes solutes continuously. Without proactive replacement, depletion is universal — particularly for phosphate, magnesium, and (in heparin-based circuits) calcium.

ElectrolyteDirectionFrequency of checkReplacement strategy
PhosphateFalls rapidly (~6 hr)Every 12 hrAdd to replacement fluid OR IV bolus per protocol
MagnesiumFalls progressivelyEvery 12–24 hrIV bolus; some replacement fluids contain Mg
Ionized CalciumCitrate: needs replacement · Heparin: usually stableEvery 4–6 hr (RCA); 12 hr (heparin)Per RCA protocol
PotassiumFalls if K-low dialysateEvery 6 hrBicarbonate dialysate K can be customized; PO/IV bolus PRN
SodiumGenerally stableDailyVerify dialysate Na ≈ patient Na
BicarbonateUsually corrects (delivery from dialysate + citrate conversion)Every 8–12 hr ABGAdjust dialysate HCO3 (typical 25–35 mEq/L)
⚠
Severe hypophosphatemia → arrhythmiaPhosphate < 1.5 mg/dL increases risk of cardiac arrhythmia, muscle weakness (respiratory failure), and rhabdomyolysis. Replace aggressively or use phosphate-containing replacement fluid (Phoxilium, Hemosol-with-Phos, or pharmacy-mixed).
04
Hypothermia
Continuous cold fluid + extracorporeal exposure

Even with bag/circuit warmers, CRRT typically causes ~1°C body temperature drop over 24 hours from cold replacement fluid + heat loss in the extracorporeal circuit. Masking of fever (an important sepsis indicator) is a real concern.

  • Use a circuit/fluid warmer (most modern platforms include this)
  • Room temperature management — keep ICU room comfortable
  • External warming (Bair Hugger or similar) for shock patients
  • Don't trust temperature alone for fever screening — culture lower threshold for infection workup
  • In hyperthermic states (heat stroke, neuroleptic malignant syndrome, malignant hyperthermia): CRRT can actively cool, useful

Drug Clearance During CRRT
A pharmacist's domain — but every nurse should know the highlights

CRRT alters drug pharmacokinetics in complex, drug-specific ways. The sieving coefficient (S), protein binding, volume of distribution, and effluent dose all determine clearance.

Drug classCRRT impactAction
VancomycinSignificantly cleared (60–90% removed)Dose more frequently; trough monitoring; pharmacy consult
AminoglycosidesCleared; risk of inadequate doseLevels; pharmacy consult
β-lactams (piperacillin, meropenem, cefepime)Variable; often under-dosedHigher dose or extended infusion per pharmacy
Antifungals (fluconazole, voriconazole)Variable; voriconazole minimally affectedLevels for voriconazole; pharmacy consult
Sedatives (midazolam, propofol)Variable; midazolam metabolites accumulateDaily sedation interruption; consider non-renal-cleared agents
Anticonvulsants (phenytoin, levetiracetam, valproate)Variable; levels essentialLevels; pharmacy consult
Vasopressors (norepinephrine, vasopressin)Minimal clearance; titrate to effectStandard titration to MAP
🔑
Pharmacist on roundsMost ICUs running CRRT have a pharmacist on daily rounds for drug dose review. Verify pharmacist has been consulted for any CRRT-treated patient. Particularly important: antibiotic dosing, sedative selection, anticonvulsant levels, anticoagulant dose adjustment.

Vascular Access Safety
The dominant long-term complication

CRRT requires a large-bore (11–14 Fr adult) dual-lumen central venous catheter, typically in the internal jugular or femoral vein. The catheter is dedicated to CRRT — no medication infusions or blood draws through CRRT lumens.

SiteProsCons
Right internal jugularLowest CLABSI rate; best flow geometryDifficult in agitated patient
FemoralEasy to place; useful in emergenciesHigher CLABSI rate; limits mobility
Left internal jugularAcceptableCatheter path more tortuous; some flow issues
SubclavianLowest infection ratePneumothorax risk; potential for stenosis — avoid in chronic dialysis candidates
  • Ultrasound-guided placement standard of care
  • Sterile barrier precautions at insertion (full body drape, mask, hat, sterile gloves, chlorhexidine prep)
  • Chest X-ray post-insertion if non-femoral
  • Daily exit-site inspection for erythema, drainage, tenderness
  • Strict sterile technique at every access — sterile gloves, mask, chlorhexidine wipe of hub
  • Dedicated CRRT lumens — no other use
  • Remove ASAP when CRRT no longer needed
  • CLABSI surveillance per institutional NHSN protocol

Filter Clotting & FFR Management
Operational safety, dose delivery, and circuit changes

Filter clotting is the most common operational issue. Filter clotting events: (1) reduce delivered dose, (2) trigger alarm fatigue, (3) waste consumables, (4) sometimes require emergent disconnection. Strategies to reduce clotting:

StrategyHow it helps
Regional citrate anticoagulationMost effective; longer filter life than heparin
Higher BFR (150–200 mL/min)Reduces stasis; reduces FFR at fixed dose
Pre-dilution replacementReduces hemoconcentration at filter
Keep FFR < 25%Below the hemoconcentration danger zone
Avoid frequent circuit interruptionStasis = clotting; minimize disconnects for transport, imaging
Filter set sizingLarger surface area = more reserve; choose appropriately
👁
Pressure trendsRising transmembrane pressure (TMP) signals filter clotting before complete failure. Track pressure trends; consider proactive circuit change when TMP rises > 250–300 mmHg or filter pressure drops are climbing. Patient dose interruption matters.
08
Hemodynamic Safety
Net UF, MAP targets, vasopressor management

CRRT is chosen for hemodynamically unstable patients precisely because it minimizes hemodynamic stress. But aggressive net UF or rapid circuit initiation can still cause hypotension.

  • Net UF target: typically 0–1.5 mL/kg/hr; pediatric 0.5–1.0 mL/kg/hr
  • MAP target per ICU team — typically > 65 mmHg; renal-protective > 70 mmHg if pre-existing CKD
  • Slow start: first 30 min low BFR (100–125), zero net UF; observe BP response
  • Hourly fluid balance verification — manual recalculation against machine display
  • Vasopressor management: not cleared by CRRT; titrate as usual
  • If hypotension: stop or reduce net UF; assess for sepsis worsening, cardiac event, citrate accumulation
09
Pediatric CRRT Safety
ECV margin, blood prime, vascular access
  • ECV ceiling 10% TBV — blood prime if exceeded
  • Pediatric TBV: ≤ 5 kg → 90 mL/kg · 5.1–15 kg → 80 mL/kg · 15.1–25 kg → 70 mL/kg
  • Smaller filter sets (HF20 for < 10 kg)
  • Vascular access 5–7 Fr for small patients
  • Citrate dosing per pediatric protocol — typically lower target than adult
  • Aggressive phosphate / magnesium / calcium monitoring
  • Drug clearance particularly important — pediatric pharmacist consultation
  • Hypothermia risk higher in small body mass — active warming standard
  • CARPEDIEM platform for neonates / < 10 kg patients
  • Family / caregiver education for prolonged ICU stays

Emergency Response
When something goes wrong
⚠ Severe Citrate Accumulation
  1. STOP citrate infusion; continue blood flow without anticoagulant if filter remains patent.
  2. Aggressive ionized calcium replacement — CaCl₂ or Ca gluconate bolus + drip.
  3. Switch to heparin or saline-flush anticoagulation if continuing CRRT.
  4. Treat metabolic acidosis with bicarbonate-containing dialysate adjustment if severe.
  5. Address underlying liver dysfunction.
⚠ Sudden Severe Hypotension During CRRT
  1. Reduce/stop net UF; continue circulation at reduced flow.
  2. IV crystalloid bolus through non-CRRT line.
  3. Investigate cause: citrate, sepsis worsening, cardiac event, anaphylactoid (rare), filter reaction.
  4. Escalate vasopressors as needed.
  5. Consider pausing CRRT if hemodynamic instability persists.
⚠ Air Embolism Suspected
  1. STOP all flow; clamp venous line.
  2. Left lateral decubitus, Trendelenburg position (traps air in right atrium).
  3. 100% O₂ via non-rebreather.
  4. Call code response; consider hyperbaric oxygen if cerebral symptoms.
  5. Root-cause analysis; equipment check.
⚠ Disconnection / Massive Blood Loss
  1. Clamp the catheter immediately; direct pressure to access site.
  2. Hold the circuit components for analysis (do not discard).
  3. Resuscitation: IV fluids, blood products if needed.
  4. Type & cross; CBC and coags.
  5. Report to safety event system; equipment / connection review.
