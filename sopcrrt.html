CRRT Standards of Practice
A USA-aligned standard for performing continuous renal replacement therapy using Baxter Prismaflex and Prismax platforms in the intensive care unit. Aligned with KDIGO 2012 Acute Kidney Injury Guideline, RICH trial findings (Zarbock et al., JAMA 2020) on regional citrate anticoagulation, RENAL/ATN/STARRT-AKI dose and timing evidence, AACN critical-care nursing standards, and current device labeling.

Standard of Practice — Overview
Continuous Renal Replacement Therapy (CRRT) provides slow, continuous solute and fluid removal for critically ill ICU patients with acute kidney injury who cannot tolerate intermittent hemodialysis. This SOP describes the procedure using Baxter Prismaflex or Prismax platforms with the M-series filter sets. The standard effluent dose target per KDIGO 2012 is 20–25 mL/kg/hr; the dominant intra-procedure issue is filter clotting from hemoconcentration; regional citrate anticoagulation (RCA) is preferred over heparin per KDIGO Grade 2B and supported by the RICH trial. Because CRRT runs continuously (24/7), shift handoff is a defined safety event, requiring structured SBAR communication, hourly fluid balance verification, and rigorous documentation.

01
Purpose & Scope

Purpose: To define the standardized procedure for continuous renal replacement therapy in adult and pediatric ICU patients with acute kidney injury, severe fluid overload, severe electrolyte disturbance, intoxication, or other indications where slow continuous extracorporeal solute and fluid management is preferred over intermittent hemodialysis.

Scope: Critical care registered nurses with documented CRRT competency, intensivists, nephrologists, ICU pharmacists, biomedical / equipment staff. Generally one nurse per CRRT patient (1:1 nursing ratio during initiation; 1:2 acceptable during steady-state per institutional policy).

Indications recognized: AKI with severe acidosis, severe hyperkalemia refractory to medical therapy, refractory fluid overload, severe uremia, intoxication (salicylate, ethylene glycol, methanol, metformin, valproate, lithium), hyperammonemia (hepatic failure, urea cycle defects, post-organ transplant), severe rhabdomyolysis. Per KDIGO 2012 staging and indication framework.

🔑
Timing — STARRT-AKI LessonThe STARRT-AKI trial (NEJM 2020, n=3,019) showed no mortality benefit of early initiation. Wait for absolute indications (severe acidosis pH < 7.10, K > 6.5 with ECG changes, refractory volume overload with pulmonary edema, intoxication, severe uremia with encephalopathy / pericarditis) rather than pre-emptively starting on lab numbers alone.
02
Roles & Responsibilities

Intensivist / Nephrologist

Determines indication; specifies modality (CVVHDF / CVVHD / CVVH / SCUF), effluent dose, anticoagulation strategy, replacement fluid composition, net UF goal; coordinates with hematology if citrate accumulation concern; daily review.

CRRT-Competent ICU RN (Primary)

Operates the CRRT machine; performs hourly fluid balance verification; manages alarms; titrates citrate / calcium per protocol; documents; communicates pressure trends and clinical response.

Pharmacy / ICU Pharmacist

Drug dose adjustment for CRRT clearance; antibiotic dosing (often higher / extended infusion); sedative selection; therapeutic drug monitoring as indicated.

Biomedical / Equipment Technician

Maintains machine per manufacturer; calibration; alarm response support; consumable inventory.

Nephrology Consultation (when nephrology not primary)

Prescription review; chronic CKD/ESRD planning; transition planning.

03
Pre-Initiation Verification
  1. Two-identifier patient ID.
  2. Order verification. Modality, prescribed dose (mL/kg/hr), pre/post replacement ratio, dialysate flow, BFR, anticoagulation strategy and dosing, net UF goal, replacement fluid composition.
  3. Calculate effluent dose target. Effluent (mL/hr) = weight (kg) × prescribed dose (mL/kg/hr). Confirm within KDIGO 20–25 mL/kg/hr range unless specific indication for higher.
  4. Pre-procedure labs. Comprehensive metabolic panel including ionized calcium, magnesium, phosphorus, lactate, baseline blood gas; CBC; PT/PTT, fibrinogen; type and screen if blood prime considered.
  5. Vascular access verification. Confirm large-bore (11–14 Fr adult; 5–7 Fr pediatric) dual-lumen central venous catheter; verify position by recent radiograph if newly placed; confirm patency on each lumen with blood return.
  6. RCA candidacy assessment. Hepatic function (Child-Pugh, MELD if cirrhosis); shock state with severe perfusion compromise; lactate. If hepatic failure or severe shock — RCA still possible but requires close monitoring of total/iCa ratio. Some institutions use heparin in severe hepatic failure.
  7. Baseline vital signs, weight, hemodynamic status. Vasopressor doses documented.
  8. Pharmacy consult initiated. All current medications reviewed for CRRT clearance adjustment.

🔑
Catheter Removal Advocacy — Daily DecisionEvery CRRT catheter is a potential CLABSI source. The CRRT RN should actively advocate for catheter removal at every shift handoff when the catheter is no longer needed — for example, after renal recovery off CRRT, when the patient is transitioning to outpatient HD, or at end-of-life when CRRT is being withdrawn. SBAR to the intensivist or nephrologist: "This CRRT catheter has been in for X days. Plan for removal — what is needed before we can remove?" Final decision per physician discretion, but the nursing advocacy is part of CLABSI prevention.

Anticoagulation Strategy

KDIGO 2012 recommends regional citrate anticoagulation (RCA) as preferred (Grade 2B), supported by the RICH trial showing longer filter life and fewer bleeding events vs heparin.

⚠
Citrate AccumulationIn hepatic failure or severe shock with poor citrate clearance, citrate can accumulate. Hallmark: rising total/ionized calcium ratio > 2.5, worsening metabolic acidosis, often with low ionized calcium needing escalating calcium replacement. Response: reduce or stop citrate; switch to heparin or saline-only; aggressive calcium replacement; consider underlying treatment for liver failure.
06
Circuit Setup & Prime
  1. Power on machine; select modality (CVVHDF default). Verify the Prismaflex or Prismax interface shows correct procedure.
  2. Install the filter set. Match to patient size — HF20 (< 10 kg), M60 (10–25 kg), M100 (standard adult), M150 (large adult / high-dose). ST series if heparin-based circuit.
  3. Hang solutions. Replacement fluid (Prismasol, Hemosol-with-Phos, Phoxilium per institutional protocol); dialysate; anticoagulant (Prismocitrate 18/0 or 10/2); calcium gluconate or CaCl₂ replacement drip.
  4. Program prescription parameters. Modality, BFR, pre/post replacement ratio, dialysate flow, net UF rate, citrate target (typically 3 mmol/L blood), calcium replacement starting rate.
  5. Run automated prime. Saline prime; verify circuit integrity; address any pressure or alarm issues.
  6. Verify alarm parameters. Pressure limits, air detector, blood leak detector.
  7. Stage replacement fluids and consumables for the planned session duration; verify supply chain for next 24–48 hours.
07
Initiation
  1. Final pre-connection checks.
  2. Sterile catheter access. Don sterile gloves and mask; chlorhexidine wipe to catheter hubs; aspirate heparin lock; verify blood return on each lumen.
  3. Connect inlet and return lines per catheter lumen assignment.
  4. Slow-start protocol. Begin BFR at 100–125 mL/min for first 30 minutes; net UF zero. Observe hemodynamic response.
  5. Citrate / calcium initiation per RCA protocol. Begin citrate; begin calcium drip.
  6. Document initial baseline. Time of start, vital signs, machine parameters, alarm settings.
  7. Advance to target BFR 150–200 mL/min if hemodynamically tolerated.
  8. Initiate net UF per order — typically start at 50% of target and titrate up if MAP stable.

09
Shift Handoff — A Defined Safety Event

CRRT runs 24/7; nursing handoff occurs at least 2–3 times per day. Each handoff is a potential point of error and must be structured.

  1. Bedside SBAR with both outgoing and incoming RN.
  2. Verify machine parameters together. Modality, BFR, doses, anticoagulation, replacement fluid composition, net UF.
  3. Manual fluid balance recalculation with two-RN verification.
  4. Review current iCa, total/iCa ratio, electrolytes, drug levels.
  5. Review filter pressures and trend; estimated filter life.
  6. Review pharmacy / antibiotic dosing changes.
  7. Review pending labs and orders.
  8. Document handoff completion in the procedure record.
10
Filter Change & Termination

Filter life typically 24–72 hours depending on anticoagulation and clinical factors. Plan filter changes proactively when TMP rises, pressures climb, or pre-emptively at 48–72 hours to avoid emergent change.

  1. Recognize the need for change. Rising TMP > 250–300 mmHg; access or return pressure trending; visible darkening or thickening of filter; rising blood-side pressure trend.
  2. Stage replacement filter set, solutions, and consumables.
  3. Discontinue current circuit per machine prompt. Return circuit blood if possible (rinseback per protocol) or discard if filter is clotted.
  4. Sterile catheter management during disconnect. Mask, sterile gloves; flush each lumen with saline + heparin lock or per protocol.
  5. Setup new circuit per Section 06.
  6. Resume CRRT. Update fluid balance counter; reset accumulators; document downtime.
  7. For termination of therapy (renal recovery / transition). Coordinate with physician — UF goal final, replacement final, discontinue per machine, disconnect, observe; transition to IHD or off renal support per nephrology recommendation.

CLABSI Management — CRRT-Specific Considerations
Recognition, antibiotic salvage, line holiday in the ICU setting

CRRT catheter CLABSI is particularly consequential because the patient is already critically ill, the catheter is dwelling in central circulation for an extended period (often 5–14 days during a CRRT course), and source control may require interrupting therapy. Aggressive prevention is paramount; when infection occurs, decision-making follows the IDSA framework.

Recognition in the ICU Patient

  • New fever or rising temperature with no other identifiable source — always consider the CRRT catheter.
  • Unexplained hemodynamic instability: new vasopressor requirement, dropping MAP, rising lactate.
  • New leukocytosis or worsening sepsis picture.
  • Local catheter exit-site findings: erythema, purulent drainage, tenderness, swelling.
  • Catheter dysfunction: declining flows, increasing pressures may signal fibrin sheath with secondary infection.

Diagnostic Workup

  1. Blood cultures from each catheter lumen plus a peripheral set. Time-to-positivity comparison (catheter ≥ 2 hours faster than peripheral) supports catheter as source.
  2. If catheter removed: catheter tip culture (semiquantitative roll-plate or quantitative). > 15 CFU/mL on roll-plate is significant.
  3. Inspect exit site; swab if purulence present.
  4. Other infectious workup as appropriate — urinalysis, chest imaging, abdominal exam — to ensure CRRT catheter is the source, not a comorbid focus.

Empiric Antibiotic Therapy

  • Vancomycin loading dose per pharmacy (CRRT-adjusted dosing) — typically 25 mg/kg IV.
  • Gram-negative coverage per institutional antibiogram and patient risk profile — cefepime, piperacillin-tazobactam, or carbapenem.
  • Consider antifungal if patient is immunocompromised, has prolonged antibiotic exposure, TPN, or other Candida risk factors.
  • Dose adjustments by CRRT pharmacist — many antibiotics require extended-interval or continuous infusion strategies on CRRT.
  • Stewardship narrowing based on culture and sensitivity results within 48–72 hours.

Line Holiday in the ICU Setting

"Line holiday" — the deliberate period of no central catheter between removal of an infected line and placement of a new tunneled or replacement catheter — is a recognized CLABSI prevention strategy. In the ICU CRRT patient, this requires careful planning because therapy may need to continue.

  • Remove the infected catheter. Send tip for culture.
  • Establish temporary access for ongoing CRRT — typically a temporary non-tunneled femoral catheter, or a peripheral non-tunneled IJ catheter. The temporary access permits CRRT continuation while the original site heals.
  • Treat with systemic IV antibiotics for 48–72 hours through the temporary access (or peripheral if possible). Continue CRRT through temporary access.
  • Repeat blood cultures at 48–72 hours. Both peripheral and through the new temporary access.
  • Only when blood cultures are negative proceed to permanent or longer-term access placement — at a different anatomic site if possible.
  • Avoid guidewire exchange in the same site for an infected catheter — full removal and new site placement has lower recurrence rate.
  • Coordinate with vascular access service or surgical team for the timing of permanent access.

Documentation & Reporting

  • NHSN CLABSI event report per CDC surveillance protocol.
  • Root-cause analysis for any CLABSI: catheter insertion technique, dressing changes, hub-care practice, dwell time, hand hygiene compliance.
  • Cluster surveillance: if multiple CLABSIs in close temporal proximity, escalate to infection prevention for unit-level investigation.
  • Closing the loop: outcome of antibiotic course, repeat cultures, catheter outcome, patient outcome.
⚠
Antibiotic Lock Therapy in CRRTAntibiotic lock therapy (high-concentration antibiotic dwelling in the catheter lumen) is less commonly used in CRRT than in chronic outpatient HD because the catheter is in active use 24/7 — there is no dwell time. For salvage attempts in CRRT, systemic antibiotics are the primary tool. Some institutions briefly pause CRRT to instill a lock during a transient period (e.g., during a planned filter change), but this is institution-specific and not standard.

Documentation & Quality Indicators
  • Prescription detail. Modality, dose, BFR, replacement composition, anticoagulation, net UF goal.
  • Hourly fluid balance. Manual recalculation with timestamp.
  • Hourly vital signs and machine parameters.
  • Anticoagulation monitoring. iCa systemic, iCa post-filter, total/iCa ratio (if RCA); aPTT or ACT (if heparin).
  • Electrolyte trends. Q-hourly to Q-12-hourly per parameter.
  • Filter pressure trends.
  • Drug dose adjustments and pharmacy interventions.
  • Shift handoff documentation. SBAR completion per shift.
  • Filter change events. Time, reason, downtime, achieved dose.
  • Adverse events. Type, time, intervention, response.
  • Termination summary. Total CRRT duration, achieved effluent dose, fluid balance summary, transition plan.
