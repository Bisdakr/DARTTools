TL;DR — The safety story in one paragraph
PD safety is dominated by one issue: peritonitis. The peritoneum has no native bacterial flora, so any introduced organism has free run of the cavity. Peritonitis is the leading cause of PD dropout and a major mortality contributor. Every other PD safety issue — exit-site infection, catheter complications, mechanical issues (hernia, leak, hydrothorax), glycemic load from dextrose-based solutions, intra-abdominal hypertension from over-fill — is meaningfully less common but still requires structured monitoring. The institutional goal is peritonitis rate < 0.4 episodes per patient-year. Aggressive aseptic technique training and exit-site care are the most cost-effective interventions.

02
Peritonitis — The Defining Complication
Recognition, treatment, prevention

Peritonitis presents with the triad: cloudy effluent, abdominal pain, fever. Cloudy effluent alone — even without pain — should prompt immediate evaluation. ISPD defines peritonitis as ≥ 2 of: typical clinical features, effluent WBC > 100/µL with > 50% neutrophils, or positive effluent culture.

Organism categoryTypical sourceEmpiric coverage
Gram-positive (S. aureus, coag-neg Staph, Strep)Skin/touch contaminationVancomycin or cefazolin IP (intraperitoneal)
Gram-negative (E. coli, Klebsiella, Pseudomonas)Bowel translocation; constipationCeftazidime or aminoglycoside IP
Fungal (Candida)Prior antibiotic courses; immunosuppressedCatheter removal mandatory; IV antifungal
PolymicrobialBowel perforationSurgical consult; broad-spectrum IV + IP

🔑
Catheter removal indicationsRemove the catheter for: refractory bacterial peritonitis (no improvement by day 5), relapsing peritonitis, fungal peritonitis, mycobacterial peritonitis, bowel perforation, recurrent peritonitis with the same organism, severe exit-site/tunnel infection.
⚠
The 0.4 / patient-year targetISPD recommends < 0.4 episodes per patient-year. Units exceeding 0.5 should audit their training, exchange technique, and exit-site protocols. Repeat peritonitis predicts technique failure and modality change.
03
Aseptic Technique — The Non-Negotiable
Every connection matters

The peritoneum has no native bacterial flora. Every introduced organism has free run of the cavity. Aseptic technique at every connection is the most cost-effective prevention.

  • Dedicated, clean room for exchanges. No pets, no open windows during exchange (pet exposure linked to Pasteurella peritonitis outbreaks)
  • Hand hygiene: surgical scrub ≥ 1 minute with antimicrobial soap, dry with single-use paper towel, then alcohol-based hand rub
  • Surgical mask on patient AND caregiver during connection — droplets from speech documented source of S. salivarius peritonitis
  • Sterile field: disinfect work surface; assemble all supplies before opening the catheter
  • Flush-before-fill: drain the bag's first ~100 mL through the drain line before infusing — clears any contaminant introduced at connection
  • Daily exit-site care: cleansing with chlorhexidine or povidone-iodine; mupirocin or gentamicin cream prophylactically
  • Catheter handling: never reuse a transfer set; replace per institutional schedule; always cap when disconnected
  • No sharps near catheter (e.g., scissors); no fingers in the bag opening; avoid touch contamination of the spike
⚠
The cardinal rule of PD nursingWhen in doubt, don't connect. If sterility is compromised, a new bag and transfer set cost less than a peritonitis admission. Document and replace.
04
Exit Site & Catheter Care
Daily inspection, prophylactic antibiotics

Exit-site infection (ESI) is a precursor to tunnel infection and peritonitis. Daily inspection and prompt treatment of ESI are central to peritonitis prevention.

FindingSeverityResponse
Clean, dry, no rednessNormalContinue daily care
Mild crusting, no erythemaMinor / equivocalIncrease cleansing frequency; monitor
Erythema, pain, drainageExit-site infectionCulture; empiric antibiotics per ISPD; weekly follow-up
Tunnel tenderness, indurationTunnel infectionAggressive antibiotic course; possibly catheter removal
Erythema + peritonitisSevereCatheter removal usually required
🔑
Mupirocin / gentamicin prophylaxisDaily exit-site application of mupirocin or gentamicin cream reduces ESI and peritonitis rates. Most ISPD-aligned centers use one of these routinely.

06
Glycemic & Metabolic Concerns
The dextrose load over time

Dextrose-based PD solutions deliver glucose continuously. A patient on 4.25% bags daily absorbs 100–200 g of glucose per day, contributing to insulin resistance, weight gain, dyslipidemia, and accelerated peritoneal membrane fibrosis.

  • Minimize 4.25% (red bag) use — reserve for true UF emergencies; use 2.5% (green) as workhorse
  • Icodextrin for long dwell — colloid osmotic, minimal glucose absorption; once-daily limit
  • HbA1c monitoring in diabetics; insulin needs increase over PD course
  • Weight surveillance; nutritional counseling
  • Lipid panel every 6 months
  • Long-term: consider biocompatible (low-GDP) solutions to preserve peritoneal membrane
⚠
Finger-stick glucose interferenceIcodextrin (Extraneal · purple bag) can falsely elevate finger-stick glucose readings with certain meters (those using GDH-PQQ enzyme). Use glucose-specific meters (GDH-FAD, GDH-NAD, glucose oxidase) for icodextrin patients to avoid spurious hyperglycemia and inappropriate insulin dosing.

Fill Safety — Weight-Based Ceilings
Adult 30–40 mL/kg; pediatric 800–1100 mL/m²

Over-filling produces intra-abdominal hypertension with respiratory compromise, hypotension, hernia / leak risk, and patient discomfort. The calculator's fill safety check uses weight (adults) or BSA (pediatrics).

🔑
Patient-tolerance testEven within numeric guidelines, individuals vary. Listen to the patient: any reproducible abdominal pain, respiratory tightness, or back pain at full fill should prompt a fill-volume reduction trial.
08
Pediatric PD
BSA-based fills, low-fill cyclers, family education
  • BSA-based fill targets (800–1100 mL/m²) for > 10 kg children; weight-based for smaller
  • Low-fill cycler mode for fills under ~500 mL
  • Start with shorter dwells (45–60 min) and scale up
  • Family training is the most important intervention — peritonitis rates correlate with caregiver technique
  • Avoid icodextrin in infants (insufficient data; acid-base concerns)
  • Fluoroscopy-guided catheter placement to verify position
  • Daily fill tolerance check: watch for respiratory distress, abdominal distention, vomiting
  • School integration — schedule overnight cycler around school hours
09
Emergency Response
When something goes wrong
⚠ Suspected Peritonitis (Cloudy Effluent / Pain / Fever)
  1. Drain a full bag for diagnostic sampling.
  2. Send effluent for cell count, differential, Gram stain, and culture.
  3. Start empiric IP antibiotics immediately (don't wait for culture) — vancomycin + ceftazidime per ISPD protocol.
  4. Investigate source: recent connection technique, exit-site exam, recent constipation / diarrhea.
  5. Daily clinical reassessment; repeat cell count in 48–72 hr.
  6. If no improvement by day 5: consider catheter removal.
⚠ Acute Respiratory Distress (Over-Fill or Hydrothorax)
  1. Drain peritoneum immediately — empties the cavity.
  2. O₂ via non-rebreather; consider BiPAP if severe.
  3. If over-fill: reduce future fill volumes; consider lower-fill mode.
  4. If hydrothorax suspected: chest film, thoracentesis (high-glucose effluent confirms), surgical consult for pleurodesis.
⚠ Bowel Perforation
  1. Recognize: sudden severe abdominal pain, feculent effluent, polymicrobial growth, hemodynamic instability.
  2. STOP all dialysate; drain peritoneum.
  3. Surgical consult emergent; CT abdomen.
  4. Broad-spectrum IV antibiotics (covering anaerobes); fluid resuscitation.
  5. Catheter removal usually required.
