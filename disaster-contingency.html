Disaster & Contingency
Power outage, water failure, equipment malfunction, mass casualty surge, evacuation. Applies to every DART modality. Compiled from CMS Emergency Preparedness rule (42 CFR 482.15 / 494.62), ESRD Network disaster guidance, AAMI standards, and Joint Commission emergency management requirements.

TL;DR — Plan Now, Respond Calmly
Every extracorporeal therapy depends on power, water, and intact equipment. When any of these fails — even briefly — patients connected to circuits are at immediate risk. CMS Emergency Preparedness requirements mandate written, drilled, and revised disaster plans for every dialysis facility and hospital. This reference summarizes the universal contingency principles: safe patient disconnection before the situation becomes critical, transparent communication, and rehearsed muscle memory. Plans are not effective unless drilled — schedule disaster drills at least annually and document.
01
Emergency Preparedness Framework
CMS, Joint Commission, ESRD Network requirements

Federal requirements provide the framework:

  • CMS Emergency Preparedness Rule (42 CFR Part 482.15 hospitals; 494.62 ESRD facilities) — Each facility must have a written all-hazards emergency preparedness plan, communication plan, training and testing program, and policies and procedures.
  • Joint Commission Emergency Management Standards — Hazard Vulnerability Assessment (HVA) annually; emergency operations plan covering 6 functional areas; drills twice annually (one community-wide).
  • ESRD Network Disaster Coordination — Regional network coordinates patient relocation, dialysis access, and resource sharing during natural disasters. Patients should have a Disaster Card with prescription, allergies, and emergency contact.
  • Manufacturer device-failure protocols — Each device manufacturer provides emergency-stop and manual-disconnect procedures.
🔑
The Three-Question TestWhenever something goes wrong, ask in order: (1) Is the patient safe right now? (2) Can the patient be safely disconnected? (3) Can therapy continue or be transferred? If the answer to #1 is no, disconnect immediately — do not attempt to save the procedure first.

⚠ Power Outage Response — In Order
  1. Stay calm. Verify what failed — single device, single station, whole unit, whole facility?
  2. Check generator status. Most institutional generators activate within 10 seconds. Verify red outlets receiving power.
  3. If generator power: continue therapy. Document the event time; monitor closely.
  4. If no generator power within 1 minute: assess each patient. Hemodynamic stability, current procedure phase.
  5. Manual disconnection per device protocol. See Section 5 of this reference for general principles; specific procedure varies by device.
  6. Document outage start, response, and outcome. Incident report; review at next QI meeting.
⚠
Generator TestingGenerator and UPS testing per facility schedule (typically monthly). Document test outcomes. Any test failure is an immediate maintenance issue; verify red outlets and life-safety circuits hold patient-critical equipment.
03
Water Failure / Treatment System Failure (HD)

Hemodialysis depends entirely on AAMI-grade water. Water system failure — RO failure, chlorine breakthrough, distribution loop contamination — must trigger immediate cessation.

⚠ Water Failure Response
  1. Stop initiating new treatments. Hold all incoming patients.
  2. Assess in-progress treatments. Can they be safely completed if the issue is short-term (e.g., brief chlorine breakthrough resolved by switching tanks)?
  3. If chlorine/chloramine breakthrough confirmed: Stop all treatments immediately. Patient blood exposure to dialysate with chloramine causes acute hemolysis.
  4. Rinseback per protocol; disconnect patients.
  5. Stat labs on exposed patients: CBC, K, free hemoglobin, LDH, ABG. Monitor for hemolysis.
  6. Notify medical director and water treatment technician.
  7. Document and report as a sentinel event per institutional policy.
  8. Resume treatment only after water quality verified acceptable per AAMI standards.
🔑
Chlorine Breakthrough = Stop NowChloramine and chlorine cause immediate hemolysis. Even brief exposure can cause hyperkalemia, AKI, and death. The carbon tank test is the canary; act on it without delay.
04
Device Malfunction

Device failure modes are varied: pump failure, alarm system malfunction, sensor failure, circuit rupture, leak. The response framework is consistent:

  1. Recognize. Listen for alarm; observe what's different; check vital signs.
  2. Stabilize the patient. If hemodynamic concern, treat first; document concurrent device issue.
  3. Pause the device per manufacturer instructions. Do not attempt repair while running.
  4. Disconnect if safe to do so — see Section 5.
  5. Notify biomed / equipment technician for device assessment; tag out of service; substitute another machine if available.
  6. Document the failure mode — exact alarm, time, patient state, machine serial; report to manufacturer per MedWatch / equipment-failure surveillance.
  7. Reschedule if needed; coordinate with team.
05
Manual Disconnection — Universal Principles
Safe patient separation from a non-functioning circuit

Manual disconnection is sometimes necessary when normal rinseback or termination procedures cannot be performed. The principles are universal across modalities.

  1. Clamp the patient access lines first — venous return line and arterial / inlet line both clamped to prevent further blood exchange.
  2. Clamp the circuit lines at the patient access — preventing back-flow of circuit fluid into the patient line.
  3. Disconnect at the catheter or access hub using aseptic technique (mask, sterile gloves where possible).
  4. Cap the catheter hub with sterile cap or apply sterile flush per access protocol.
  5. Apply pressure to peripheral access sites; secure central catheter dressing.
  6. Document patient stability — vital signs, symptoms, blood loss estimate.
  7. Discard the circuit per institutional waste protocol.
  8. Plan re-initiation when conditions allow.
⚠
Estimated Blood LossCircuit blood loss varies by device: TPE/RBCx circuit ~185 mL + warmer 50 mL; HD circuit ~120–190 mL; CRRT circuit ~150–250 mL; CELLEX ~240 mL. Document this estimate after manual disconnection — particularly important for pediatric patients and anemic adults.
06
Mass Casualty / Surge Planning

Mass casualty events (natural disasters, mass shootings, infectious surge) can create sudden demand for renal replacement therapy that exceeds capacity. Pre-planning is essential.

  • Pre-identify surge capacity — Maximum patients per shift; staffing required; equipment available; consumable inventory.
  • Triage protocol — Categorize patients by need (life-threatening hyperkalemia first; chronic missed sessions second; routine maintenance last).
  • Modify schedule — Run extended hours; reduce session time from 4 hr to 2.5–3 hr; increase frequency where possible.
  • Coordinate with ESRD Network — Regional patient relocation; sharing resources across facilities.
  • Plan for chronic patients from other facilities — Many displaced patients during natural disasters; receive their prescription, verify access, treat.
  • Document and report — Both NHSN and ESRD Network track surge events.
07
Patient Evacuation

Evacuation may be required for fire, severe weather, infrastructure failure, or security event. Patients connected to extracorporeal circuits add complexity.

  1. Activate facility evacuation protocol.
  2. Triage patients — Connected (highest priority for safe disconnect), ambulatory (assist as needed), critical (medical evacuation).
  3. Disconnect patients per Section 5 as quickly as is safe.
  4. Move patients per the institutional plan — Stretcher, wheelchair, walking; designated routes; designated meeting area.
  5. Bring critical supplies — Patient identifiers, chart access (paper backup), portable oxygen, emergency medications.
  6. Account for all patients at the assembly point; report to incident command.
  7. Coordinate continuation of care — Transfer to alternate facility; plan resumption.
08
Communication Chain

Effective response requires clear, rehearsed communication.

  • First responder at the bedside notifies charge nurse immediately.
  • Charge nurse activates appropriate response: rapid response, code, security, infection prevention, biomed.
  • Charge nurse notifies medical director for procedural or facility-level decisions.
  • Hospital incident command activated for facility-wide events per institutional policy.
  • External notification — Police, fire, FBI, public health, ESRD Network as appropriate.
  • Family notification per chaplaincy / social work / institutional protocol for major events.
  • Media communication via public affairs only; staff do not speak to media directly.
  • Documentation by all involved as soon as practical; incident report; sentinel event review if applicable.
🔑
Drill, Drill, DrillDisaster plans only work if they're rehearsed. CMS requires twice-annual drills (one community-wide); Joint Commission requires HVA review and drill cycles. Schedule, conduct, debrief, and revise — and document every drill.
