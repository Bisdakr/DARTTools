Pediatric Apheresis

TL;DR — Small Patients, Big Stakes
Pediatric apheresis introduces a series of physical and physiological constraints that demand careful calculation and team coordination. The single most important parameter is extracorporeal volume (ECV) as a fraction of total blood volume — when circuit + warmer exceeds 10–15% of TBV, blood priming is generally indicated. TBV is age-banded (90 mL/kg neonate, 80 mL/kg infant, 70 mL/kg child, 65 mL/kg adolescent). Sedation, PICU coordination, child-life support, and family-centered care are integral. FACT-JACIE accreditation requires pediatric-specific protocols for any pediatric collection program.
01
Core Pediatric Principles
  • Always calculate TBV first. Use age-appropriate band (see Section 2).
  • Calculate ECV. Sum of circuit volume + warmer + access tubing. Spectra Optia disposable ~185 mL; warmer ~50 mL; CRRT circuit 60–150 mL depending on size; Therakos CELLEX ~240 mL.
  • ECV as % TBV is the key decision metric. > 10–15% → blood prime; > 20% → potentially life-threatening without prime.
  • Slow ramp-up of inlet flow. Reduces hemodynamic perturbation in small patients.
  • Hemodynamic monitoring is more intensive than in adults — arterial line common; continuous SpO₂; warming.
  • Replacement fluid choice — Albumin vs. plasma vs. albumin/plasma blend depends on indication and bleeding/coagulation risk.
  • Citrate tolerance is lower — Pediatric patients more prone to hypocalcemia; reduce inlet rate, calcium chloride infusion via second line, monitor ionized calcium more frequently.
  • Temperature management — Small patients lose heat quickly; warmer, blankets, ambient temperature.
  • Pediatric intensivist / specialist team involvement for any complex pediatric procedure.
  • FACT-JACIE pediatric program standards apply when collecting stem cells from pediatric donors / recipients.

Use Actual Body WeightFor obese patients, use ideal body weight or adjusted body weight per institutional protocol — using actual weight overestimates TBV in obesity. For lean patients, actual weight is appropriate. Pediatric Boyd / Nadler-equivalent formulas are also accepted.

Calculating ECV CorrectlyECV = disposable kit volume + warmer + return-line dead space. For Spectra Optia TPE/RBCx: ~185 mL + 50 mL warmer = ~235 mL. For Therakos CELLEX: ~240 mL. For CRRT (Prismaflex M60 set): ~93 mL. Verify against IFU for your specific kit and warmer.
04
Blood Prime Procedure
  1. Calculate prime volume. Equal to ECV. Typically 1 unit RBC = ~250–300 mL; partial unit for smaller circuits.
  2. Order blood product. Leukoreduced, irradiated (if immunocompromised), CMV-safe per institutional policy. Type-and-crossmatch.
  3. Verify product per institutional blood administration check (2 RN verification).
  4. Prime the circuit with the RBC product per device IFU. Spectra Optia has a specific blood-prime workflow.
  5. Calcium and electrolyte considerations: Stored blood is high in K+, citrate, glucose; low in calcium. Buffer the prime per institutional pediatric protocol (e.g., calcium addition, washed RBC for hyperkalemia risk).
  6. Warmer ON — Cold prime exacerbates hypothermia.
  7. Slow inlet ramp-up.
  8. Monitor: ionized calcium, K+, glucose, hematocrit, SpO₂, BP, HR.
  9. Rinseback strategy at end of procedure — May give back the blood prime to the patient if hemoglobin transfusion is also desired; otherwise discard per protocol.
🧪
Washed vs. Unwashed Blood PrimeFor very small patients or those at hyperkalemia risk (renal failure, hemolysis-prone), use washed RBC for prime — removes accumulated K+, citrate, and supernatant. Adds processing time; coordinate with blood bank.
05
Vascular Access in Children
  • Peripheral access: Often inadequate for apheresis; small veins, low flow.
  • Central catheter sizing: Match catheter diameter to patient size — 6.5–8 Fr neonates; 8 Fr infants; 10 Fr children; 11.5–13.5 Fr adolescents.
  • Catheter type: Non-tunneled for short courses; tunneled cuffed for longer; port less ideal due to flow limitations for apheresis.
  • Site selection: Internal jugular preferred; femoral for short-term acute use; subclavian for stenosis-avoiding (especially future HD access patients).
  • Ultrasound guidance mandatory for pediatric central access.
  • Sedation / general anesthesia for insertion in younger children.
  • Catheter care: Same CLABSI prevention principles; daily necessity review especially important.
06
Sedation & PICU Coordination
  • Coordinate with pediatric anesthesia / sedation team for procedures requiring sedation.
  • Sedation choices: Minimal sedation for cooperative older children; moderate sedation (midazolam, ketamine) for school-age; general anesthesia for younger children, especially when prolonged immobility required.
  • Fasting requirements per anesthesia (NPO 6 hr solids, 2 hr clear liquids).
  • PICU setting for blood prime procedures, neonatal apheresis, high ECV ratio, or compromised patient.
  • Continuous monitoring per pediatric ICU standards: ECG, SpO₂, BP, end-tidal CO₂ for sedated.
  • Anesthesia recovery per institutional pediatric protocol.
07
Neonatal Considerations & CARPEDIEM
  • Standard CRRT circuits often unsuitable for neonates < 8 kg due to ECV / TBV ratio > 30%.
  • CARPEDIEM (Cardio-Renal Pediatric Dialysis Emergency Machine) — small-volume CRRT device specifically designed for infants (1–10 kg); ECV ~30 mL; FDA-approved for pediatric use in the US.
  • NIDUS — Newcastle Infant Dialysis and Ultrafiltration System; another small-patient option (UK).
  • Aquadex — Slow continuous ultrafiltration; useful for fluid overload without solute clearance need.
  • Neonatal apheresis (TPE, exchange transfusion): Specialized; coordinated with neonatology; usually in NICU.
  • RBCx for severe neonatal jaundice / hemolytic disease — Single-volume or two-volume exchange transfusion per OB / neonatology.
  • Temperature regulation paramount — Warmer, isolette, swaddling.

Modality-Specific Pediatric Notes
ModalityPediatric-Specific
TPEMost common indications: TTP, HUS (atypical), neuromyologic disease (GBS, MG, NMOSD), severe sickle cell crisis with neurological sequelae, hyperviscosity. Replacement: albumin standard; FFP for TTP / coagulopathy. Citrate vigilance.
RBCxSickle cell — acute chest, stroke prevention, priapism, surgery preparation. Pediatric Hb S target < 30% (often more stringent than adult). Calculate FCR carefully for pediatric weights.
HPC-A (Stem Cell Collection)Mobilization with G-CSF (filgrastim 10–16 μg/kg/day × 4–5 d). Goal CD34+ cells/kg recipient weight. Adolescent donor / sibling donor protocols; consent considerations; FACT-JACIE pediatric program standards.
LeukapheresisHyperleukocytic AML / ALL with leukostasis: WBC > 100k with symptoms. CMNC procedure on Optia. Premature infants rarely candidates.
ECP (Therakos)Pediatric GVHD; offline preferred; CELLEX adapted for pediatric volumes. Methoxsalen pediatric dosing per weight.
HDPediatric-specific dialyzers (smaller surface area, lower priming volume); pediatric BFR (3–5 mL/kg/min); KDIGO pediatric guidelines. AVF possible in adolescents; catheter in younger.
PDOften preferred for infants and small children: home-based, no vascular access, less hemodynamic perturbation. ISPD pediatric guideline. Cycler dwell and fill volumes scaled to BSA. PD catheter placement by pediatric surgery.
CRRTPediatric circuits (Prismaflex HF20); CARPEDIEM for < 10 kg. Blood prime required for most pediatric circuits. CARPEDIEM ECV ~30 mL.
LDL ApheresisHoFH pediatric protocols; start as early as age 5–8 yr per pediatric lipidology. Lower volumes; child-friendly setting; family education.
09
Family-Centered Care & Child Life
  • Family presence: Allow / encourage during procedures per institutional family-centered care policy.
  • Child life specialist involvement: preparation, distraction, coping strategies, post-procedure debriefing.
  • Age-appropriate communication — Concrete language for younger children; honest discussion of sensations.
  • Consent / assent: Parental consent + age-appropriate assent from the child (typically formal assent from age 7+).
  • School / activity considerations — Coordinate with school nurse; minimize disruption; consider home / community resources.
  • Long-term support: Pediatric chronic disease patients need ongoing psychosocial support; refer to chronic disease support services.
  • Sibling support: Often overlooked; offer child-life resources.
🔑
Transition to Adult CareAdolescents with chronic apheresis / dialysis needs require structured transition to adult services typically beginning at age 14–16. Coordinate with adult nephrology / hematology / specialty team well in advance of transfer.
