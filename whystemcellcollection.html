Why HPC-A?
From standard autologous SCT to gene-therapy collection for sickle cell: how CD34 mobilization, CMNC apheresis, and the new manufacturing era come together at the bedside.

TL;DR — The 60-second version
Hematopoietic progenitor cell apheresis (HPC-A) collects CD34+ stem cells from peripheral blood for autologous or allogeneic transplant — and increasingly, for gene therapy and CAR-T cell manufacturing. The patient or donor is first mobilized (chemo + G-CSF, plerixafor, or both) to drive CD34+ cells out of the bone marrow into circulation. The Optia's Continuous Mononuclear Cell (CMNC) protocol then runs for 4–6 hours, processing 2–3 blood volumes through a centrifuge that selectively skims the mononuclear cell layer. Targets vary by indication: 2–5 × 10⁶ CD34/kg for autologous SCT, 8–10 for tandem, ≥ 15 for sickle-cell gene therapy.

01
How CMNC Works
Skimming the buffy coat at scale

Normal peripheral blood contains almost no CD34+ stem cells — they live in bone marrow niches, anchored to stromal cells via CXCR4-SDF1 binding. The whole apheresis project depends on first mobilizing them out of the marrow and into the bloodstream where the Optia can collect them.

The Optia's Continuous Mononuclear Cell (CMNC) program is software-optimized for this collection. Blood enters the centrifuge at 50–90 mL/min with ACD-A anticoagulant at 12:1 ratio. The centrifuge spins down the layers: RBCs (densest) to the outside, then a thin buffy coat (mononuclear cells + platelets) at the interface, plasma at the inside. The CMNC software actively tracks the buffy coat position via optical sensor and skims it into the collection bag, returning everything else to the patient.

The collected product is dilute and yellow-tinged — mostly plasma with concentrated mononuclear cells. Per session you collect roughly 200–400 mL of product containing the target CD34+ cells, plus a lot of passenger T-cells, monocytes, and platelets that the cell processing lab will manage downstream.

💡
CE2 — what it measuresCollection Efficiency 2 = CD34 collected ÷ CD34 available in the volume processed. Typical CE2 ranges 40–60% for healthy donors and post-G-CSF-mobilized autologous patients. CE2 depends on technique, settings, and biology — not all CD34+ cells in the circulating pool get captured. The DART calculator shows the predicted CD34 yield as a range using 40% and 60% CE so the operator sees the realistic span.
02
Mobilization Strategies
Getting CD34+ out of the marrow

Mobilization is the strategy of forcing CD34+ cells out of the bone marrow stroma into peripheral blood. Four common approaches, each with mechanism and indication:

MethodMechanismTypical use
G-CSF aloneCytokine — drives myeloid expansion, CXCR4 downregulationHealthy allo donors, low-risk autologous
G-CSF + PlerixaforCXCR4 antagonist disrupts CD34-stroma bindingPoor mobilizers, MM, NHL, tandem-target patients
Chemo + G-CSFCyclophosphamide rebound mobilizationLymphoma patients where chemo is also clinically indicated
Plerixafor monotherapyCXCR4 antagonist aloneSCD gene therapy (G-CSF contraindicated)
🚫
G-CSF in sickle cell disease — dangerousGranulocyte colony-stimulating factor (G-CSF, Neupogen / filgrastim) triggers vaso-occlusive crisis in patients with SCD — well-documented in case reports and a major reason gene therapy collections require a different mobilization approach. For SCD gene therapy collections, mobilization is plerixafor monotherapy (0.24 mg/kg SQ ~6 hours before each apheresis day) with HbS < 30% pre-collection achieved via RBCx.

The pre-apheresis CD34 count (cells/µL) drawn the morning of collection is the most predictive single number. ≥ 20/µL predicts a productive day; 10–19/µL is marginal — consider plerixafor add-on; < 10/µL is a poor mobilizer that may need remobilization with a different strategy.

The CMNC software handles the active interface tracking. The operator's job is steady inlet flow, patient monitoring (especially calcium symptoms), watching the collection product accumulate, and being ready to adjust collection preference when the message bar reads "Collecting MNC."

For Bluebird Bio / Genetix SCD gene-therapy collections, the protocol shifts: AC ratio drops to 8:1 (sometimes 6:1 if clumping), collection preference goes to DARK (the Optia color tool), and the AIR ceiling effectively forces lower inlet flow — meaning total whole blood processed in the same time period drops.

04
Indications
Autologous, allogeneic, and the new "manufacturing" category

Autologous collections store the patient's own cells for rescue after high-dose chemotherapy. Classic indications: multiple myeloma (single or tandem ASCT), relapsed lymphoma (NHL, Hodgkin), germ cell tumors, autoimmune disorders.

Allogeneic collections come from related (sibling) or unrelated (matched URD) donors. Used for AML, ALL, MDS, severe aplastic anemia, certain inherited bone marrow failure syndromes, hemoglobinopathies when transplant is appropriate.

Manufacturing collections are the new and rapidly growing category — collected cells are sent to a manufacturer for gene editing (Casgevy/Lyfgenia for SCD/TDT) or CAR-T construction (Yescarta, Kymriah, Abecma, Breyanzi, Tecartus). These collections often have unique requirements: specific bag specs, identifier chains, plasma volume in the product, manufacturer-specific TBV processing protocols. The DART CMNC calculator surfaces these in the company-dropdown info card.

05
CD34 Yield Math
Why the calculator looks the way it does
📐
The yield formulaTotal CD34 collected (×10⁶) = (Pre-CD34 [/µL] × Whole blood processed [mL] × CE) ÷ 1000

Where:
• Pre-CD34 = morning-of CD34 cell count (the predictive number)
• Whole blood processed = Total Inlet − AC volume = inlet × time × (acd − 1)/acd
• CE = Collection Efficiency, typically 0.40–0.60 (DART shows the range)

The arithmetic is simple but the inputs are dynamic. Pre-CD34 is measured the morning of each session. Whole blood processed is determined by inlet flow, total time (max 8 hours typically), and AC ratio (tighter AC ratios mean more AC per unit inlet, less whole blood). CE varies by patient, technique, and slightly with collection-preference setting.

To estimate the per-kg dose, divide total by patient (or recipient) weight: 4.5 × 10⁶ ÷ 80 kg = 0.056 × 10⁶ CD34/kg. Most autologous targets sit between 2 and 5 × 10⁶ CD34/kg per transplant. Tandem ASCT planning multiplies that.

The DART calculator computes both the predicted CD34 yield AND the estimated collection bag volume (using interface establishment time + collect rate × duration). The cells-per-mL density is reported at the midpoint CE — useful for the cell processing lab.

06
The Gene Therapy Era
Why your apheresis center now talks to Genetix Biotherapeutics

FDA approvals of Casgevy (exa-cel) and Lyfgenia (lovo-cel) for sickle cell and TDT in December 2023 created a new kind of HPC-A collection — one where the product gets shipped to a manufacturer who edits the cells ex vivo and ships them back for infusion. This changes everything operationally.

🧬
SCD-specific protocol differencesHbS must be < 30% at collection (achieved via RBCx in the weeks prior). No G-CSF — plerixafor monotherapy at 0.24 mg/kg SQ ~6 hours pre-collection. Tight AC ratio (start 8:1, drop to 6:1 if clumping). DARK collection-preference — Genetix specifically recommends collecting on the darker end of the Optia color tool. Targets: ≥ 15 × 10⁶ CD34/kg for SCD, ≥ 25 × 10⁶ CD34/kg for TDT, plus an additional 1.5 × 10⁶/kg for back-up. The DART calculator integrates these as a Bluebird company selection with tooltips and warnings.

The labeling and chain-of-custody requirements are also strict. Each product bag has a patient-specific Apheresis ID that must be verified across the Optia screen, the bag label, the manufacturer's traceability portal, and the cell-processing lab manifest before the bag leaves your unit. A wrong sticker can disqualify the entire collection.

💭
A cell therapy supply chainThe patient's own cells become a manufactured drug product. Apheresis is the raw material harvest; the manufacturer does the editing; the patient gets the edited cells back as an infusion. Your unit is now part of a multi-week, multi-stakeholder, manufacturer-driven supply chain. Document everything.
07
CAR-T Collections
T-cells, not CD34

CAR-T therapy starts with collecting the patient's T-cells (not CD34+ stem cells) for ex vivo transduction with a chimeric antigen receptor that targets a tumor-specific antigen. The Optia protocol is similar but the target is different: instead of pre-CD34, you're driven by pre-CD3 (T-cell count) and the manufacturer's specific volume processing target.

09
2024–2025 Developments
Where HPC-A is going
🧬
Multi-product CAR-T pipelines2024 brought continued expansion of CAR-T products (autologous + allogeneic) across more cancers — MM relapsed/refractory, follicular and mantle cell lymphoma, ALL. Each new product means new collection-protocol nuances. The institutional apheresis team needs ongoing certification per product to maintain manufacturer registration.
🩸
Gene therapy for β-thalassemiaCasgevy/Lyfgenia for TDT approved in many jurisdictions. Targets are higher than SCD (≥ 25 × 10⁶ CD34/kg) because manufacturing yield is lower in TDT cells; collection often requires multiple cycles spanning weeks. Patient counseling about expected procedure burden is now a key pre-collection conversation.
⚡
Motixafortide — longer-acting CXCR4 inhibitorFDA-approved 2023; reduces day-of-collection plerixafor logistics and improves CD34 yields vs G-CSF + plerixafor in MM. Adoption growing, particularly at high-volume MM centers where collection day efficiency matters.
🤝
Allogeneic "off-the-shelf" CAR-TSeveral allogeneic CAR-T candidates (donor-derived, gene-edited to be hypoallergenic) are in late-phase trials. If approved, these would shift the apheresis workflow from patient-specific to donor-bank collections, dramatically reducing time-to-infusion for individual patients.
