TL;DR — Protect Yourself, Protect Patients
Apheresis and dialysis nursing carry meaningful occupational risks: bloodborne pathogen exposure, sharps injury, chemical exposure (bleach, peracetic acid, glutaraldehyde, citrate), musculoskeletal strain, and infectious disease transmission. The OSHA Bloodborne Pathogens Standard (29 CFR 1910.1030) and CDC PEP guidance establish the framework. The single most important principle: treat every patient's blood as if it carries an infection — universal precautions. If an exposure occurs, the response window is short; PEP for HIV must begin within 72 hours and ideally within 2 hours.
01
Standard Precautions — The Universal Default
CDC: treat all blood and body fluids as infectious

Standard Precautions apply to all patients regardless of suspected or confirmed infection status. They include:

  • Hand hygiene per the WHO 5 Moments (see CLABSI Prevention reference).
  • PPE selection based on anticipated exposure.
  • Respiratory hygiene / cough etiquette.
  • Safe injection practices — one needle, one syringe, one time.
  • Safe handling of contaminated equipment / linens.
  • Environmental cleaning — high-touch surfaces and patient zone after each session.
  • Sharps safety — engineered safety devices, no recapping.

Transmission-based precautions (Contact, Droplet, Airborne) are added on top of Standard Precautions when specific pathogens are suspected or confirmed.

🔑
Why "Universal" PrecautionsYou cannot reliably identify who carries hepatitis B, hepatitis C, HIV, or other bloodborne pathogens by appearance. Many patients are unaware they are infected. The default of treating every patient's blood as infectious protects everyone — patients and staff — from transmission.

Critical Apheresis / Dialysis Tasks — PPE Specific

  • Cannulating AVF / AVG: Gloves, mask, eye protection. Mask both patient and staff during cannulation in dialysis.
  • HD catheter connection / disconnection: Mask (patient + staff), sterile gloves, eye protection. Sterile field at hub.
  • Apheresis circuit connection / disconnection: Same as HD catheter. Plus gown if splash anticipated.
  • PD exchange / cycler connection: Mask (patient + staff), hand hygiene, gloves. Aseptic technique critical.
  • Dialyzer reprocessing (if performed): Gloves, gown, eye protection, respirator if peracetic acid; never bypass ventilation.
  • Spectra Optia setup / loading: Gloves; mask and gown if blood handling.
  • Therakos CELLEX setup / leukocyte handling: Gloves; mask and eye protection at connection / disconnection / methoxsalen handling.
  • Cleaning blood spill: Gloves, mask, eye protection, gown; 1:10 bleach (5,000 ppm sodium hypochlorite) per OSHA.
03
Sharps Safety
Engineering controls, work practices, post-injury
  • Engineered safety devices — Required by OSHA (Needlestick Safety and Prevention Act 2000). Self-retracting needles, blunt cannulae, sheathed fistula needles. Activate the safety mechanism immediately after use.
  • No recapping — Single-handed scoop technique only if absolutely required.
  • Sharps containers — Within arm's reach of point of use; replace at fill line (typically 2/3 full); never overfilled; never reach into.
  • Cannulation technique — Properly trained, well-lit, supported position, controlled patient.
  • Set-up safety — Have everything ready before patient contact; don't break sterility to retrieve forgotten supplies.
  • Glove change after suspected breach — Even a small needle puncture in the glove warrants change.
⚠
Highest-Risk StepsFistula needle removal, recapping (avoid), cleanup, equipment disassembly, garbage handling. Pause before these steps and verify sharps disposal is at hand.
04
Bloodborne Pathogen Exposure Response
Needlestick, splash, mucocutaneous exposure
⚠ Immediate Exposure Response (Within Minutes)
  1. Stop the procedure if safe to do so; stabilize the patient if not.
  2. Wash the exposed area immediately — soap and water for skin; saline or clean water for eye (irrigate > 15 minutes); rinse mouth for oral exposure. Do not scrub aggressively or apply caustic agents.
  3. Express bleeding gently from puncture wound; do not squeeze hard or apply caustics.
  4. Notify charge nurse and supervisor.
  5. Report to Employee Health Service / Occupational Health immediately; if after hours, the Emergency Department.
  6. Source patient identification — Notify the patient and obtain consent for source testing (HBsAg, anti-HCV, HIV Ag/Ab). Most states permit testing under specific employee-exposure provisions even without consent.
  7. Exposed worker baseline labs: HBsAg, anti-HBs, anti-HCV, HIV Ag/Ab.
  8. Decision-making with Occupational Health / ID: Need for PEP, vaccination booster, follow-up schedule.
  9. Complete incident report — Required by OSHA; tracked in Sharps Injury Log.

🔑
PEP Time Window Is ShortHIV PEP starting within 2 hours is far more effective than starting at 48–72 hours. Do not wait to "get the source result." If risk is high, start PEP empirically and stop later if source tests negative. Coordinate with on-call Occupational Health or ID.
⚠
Risk Stratification (CDC Estimates per Percutaneous Exposure)HIV: ~0.3% from infected source; ~0.09% from mucous membrane. Hep C: ~1.8%. Hep B: 6–30% in unvaccinated. The vaccine is the most powerful prevention available; ensure your series is complete and anti-HBs > 10 mIU/mL.

Safety Data Sheets (SDS): OSHA requires SDS for every chemical in the workplace, accessible to all staff. Know where the binder or electronic SDS database is located before you need it.

07
Ergonomics & Musculoskeletal Health

Apheresis and dialysis staff have high rates of musculoskeletal injury — back, shoulder, neck, wrist. Standing for long hours, repetitive bending, patient transfers, heavy bag handling, and prolonged cannulation positions contribute.

  • Patient positioning equipment: Use lift devices, slide sheets, and gait belts for patient transfers; never lift > 35 lbs alone per NIOSH.
  • Adjustable chairs / beds: Bring patient to comfortable working height; do not bend over for prolonged tasks.
  • Anti-fatigue mats at standing stations.
  • Rotate tasks — Avoid prolonged repetitive cannulation in the same shift; rotate with colleagues.
  • Stretching breaks every 1–2 hours during procedures.
  • Report MSK symptoms early — Early intervention prevents chronic injury.
  • Two-person assist for patient transfers, lifting dialysate bags, moving heavy equipment.
