TL;DR — Universal Prevention
CLABSI is preventable. The CDC and IDSA frameworks have demonstrated that bundled interventions — proper hand hygiene, full sterile barrier at insertion, chlorhexidine prep with adequate dry time, hub disinfection at every access, sterile dressing care, and daily assessment of catheter need — reduce CLABSI rates by 50–70% when implemented reliably. This reference applies to every DART modality that uses a central venous catheter: TPE, RBCx, HPC-A, leukapheresis, LDL apheresis, ECP, HD, CRRT. The most important nursing intervention is the one that doesn't appear in any procedure manual — active advocacy for catheter removal when it is no longer needed.

01
Why CLABSI Prevention Matters
The patient, the institution, and the nursing accountability

CLABSI (central line-associated bloodstream infection) is a serious, preventable hospital-acquired infection with significant patient and institutional impact:

  • Mortality: CLABSI is associated with attributable mortality of 12–25% in critically ill patients.
  • Length of stay: Each CLABSI extends hospitalization by an average of 7–14 days.
  • Cost: Each CLABSI episode adds approximately $46,000 to the cost of care (CDC estimate).
  • Catheter loss: CLABSI often requires catheter removal, complicating ongoing therapy and requiring re-insertion at a different site.
  • Reporting: CMS publicly reports CLABSI rates and ties them to reimbursement (Value-Based Purchasing, HAC Reduction Program); these are tracked publicly on Hospital Compare.
  • Cross-modality risk: DART patients often have catheters for prolonged periods (chronic HD/PD, chronic ECP, repeated apheresis). The longer the dwell, the higher the cumulative risk.
🔑
The Bundle Is the Floor, Not the CeilingThe CDC bundle is the minimum standard. High-performing units track sub-bundle compliance (e.g., chlorhexidine dry time, hub scrub seconds) and use audit-feedback cycles to drive continuous improvement. Zero CLABSI is the institutional aspiration; rates below the CDC benchmark are achievable.
02
Hand Hygiene — The Foundation
CDC 6-step technique, indications, audit

Hand hygiene is the single most important intervention to prevent healthcare-associated infections. The WHO "5 Moments" and CDC indications cover every patient encounter.

The 5 Moments for Hand Hygiene (WHO)

  1. Before touching the patient — Including before any patient contact, even pulling the curtain back.
  2. Before a clean / aseptic procedure — Before any catheter access, before sterile dressing change, before medication administration.
  3. After body fluid exposure risk — After contact with blood, secretions, mucous membranes, or non-intact skin.
  4. After touching the patient — When leaving the bedside, even if no apparent contamination.
  5. After touching patient surroundings — Bed rails, IV pole, dialysis machine, monitor, chart, anything in the patient zone.

Technique — Alcohol-Based Hand Rub (ABHR)

  1. Apply enough ABHR to cover all surfaces of both hands.
  2. Rub palms together.
  3. Right palm over left dorsum with interlaced fingers; vice versa.
  4. Palm to palm, fingers interlaced.
  5. Backs of fingers to opposing palms with fingers interlocked.
  6. Rotational rubbing of left thumb in right palm; vice versa.
  7. Rotational rubbing back and forth, fingers in palm.
  8. Allow hands to dry fully — at least 20–30 seconds.

Soap & Water — When Required

  • Visibly soiled hands — ABHR not sufficient; use soap and water.
  • After caring for a patient with C. difficile or norovirus — alcohol does not kill C. diff spores; mechanical washing required.
  • Before eating, after restroom use — Routine hygiene.
  • Technique: Wet hands, apply soap, vigorous friction all surfaces ≥ 20 seconds, rinse thoroughly, dry with single-use towel, use towel to turn off faucet.
⚠
Bare Below the Elbows (Where Adopted)Many institutions and most ICUs / dialysis units adopt the "Bare Below the Elbows" practice — no long sleeves, rings (other than plain band), bracelets, watches, or artificial nails during direct patient care. These items harbor pathogens and impede effective hand hygiene. Verify your institution's policy.
03
PPE Selection — Match the Task
Gloves, gown, mask, eye protection, N95

PPE selection is task-based. The principle is: anticipate the exposure, select PPE to prevent it, don and doff in the correct sequence.

Standard PPE Inventory

🧤
Gloves
Required for any catheter contact, blood, body fluids, mucous membranes, non-intact skin. Change between patients and between tasks on same patient. Sterile gloves for sterile procedures.
😷
Surgical Mask
Required for sterile catheter access, central line insertion / dressing change, and droplet precautions. Both patient and provider mask during catheter connection in apheresis / dialysis.
🥽
Eye Protection
Required whenever blood or body fluid splash is possible — initial catheter connection, disconnection, dressing change with active bleeding, emergency procedures. Goggles or face shield.
🥼
Gown
Required if splash anticipated, contact precautions, sterile dressing change, or per institutional policy. Sterile gown for central line insertion. Fluid-resistant for splash risk.
🛡️
N95 Respirator
Required for airborne precautions (TB, measles, varicella, COVID-19 per institutional policy). Fit-tested annually. Not a substitute for surgical mask in droplet precautions.
👕
Cap
Required for sterile procedures (full sterile barrier at central line insertion). Covers all hair.

PPE by Task — Quick Reference

TaskMinimum PPE
Central line insertion (sterile)Full sterile barrier: cap, mask, sterile gown, sterile gloves, large sterile drape. Patient masked. Hand hygiene + chlorhexidine prep.
Catheter hub access (apheresis, HD, CRRT)Hand hygiene, mask (patient + provider), sterile gloves, eye protection if splash possible.
Dressing changeHand hygiene, mask (patient + provider), sterile gloves, fluid-resistant gown if drainage present, eye protection if splash anticipated.
Routine catheter inspection (no manipulation)Hand hygiene, gloves.
Apheresis procedure initiationHand hygiene, mask, sterile gloves at the connection step, eye protection. Gown if splash anticipated or per institutional policy.
Disconnection / rinseback completionHand hygiene, mask, gloves, eye protection.
Patient cleanup / linen changeGloves; gown if soiled linen.

Droplet precautions (influenza, RSV)Surgical mask within 3–6 feet of patient. Plus standard precautions.
Airborne precautions (TB, COVID-19, measles)N95 respirator (fit-tested), negative-pressure room, eye protection, gown, gloves.

Don and Doff Sequence (CDC)

Don (put on): 1. Gown → 2. Mask/respirator → 3. Goggles/face shield → 4. Gloves (over gown cuffs).

Doff (remove): 1. Gloves → 2. Goggles/face shield (handle by headband or earpieces) → 3. Gown (peel from inside) → 4. Mask/respirator (handle by straps). Hand hygiene after each step.

⚠
PPE Failure PointsMost PPE-related exposures occur during doffing, not donning. Contaminated outer surfaces of gloves and gown are the highest risk. Doff slowly, in the correct order, with hand hygiene between each step. Practice with infection prevention until automatic.
04
Central Line Insertion Bundle
The non-negotiables at the point of insertion

The CDC central line insertion bundle has demonstrated CLABSI reductions of 50–70% when implemented reliably. Every element matters.

CDC Insertion Bundle (verified at each insertion)
  • Hand hygiene with ABHR or soap/water before donning sterile attire.
  • Maximum sterile barrier precautions — full body sterile drape on patient; cap, mask, sterile gown, sterile gloves on inserter; cap and mask on all in the procedure area; assistants gowned and gloved sterile.
  • Chlorhexidine 2% in 70% alcohol skin prep with appropriate scrub (typically 30 seconds for non-greasy site, 2 minutes for greasy site) and ≥ 30 second dry time before puncture.
  • Optimal site selection — Subclavian preferred for CLABSI prevention; femoral avoided when possible; ultrasound guidance reduces complications.
  • Daily review of catheter necessity — Begins at insertion as a documented expectation.
  • Insertion checklist with stop-the-line authority — Any team member can halt the procedure if bundle elements are missed.
🔑
Chlorhexidine Dry TimeThe single most commonly skipped element. Chlorhexidine kills via slow, residual action — wiping it off before it dries cuts efficacy by > 80%. Watch the prep dry before puncturing. Set a 30-second timer if needed.
05
Hub Access Technique — "Scrub the Hub"
Every connection, every time

Every catheter hub access is a potential infection entry point. The CDC's "scrub the hub" guidance is built around the chlorhexidine/alcohol contact time and friction needed to disinfect the connector surface.

Standard Hub Access Sequence

  1. Hand hygiene. Per Section 02.
  2. Don mask (both patient and provider for apheresis/HD catheter access).
  3. Don sterile gloves.
  4. Vigorously scrub the hub / needleless connector with an alcoholic chlorhexidine (preferred) or 70% alcohol pad for at least 5 seconds using friction. Cover the top and threaded sides of the connector.
  5. Allow to dry fully — at least 5 seconds; do not wipe off.
  6. Connect or aspirate. Use sterile saline syringe or apheresis/HD line per protocol.
  7. Repeat scrub for every subsequent access — even if scrubbing the same connector minutes apart, re-scrub before each access.

Common Errors

  • Insufficient scrub time — Most providers scrub for < 3 seconds when audited; the standard is ≥ 5 seconds with friction.
  • Wiping without friction — Friction is what disrupts biofilm; gentle wiping is insufficient.
  • Not allowing to dry — Same principle as skin chlorhexidine — residual action requires drying.
  • Skipping the scrub for "quick" accesses — Every access requires the scrub, including saline flushes and blood draws.
  • Re-using alcohol pads — Single-use only.
👁
Disinfection CapsPassive disinfection caps (e.g., SwabCap, Curos) that contain isopropyl alcohol can be applied to needleless connectors when not in active use. They eliminate the need for active scrub before next access (per manufacturer instructions — typically 1 minute contact time), but only when the cap has been in place continuously. Replace after each access.
06
Dressing & Site Care
Inspection, change schedule, CHG-impregnated dressings
  1. Daily exit-site inspection — Look for erythema, swelling, drainage, tenderness; palpate gently. Document.
  2. Dressing change schedule — Per institutional policy. Typical: transparent semi-permeable membrane (TSM) dressing every 7 days; gauze dressing every 48 hours or sooner if soiled/loose. CHG-impregnated dressing 7 days.
  3. Dressing change procedure — Hand hygiene, mask (patient and provider), sterile gloves and field. Remove old dressing carefully (avoid catheter dislodgement). Inspect site. Chlorhexidine prep with friction and ≥ 30-second dry time. Apply new dressing per manufacturer.
  4. Chlorhexidine-impregnated dressing for adults with catheters expected to dwell ≥ 5 days, per CDC. Reduces CLABSI further beyond standard dressing.
  5. Bathing — Chlorhexidine bathing daily for ICU patients with central catheters (CDC recommendation; reduces MRSA and CLABSI). Cover the dressing during bathing for skin-contact precautions; do not soak dressings.
⚠
When Dressing Comes Loose or WetA loose, lifted, or soiled dressing is no longer protective. Change immediately — do not "wait for the scheduled change" — even if changed in the last 24 hours.
07
Daily Necessity Review — The Most Important Element
"Can we remove this catheter today?"

Every CLABSI is a catheter that should have been removed. The single most powerful CLABSI prevention measure is shortening dwell time. The expectation in every unit running central catheters: at every shift handoff, every patient with a central line is asked, "Is the line still needed?"

  • Document the daily assessment in the procedure record or daily nursing note.
  • SBAR to the primary team if a catheter is no longer needed: "This catheter has been in for X days. The plan is [transition/recovery]. What is needed before removal?"
  • Modality-specific transition criteria:
    • HD: AVF maturation reliable; renal recovery off HD; switch to PD planned.
    • PD: Catheter no longer used (transplant, switch to HD); EPS resolved.
    • CRRT: Renal recovery; transition to IHD; comfort care.
    • Apheresis (TPE/RBCx/HPC-A/WBCx): Course complete; bridge to alternate access; goal achieved.
    • ECP: Course taper complete; transition to alternate care.
    • LDL Apheresis: AVF or alternate access in use.
  • End-of-life / comfort care — Catheter removal may be part of comfort transition; coordinate with palliative care.
  • Track median dwell time as a unit-level QI metric.
08
Maintenance Bundle — Cross-Modality Summary
The continuous nursing responsibilities
CDC Maintenance Bundle
  • Hand hygiene per the 5 Moments.
  • Hub disinfection with ≥ 5-second alcoholic chlorhexidine scrub before every access.
  • Sterile gloves and mask for any catheter access.
  • Dressing change per schedule; immediately if loose or soiled.
  • Chlorhexidine-impregnated dressing for catheters > 5 days dwell.
  • Daily chlorhexidine bathing for ICU patients with central catheters.
  • Daily necessity review with advocacy for removal.
  • Catheter site clearly visible for inspection; do not over-bandage.
  • No medication infusions through dialysis/apheresis catheters unless absolutely necessary and per institutional policy.
  • Audit and feedback — Track bundle compliance; share results with staff monthly.
09
Recognition & Reporting
When CLABSI is suspected and what happens next

Clinical Recognition

  • Fever or rigors during/after a procedure in a catheter patient.
  • Hypotension or unexplained hemodynamic change.
  • New leukocytosis.
  • Exit-site erythema, drainage, tenderness.
  • Tunnel infection signs: erythema and tenderness along the tunnel tract.
  • Catheter dysfunction (declining flows, increasing pressures) may signal fibrin sheath with secondary infection.

Initial Workup

  1. Blood cultures × 2: One peripheral, one (or one per lumen) through the catheter, sent simultaneously. Note differential time-to-positivity.
  2. Empiric IV antibiotics per institutional protocol (vancomycin + Gram-negative coverage typical).
  3. Notify physician and infectious disease consultation per institutional CLABSI protocol.
  4. Continue or remove catheter per IDSA / institutional criteria (see treatment-specific SOPs — HD, CRRT).
  5. Document NHSN CLABSI event per CDC surveillance protocol.

NHSN Reporting Criteria (Simplified)

  • Laboratory-confirmed bloodstream infection (LCBI) meeting CDC NHSN definitions.
  • Patient had a central line in place > 2 calendar days at the time of infection (or removed < 24 hours before).
  • No other primary source of infection identified.
  • Reported by infection preventionist per institutional process; tied to publicly reported metrics.

Post-Event Review

  • Root-cause analysis — Insertion bundle compliance, hub access technique, dressing care, dwell time. Identify modifiable factors.
  • Education / re-training for individual or unit if patterns emerge.
  • Cluster surveillance — Multiple CLABSIs in close proximity escalate to infection prevention for unit-level review.
  • Closing the loop with quality / safety committees.
